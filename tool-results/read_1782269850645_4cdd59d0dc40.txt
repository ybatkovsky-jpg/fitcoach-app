    50→with a rest day scheduled every other day. Intermediate athletes should
    51→exercise four to seven times a week. Advanced athletes may exercise up to
    52→15 times a week. More competitive athletes often exercise twice a day,
    53→incorporating one aerobic session and perhaps one weightlifting session, or
    54→one session may be slow and the other fast. The bottom line is that virtually
    55→all of the more competitive athletes exercise at least seven times a week.
    56→Intensity is a measure of how hard you work, and your intensity must be
    57→planned and correlated with your progression. In chapter 1, we showed that
    58→intensity can be measured by percent MHR (maximum heart rate) or
    59→percent O max. Heart rate is the simplest and most effective tool for
    60→2
    61→guiding intensity. You can ensure the adaptation you need for the training
    62→phase you are in by selecting the appropriate intensity. People often
    63→exercise too hard too early in their training programs and then get injured or
    64→fail to develop the appropriate foundation needed for higher intensity later
    65→on. Classifying yourself properly from the beginning will guide you in
    66→selecting the right intensity.
    67→--- PAGE 163 ---
    68→Duration is the length of the exercise session, the recovery interval, or
    69→the repetition. As a general rule, the longer the duration, the more the
    70→intensity will increase. Duration becomes an intensity factor over time
    71→because fatigue during a workout is cumulative, unless the exerciser slows
    72→the rate or pace of the workout. Even then, duration will eventually become
    73→an intensity factor. Duration is important because it is often manipulated to
    74→ensure a certain intensity stimulus. It also determines whether you will need
    75→to refuel your energy during an exercise session. Duration and intensity are
    76→closely intertwined.
    77→Mode refers to the type of activity you do. Running, swimming, and
    78→cycling are all examples of exercise modes. Mode is relevant for the
    79→development of efficiency, especially mechanical and metabolic efficiency.
    80→It also has implications for energy expended because different activities
    81→burn calories at different rates, even if the heart rate is the same. We also
    82→can be at different intensities or perceived efforts in different modes while
    83→exhibiting the same heart rate. Triathletes often notice greater comfort
    84→running at a heart rate of 140 bpm versus biking or swimming at 140 bpm.
    85→The four factors discussed so far—frequency, intensity, duration, and
    86→mode—are the four core principles that are essential in the initial design of
    87→any exercise program. The next four components are more concerned with
    88→the progression of the program.
    89→Overload refers to the increased demand placed on the muscular system.
    90→It simply means requiring the system to do more. Overload, sometimes
    91→referred to as progression, is also concerned with how much you increase
    92→your volume or intensity over time. As a general rule, beginner and
    93→intermediate athletes should not increase volume by more than 10 percent in
    94→any given session during a two- to three-week period, the exception being
    95→when total duration is 10 or fewer miles per week, which may make a 15
    96→percent increase more appropriate. This increase assumes a minimum of
    97→three exercise sessions each week. In other words, you should do at least
    98→eight exercise sessions in a row at the same load before implementing an
    99→increase.
   100→For example, let’s say a beginner runs Monday, Wednesday, and Friday.
   101→He or she runs 3 miles (4.8 km) a day for a total of 9 (14.5 km) miles a
   102→week. After two weeks, this mileage would increase to approximately 10.5
   103→miles (17 km) a week, or three runs at 3.5 miles (5.6 km) each. This
   104→--- PAGE 164 ---
   105→progression model is slow, but it helps prevent injury and muscle damage
   106→while also allowing adequate time for multiple systems to adapt to the new
   107→overload. Using percentage of volume to increase overload is difficult
   108→because of the small amounts of increase. Instead, you can use time, which
   109→is easier to measure and monitor.
   110→Specificity has two components—metabolic specificity and muscle
   111→contraction specificity. Specificity is concerned with putting the correct
   112→type of stress on your metabolic and musculoskeletal systems. For example,
   113→have you ever seen a really good runner struggle to swim 400 meters in the
   114→pool? Essentially, we become efficient at specific muscle contractions
   115→through practice and training. For runners this might require sprint work,
   116→for cyclists it might be hill work, and for triathletes it might involve
   117→working on transitions. The bottom line is that specificity has to do with
   118→coordinating your central nervous system to work in conjunction with your
   119→metabolic demands during muscular contractions. For many athletes this
   120→requires training at various speeds and intensities and in the mode that is
   121→most important. Of particular importance in this category is speed work, but
   122→again, only when the endurance base has been built. LeBron James doesn’t
   123→practice his free throws by dunking the ball and Yo-Yo Ma doesn’t prepare
   124→for his cello concerts by playing the violin; their training is specific.
   125→Reversibility relates to losing whatever fitness you have gained. The
   126→amount of time it takes to lose fitness varies among athletes and types of
   127→fitness. For example, aerobic fitness begins to decline without exercise in
   128→about 10 days. Muscular strength will not decline for about 30 days.
   129→Athletes often have a hard time grasping this concept. They believe that if
   130→they can’t work out, the system starts to reverse and fitness is lost within
   131→one or two days. Consequently, many athletes do not rest appropriately for
   132→important races, especially long races such as marathons or Ironman
   133→triathlons. In reality, well-trained aerobic athletes do not lose fitness for at
   134→least seven days. In fact, with appropriate rest, they will improve
   135→performance, which is what happens during tapering. Remember, the hard
   136→part is getting fit; staying fit is a bit easier.
   137→Maintenance and reversibility are somewhat linked. Maintenance refers
   138→to hanging onto current fitness. Maintenance of fitness is achieved with
   139→much less work and effort than improving fitness. If your goal is simply to
   140→maintain fitness, you can achieve this with a 30 to 40 percent decrease in
   141→--- PAGE 165 ---
   142→volume at your current intensity. However, at certain points in your training
   143→you can improve performance by decreasing volume and increasing
   144→intensity. During the tapering phases of training, you can decrease your
   145→volume up to 80 percent provided that you keep the intensity very high
   146→(usually greater than 90 percent of MHR). This is why many athletes
   147→dramatically cut volume but increase speed and intensity in the weeks
   148→leading up to big races, yet still race faster. This is typically known as
   149→“peaking” when done near the end of a season in preparation for
   150→championship performances.
   151→Now that you understand these programming principles, let’s consider
   152→how you can manipulate them during training using heart rate training
   153→zones. Remember, you are trying to build a logical and systematic program
   154→to develop the elements of power, speed, and endurance that you need—and
   155→do it without getting hurt.
   156→Periodization
   157→Before we get into the real nuts and bolts of building the program, we want
   158→to take a look at the big picture of a training program. We refer to this as
   159→periodization, and it describes the organization of an overall training
   160→program. The program might be 12 weeks, six months, or one year long.
   161→For Olympic athletes, it might be four years. Regardless of the length, the
   162→program is organized around the scientific principles of progression. If you
   163→plan your periodization correctly, you will find that you can align heart rate
   164→intensities with periods in the cycle for the most part. The longer, slower,
   165→base-building cycles will be characterized by much lower heart rates overall
   166→(both at rest and during exercise) and display the phase I endurance zone.
   167→As you move into higher-intensity cycles, your heart rate will elevate
   168→significantly during exercise as you prime for race condition.
   169→A well-organized program focuses on different components of overall
   170→fitness during different periods—macrocycles, microcycles, and
   171→mesocycles. The four phases of training—endurance, stamina, economy,
   172→and speed—fit within these periods. In general, macrocycles are longer, or
   173→more generally defined, periods of training that lack the specificity and
   174→detail of microcycles and mesocycles. For example, a macrocycle in a six-
   175→--- PAGE 166 ---
   176→month training program might comprise the first 12 weeks of simple, low-
   177→intensity aerobic conditioning designed to build an endurance base.
   178→Microcycles are shorter periods with an increased focus on another
   179→aspect of fitness. For example, in a six-month training program in which the
   180→first 12 weeks are a macrocycle, two microcycles of four weeks each might
   181→follow. These microcycles might include a focus on speed work, hill work,
   182→tempo runs, or flexibility in order to improve stamina. During microcycles
   183→we tend to focus a little more on the specific components, such as the
   184→economy required for performance and racing.
   185→Mesocycles are the final part of the periodization process. These are
   186→individual sessions designed to address a particular skill component. A
   187→mesocycle for a runner might focus on pacing, speed and power
   188→development, or mechanics. For a cyclist, a mesocycle might focus on body
   189→position or climbing posture. For a swimmer, it might focus on stroke
   190→technique.
   191→The use of periodization forces you to consider in detail the type of
   192→adaptations you are looking for during a particular phase of your program.
   193→It forces you to be organized, consider how long you have to adapt, and
   194→decide where the competition occurs in your overall training program.
   195→When designing an exercise program, you should be looking at the desired
   196→end point, or product. Ask yourself, “What am I looking for in terms of
   197→performance at the end of the training program?” When you can define that,
   198→you can work backward to ensure that the correct amount of time is
   199→available to ensure the adaptations needed. This prevents you from making
   200→the basic mistake of progressing too rapidly and increasing your risk of
   201→injury.
   202→If you look back at the general structure of a training program through
   203→the lens of periodization, you will see that more general conditioning
   204→sessions occur early on (macrocycles), and that these become progressively
   205→more specific (microcycles and mesocycles). Within each of these periods
   206→you will also see variations in intensity, distance, recovery, and so on. Here
   207→again, heart rate is your guiding tool to ensure that you stay within the
   208→parameters of intensity you need to get the appropriate adaptation. Clearly,
   209→being organized and plotting out your program is paramount.

   211→Principle of Progressive Resistance
   212→The principle of progressive resistance can be applied to either slow or
   213→fleet-footed movements. A primary law of exercise physiology is that we
   214→can condition our muscles by working them with repetitive movements to a
   215→state of fatigue and then overloading them with a few more repetitions.
   216→The most familiar application of this principle is weight training. We all
   217→know that lifting weights can make us stronger. Perhaps you are familiar
   218→with the two ways to weight train: lift a light weight many times or lift a
   219→heavy weight a few times. Either one works, but only if the weight is lifted
   220→enough times or is heavy enough to stimulate more muscle fibers to be
   221→recruited into action, almost reach failure, and then be made to overload to
   222→the quiver, struggle, and burn level.
   223→Although there are important secondary fitness benefits to both types of
   224→weight training, let’s consider a standard set of 10 repetitions (or reps) as an
   225→example. A set of 10 reps is both safe and efficient for improving strength.
   226→Start by selecting, through trial and error, the amount of weight that will
   227→fatigue your muscle group within seven reps. The overload that occurs with
   228→the eighth, ninth, and tenth reps stimulates adaptation. Once the muscle is
   229→strong enough to move that weight 10 times without reaching overload,
   230→increase the resistance to reach the next level of fitness and strength. How
   231→long this process continues depends on your goal, but more resistance in the
   232→form of heavier weights is necessary for getting stronger. For example, if
   233→Larry Lifter found that he could reach overload with 95 pounds (43 kg)
   234→when doing bench presses, once he could do his set of 10 reps, he would
   235→add 10 pounds (4.5 kg) to reach the new overload. This process would
   236→continue to 115 pounds (52 kg) once he could not overload with 105 pounds
   237→(48 kg).
   238→Now, let’s apply the principles of progressive resistance training to
   239→walking, jogging, and running. In fact, you can extend this example
   240→creatively to any of the endurance sports. The resistance for these activities
   241→is the body’s weight against gravity. However, you won’t be moving heavier
   242→weights to increase the resistance. In fact, as you get in better shape, you
   243→may actually move less weight. Therefore, you have three choices for
   244→increasing resistance:
   245→--- PAGE 168 ---
   246→1. Lengthen the duration of the exercise sessions—walk, jog, or run
   247→farther at the same pace.
   248→2. Add intensity by lifting your weight higher against gravity—walk,
   249→jog, or run faster.
   250→3. Add intensity by changing the degree of incline—head for the hills.
   251→In other words, getting stronger and fitter by walking is like lifting a very
   252→light weight many times. Jogging is like lifting the next increment of still
   253→fairly light weight not quite so many times. Running is like lifting heavy
   254→weights fewer times. Running up hills is akin to lifting the maximum
   255→weight just a few times.
   256→Workouts at slower paces in lower-intensity heart rate zones will take
   257→more time, whereas faster workouts in higher-intensity zones will be over
   258→more quickly. Your heart rate, patience, and goals will determine how much
   259→or how little time you spend.
   260→Monitoring Progress and Recovery
   261→By now the concept of monitoring heart rate is familiar to you. You also
   262→know that increased duration, if the pace stays the same, represents
   263→increased intensity, and in general, increases in duration require more
   264→recovery. However, because we know that heart rate is extremely versatile
   265→and responsive, we can use it to monitor both acute and chronic recovery as
   266→well as adaptations. In the next section we take a look at both acute and
   267→chronic heart rate responses and consider how they can be used to regulate
   268→intensity and recovery within an exercise session and also guide recovery
   269→between workouts to prevent overtraining.
   270→Using Acute Heart Rate to Guide Recovery Within an
   271→Exercise Session
   272→Heart rate response during exercise is typically a reflection of the intensity
   273→of the exercise. During an exercise session, you may experience several
   274→increases in intensity that cause a higher heart rate, such as after you climb
   275→a hill. It also happens during speed interval sessions on the track. You may
   276→have to slow down or stop after this brief period of increased intensity to
   277→recover to some degree. An important question at this point is: When are
   278→--- PAGE 169 ---
   279→you ready to perform the next interval? Your within-exercise recovery heart
   280→rate can help guide you safely.
   281→You can determine when you are ready to start again by monitoring your
   282→recovery heart rate. As a general rule, 65 percent MHR is used as the
   283→criterion for adequate recovery. However, this is not a hard-and-fast rule. If
   284→you are doing high-intensity speed or power workouts that last only a few
   285→seconds, your coach might select a 50 percent recovery rate for 800-meter
   286→intervals. The bottom line with recovery heart rates is that you must achieve
   287→enough recovery to allow you to perform your complete workout with
   288→sufficient quality rather than at significantly reduced speeds or efforts.
   289→Generally, fitter athletes use higher percentages of MHR while recovering.
   290→Your appropriate heart rate recovery depends on how much and how
   291→well you want to recover between bouts. In general, the faster or the higher
   292→the intensity of the exercise piece, the greater your recovery should be. So,
   293→for example, if you are running repetitions at 85 percent MHR, you would
   294→need less recovery than if you were running them at 95 to 100 percent
   295→MHR.
   296→Here’s an example. A coach might select a 50 percent MHR target as a
   297→recovery number. When the athlete’s heart rate drops back down to that
   298→level, she can resume the workload or perform the next piece. Here is an
   299→easy math example: A 20-year-old runner has a maximum heart rate of 200
   300→bpm. The session comprises ten 200-meter repeats on the track. Following
   301→the repeats, the coach can use time or heart rate to determine when the
   302→athlete should start again. Some coaches use a combination of both. In the
   303→current example, the coach does not have the athlete perform the next 200
   304→meters until her heart rate drops below 100 bpm (50 percent of the
   305→maximum heart rate). This is an example of using acute heart rate response
   306→during recovery when monitoring heart rate solely within a given exercise
   307→session.
   308→Using Chronic Heart Rate to Guide Recovery After an
   309→Exercise Session
   310→Changes in heart rate measurement technology now allow us to collect data
   311→for much longer periods extending beyond the exercise session (over 24
   312→hours at a time). Because we can monitor heart rate over longer periods, we
   313→--- PAGE 170 ---
   314→can collect excellent resting and recovery data between days of exercise.
   315→(You could refer to this as daily heart rate recovery.) This can guide daily
   316→training intensities and allow you to make changes if you’re not fully
   317→recovered from the previous exercise bout. This can reduce the likelihood of
   318→acute overtraining and even injury.
   319→Look back at figure 1.2 (page 12) to see this depicted. Here is an
   320→example to illustrate this point. Let’s say you get into the habit of recording
   321→resting heart rate, and your average heart rate when you wake up in the
   322→morning is 52 bpm. On Sunday you do a 10-mile (16 km) run. You wake up
   323→on Monday with a resting heart rate of 55 bpm. You choose to go ahead
   324→with a scheduled track and speed session (low volume but high intensity) on
   325→Monday. When you wake up Tuesday morning, your resting heart rate is 56
   326→bpm. This is an indication that you have not recovered from the Sunday
   327→workout (and Monday added to the dilemma) and that you should
   328→implement either a very easy workout on Tuesday or preferably take a
   329→complete rest day. This illustrates why good record keeping is useful. Using
   330→this approach, you adjust the Monday (and Tuesday) workout and then
   331→hopefully see that within one or two more days your resting heart rate
   332→recovers to normal. If it does not, you may have a more serious problem of
   333→overtraining or staleness on your hands. This will necessitate longer periods
   334→of rest and strategic nutrition.
   335→If you pay close attention to your heart rate not only during exercise but
   336→also throughout the day, you will get a lot of feedback about your overall
   337→state of recovery and readiness for the next training session. Again, this will
   338→also help reduce the likelihood of injury.
   339→Avoiding Overtraining and Staleness
   340→You can avoid overtraining and staleness by both resting and training your
   341→heart rates. Although overtraining is a complex issue, you can use heart rate
   342→data to detect when you are training too much in the early stages of getting
   343→in shape. Heart rate data can also warn when you are at risk of staleness
   344→from stretching a running season past your peak.
   345→Another important concept is overreaching, which applies to short-term
   346→overtraining, as might happen after a particularly hard training session. This
   347→usually occurs in the few days following a longer, more strenuous workout.
   348→--- PAGE 171 ---
   349→Overtraining applies to longer, more chronic periods of training too hard or
   350→racing too often. This condition results after weeks or months of training
   351→without adequate rest and nutrition. This is a more serious condition,
   352→requiring lengthy periods of rest and attention to nutrition and perhaps
   353→medical intervention. In contrast, overreaching can normally be addressed
   354→with a few days of rest and a few hearty meals.
   355→Using your heart rate monitor can keep you from developing either
   356→overtraining condition. To use it effectively, you need to do two things:
   357→1. Record and monitor your resting heart rate and exercise heart rate
   358→response to a given workload as often as possible. These should be
   359→documented over days, weeks, and months.
   360→2. Understand how your heart rate should respond both at rest and
   361→during exercise as you train. Make sure it is increasing and decreasing
   362→as you would expect.
   363→Addressing the first issue is fairly straightforward. The second requires
   364→that you read on a little further. Under normal conditions, resting heart rate
   365→should be back to normal within 24 hours of a workout. You should make
   366→sure this is the case before proceeding with a higher-intensity workout the
   367→following day. Over time, however, your heart rate should decrease both at
   368→rest and in response to a fixed workload. The decreased heart rate response
   369→under both situations is a result of improved cardiac muscle function—the
   370→heart is stronger and can move more blood with each beat. We call this
   371→increased stroke volume. If you are not fully recovered, then your heart rate
   372→at rest will be elevated because it is still working at repairing tissues and
   373→replenishing fuels. What’s more, if you are not recovered, your heart rate
   374→during exercise will not increase to where it needs to be to supply the
   375→needed blood flow. This results in the sluggishness or staleness athletes
   376→often experience during early overreaching. It feels as though someone has
   377→put the lid on the heart rate. As a result, you try to work harder but cannot
   378→because the heart rate cannot elevate to give you the blood supply you need.
   379→The result is a frustrating and psychologically damaging workout. Your legs
   380→and arms are heavy, and the best thing you can do is go home, eat, and
   381→sleep.
   382→It is nice to have your 12-week program carved in stone. However, you
   383→need to be open-minded enough to change your daily plan if your body
   384→--- PAGE 172 ---
   385→needs it. The question is, What should you do if your heart rate indicates
   386→overtraining? The obvious answer is to cut back the volume of exercise you
   387→are doing, especially for the next few days. For some athletes this is not
   388→always possible, but there are a few other precautions you can take, like
   389→decreasing intensity or frequency of hard workouts.
   390→The first step is to determine whether you are in an acute stage of
   391→overtraining or overreaching, or whether these are early symptoms of a
   392→more long-term, chronic problem. To determine this, we ask athletes three
   393→questions. (1) Are you finding your exercise sessions getting more difficult
   394→or easier? If they are getting more difficult and they are the same workouts
   395→(i.e., same speed), you’re overtraining. (2) Are you very tired but having
   396→trouble getting good-quality sleep? If so, you are overtraining. (3) Do you
   397→find it difficult to exert maximum effort during a routine training session? If
   398→so, you’re overtraining. These three simple questions will help you
   399→determine whether you are starting to overtrain.
   400→Early stages of overtraining are really overreaching. Overreaching
   401→generally occurs when there is a marked increase in intensity or volume,
   402→which quite often happens to athletes attending a training camp where there
   403→is a significant increase in workload within a short period of time. The good
   404→news about overreaching is that it can be addressed relatively quickly and
   405→easily, with three or four days of planned rest and appropriate nutritional
   406→intervention.
   407→Ignoring the symptoms and continuing to train with the mentality that
   408→you just need to grind through the sessions will inevitably lead to classic
   409→overtraining. Classic overtraining is a more long-term problem with more
   410→serious consequences. It usually exhibits all the signs and symptoms of
   411→overreaching plus a couple more: heart rate continues to be elevated at rest,
   412→and exercise heart rates continue to be depressed despite efforts to work
   413→harder. What follows tends to be a period of illness or sickness as a result of
   414→a compromised immune system. At this point you are clearly overtraining
   415→and facing several weeks or possibly several months of layoff from
   416→exercise.
   417→Fortunately, by monitoring your heart rate regularly both at rest and
   418→during exercise, you can detect these early symptoms. Then, you can take
   419→appropriate action to vary the intensity and duration of workouts to ensure
   420→--- PAGE 173 ---
   421→that you do not move from the overreaching condition into the overtraining
   422→condition.

   469→Walking
   470→T
   471→he previous chapters delivered a great deal of theory as well as
   472→practical advice on monitored training and its application to
   473→exercising in general. The submaximal nature of walking, the activity
   474→covered in this chapter, requires some exceptions to the concepts discussed
   475→previously. Creative alternatives are needed. For example, although you
   476→will use percentage of maximum heart rate (MHR) to determine your
   477→training zones, you will use a special, perhaps slightly less than fully
   478→maximum heart rate. The challenge is to find a lower zone of intensity that
   479→will still accomplish the goals of improving endurance and stamina.
   480→Your training zones will have wide ranges. You may need to rely on your
   481→own perception of your effort to verify that the heart rates you see are,
   482→indeed, reliable and effective. You’ll still use your heart rate to determine
   483→how far, how “fast,” and how hard you should work out. This approach
   484→should be both easy to follow and yet challenging enough to be effective at
   485→elevating your fitness level.
   486→In this chapter, you will learn to apply the overload principle of
   487→progressive resistance to your walking. Just as someone must lift a light
   488→weight many times to get stronger, walkers must take a lot of steps to reach
   489→the point of muscle overload because of the rather weak challenge to
   490→gravity. By comparison, sprinting is like lifting a heavy weight a few times
   491→to reach muscle overload. In either case, you can track the oxygen demands
   492→of your working muscles by keeping track of your cardiac system’s
   493→response in heartbeats per minute.
   494→--- PAGE 177 ---
   495→This chapter will also require that you be the subject in a scientific
   496→“study of one.” In designing your workouts, you will manipulate time,
   497→distance, pace, and, especially, your effort as measured by your heart rate

   567→Heart Rate
   568→We have to admit that walkers present a bit of a conundrum when it comes
   569→to testing for MHR. Estimating MHR through the methods described in
   570→chapter 2 isn’t very reliable. For other sports and exercise activities, it is
   571→standard practice to base target heart rates on percentages of MHR
