   144‚Üí   168‚Üí   168‚ÜíPhysiological adaptation refers to how the body responds to, accommodates, or
   145‚Üí   169‚Üí   169‚Üíadjusts to exercise. To give an over-simplified example, if you lift heavy weights,
   146‚Üí   170‚Üí   170‚Üíyour muscles get bigger. But there are actually a number of adaptations taking
   147‚Üí   171‚Üí   171‚Üíplace in response to such a stimulus. These adaptations include a more coordi-
   148‚Üí   172‚Üí   172‚Üínated neural signal to the activated muscles, uptake of nutrients, and the release
   149‚Üí   173‚Üí   173‚Üíof muscle-building hormones.
   150‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚ÜíLearning about the process of physiological adaptation will help you design
   151‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üímore effective workouts, understand how your body will respond to training,
   152‚Üí   176‚Üí   176‚Üíand know what to expect with consistent and focused training. Two of the most
   153‚Üí   177‚Üí   177‚Üícommon fitness goals are burning fat and enhancing muscular development. This
   154‚Üí   178‚Üí   178‚Üíchapter takes a deeper dive into some of the physiological adaptations to training
   155‚Üí   179‚Üí   179‚Üíthat can turn these goals into realities.
   156‚Üí   180‚Üí   180‚ÜíAdapting to Demands
   157‚Üí   181‚Üí   181‚ÜíAs discussed in chapter 1, in reference to the specificity principle, adaptations are
   158‚Üí   182‚Üí   182‚Üíspecific to imposed demands. Adaptions can be morphological (related to muscle
   159‚Üí   183‚Üí   183‚Üísize), biomechanical (related to strength and power), anatomical (related to muscle,
   160‚Üí   184‚Üí   184‚Üíbone, or fat volumes), neurological (related to motor control), or metabolic (related
   161‚Üí   185‚Üí   185‚Üíto aerobic or anaerobic energy pathways) in nature. Workout parameters such
   162‚Üí   186‚Üí   186‚Üías intensity level and rest duration can be set to elicit specific adaptations. For
   163‚Üí   187‚Üí   187‚Üíinstance, short, high-intensity exercise intervals (up to six seconds in duration,
   164‚Üí   188‚Üí   188‚Üífocusing on quick movement with maximal intensity) paired with longer rest
   165‚Üí   189‚Üí   189‚Üíintervals (1:1 to 1:4 work:rest ratio) can target the short-duration phosphagen meta-
   166‚Üí   190‚Üí   190‚Üíbolic pathway. Conversely, lower-intensity activity paired with shorter-duration
   167‚Üí   191‚Üí   191‚Üírest intervals can target the aerobic metabolic pathway. Also, keep in mind that
   168‚Üí   192‚Üí   192‚Üíexercises targeting multiple joints and muscle groups at one time are more meta-
   169‚Üí   193‚Üí   193‚Üíbolically challenging than exercises targeting individual joints and muscle groups.
   170‚Üí   194‚Üí   194‚ÜíIt should be pointed out that metabolic training utilizes both aerobic and anaero-
   171‚Üí   195‚Üí   195‚Üíbic metabolic pathways, while also placing demand on the neuromuscular system
   172‚Üí   196‚Üí   196‚Üí11
   173‚Üí   197‚Üí   197‚Üí--- PAGE 21 ---
   174‚Üí   198‚Üí   198‚Üí12 | Metabolic Training
   175‚Üí   199‚Üí   199‚Üíby way of strength and power exercises. In other situations, metabolic training
   176‚Üí   200‚Üí   200‚Üícan elicit more of an aerobic metabolic (endurance) response‚Äîfor example, when
   177‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚Üílighter weights are used. This de-emphasizes the neuromuscular system. These
   178‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üítwo examples represent the various ways in which metabolic training can be pro-
   179‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚Üígrammed. Neither approach is superior to the other; the optimal program is best
   180‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üídetermined by the goals of the program and the individual. With this concept in
   181‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üímind, both aerobic and anaerobic adaptations can be observed.
   182‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚ÜíSome metabolic-training programs implement strength-power and aerobic
   183‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚Üí(endurance) methodologies together. This approach is called concurrent training.
   184‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚ÜíResearch on the effects of concurrent training has yielded the following conclu-
   185‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üísions:
   186‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚Üí‚Ä¢ Concurrent training does not appear to compromise gains in aerobic capacity.
   187‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚Üí‚Ä¢ The incorporation of resistance training may have a beneficial effect on
   188‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üíshort-term endurance.
   189‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚Üí‚Ä¢ The incorporation of aerobic training may compromise maximal strength in
   190‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚Üíexperienced, resistance-trained individuals.
   191‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚ÜíConsider the intensity of training: Short bursts of high-intensity training target
   192‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚Üíthe anaerobic pathway. Longer-duration efforts, lasting more than three minutes,
   193‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚Üístart to emphasize the aerobic pathway. Metabolic activity is elevated by anaerobic
   194‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üíefforts of shorter duration if these efforts are performed at high intensity, which
   195‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üíincludes heavy resistance training exercise (Herda & Cramer 2016). This idea
   196‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üíconstitutes a fundamental element and benefit of metabolic training.
   197‚Üí   221‚Üí   221‚ÜíThe Role of Excess Postexercise

   347‚Üí   371‚Üí   371‚Üíbalance and coordination (Harrison 2011).
