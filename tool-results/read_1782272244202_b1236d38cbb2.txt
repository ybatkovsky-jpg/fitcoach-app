    55→   244→WHAT IS VELOCITY-BASED TRAINING?
    56→   245→VBT is a method for evaluating the intensity of a given movement by calculating
    57→   246→displacement and time through the monitoring of bar or body speeds. For many
    58→   247→years, the standard method coaches used was determining the weight of that load
    59→   248→based on a percentage of a one-repetition maximum (1RM). VBT, on the other
    60→   249→hand, is based on the speed of a movement or load lifted.
    61→   250→Today’s advancements in technology allow us to more precisely focus on the
    62→   251→speed at which a bar or an athlete is moving as well as the percentage of loss in
    63→   252→velocity from rep to rep or from set to set. The market has propelled the concept
    64→   253→and application of VBT in recent years, which has caused the need for more
    65→   254→information regarding this emerging technology. That is the purpose for me
    66→   255→having written this book. While you don’t need to use VBT technology to be a
    67→   256→good coach, having a better set of tools will always help a great craftsperson.
    68→   257→Don’t just use VBT concepts or tools for the sake of relevance; use them to solve
    69→   258→problems that are unique to your environment.
    70→   259→Research in Spain revealed a few key findings about some of the benefits of
    71→   260→VBT (González-Badillo and Sánchez-Medina 2010):
    72→   261→People who train with maximal velocity during the concentric phase of a lift
    73→   262→
    74→   263→or movement attain better strength and power results than those who do not
    75→   264→train with maximal intended velocity.
    76→   265→Velocity decreases fairly linearly across a set of traditional strength training
    77→   266→exercises such as bench presses and squats.
    78→   267→Velocity is closely related to the percentage of the 1RM.

    91→   280→American football, baseball, or track and field, this time is even faster. While
    92→   281→absolute strength, or more specifically, peak force, is still and always will be the
    93→   282→foundation for all other types or speeds of strength, the key is to figure out which

    99→   288→higher velocities alone. VBT is an objective method of evaluating intensity of a

   101→   290→tracked by a piece of technology or device known as a linear position transducer,

   103→   292→PUSH Band can be worn around the arm (see figure 1.2), ankle, or waist (the

   108→   297→

   148→   337→WHAT ARE VBT’S GOALS?
   149→   338→This section defines 1RM as well as discusses its relationship to various types of
   150→   339→strength. It also explores some of the shortcomings of this standard method, such
   151→   340→as residual fatigue from stress and sport, and how VBT may be a much better

   154→   343→1RM: The Load–Velocity Relationship
   155→   344→A 1RM is the maximum amount of weight that a person can lift for one
   156→   345→repetition. This can also be used as an upper limit for determining the desired
   157→   346→load of an exercise, as in a percentage of 1RM. This long-standing percentagebased approach has been used to gauge training intensity by helping determine
   158→   347→appropriate load percentages to use at the start of a new training program. These
   159→   348→percentages range from less than 60 percent maximum to train muscular
   160→   349→endurance and hypertrophy to between 90 and 100 percent maximum when
   161→   350→
   162→   351→improvements in absolute strength are the focus. These percentages also help
   163→   352→track improvements at the end of a training block in order to gain insight about a
   164→   353→program’s validity based on the athlete’s pre- and post-1RM testing.
   165→   354→
   166→   355→A 1RM is the maximum amount of weight that a person can lift
   167→   356→for one repetition.
   168→   357→While traditional 1RMs work, and I have used them for many years, this
   169→   358→method becomes somewhat problematic when we consider that strength levels
   170→   359→vary before and after a competition or from other stressors, such as lack of sleep
   171→   360→or dehydration. These day-to-day fluctuations in strength have been shown to be
   172→   361→as large as 18 percent above and below a previously tested 1RM (Flanagan and
   173→   362→Jovanovic 2014). For example, table 1.1 demonstrates how an athlete, with a
   174→   363→“tested” 225 1RM for the bench press, performed 8 repetitions at 180 pounds (80
   175→   364→percent of his estimated 1RM, or [0.80 m/sec]) on Monday. However, on
   176→   365→Wednesday, this athlete performed the same 8 repetitions at 0.50 meters per
   177→   366→second with a load of 185 pounds, making this approximately 83 percent of his
   178→   367→1RM and showing a 3 percent fluctuation (improvement) in his baseline 1RM.
   179→   368→Once again, on Friday, after a great night’s sleep, he hits a personal best of 190
   180→   369→pounds at the same 0.50 meters per second velocity. This is now 85 percent of
   181→   370→his tested 1RM—showing a fluctuation (improvement) of 5 percent in his
   182→   371→baseline 1RM. But, overdoing it by lifting again on Saturday without adequate
   183→   372→rest shows a decline of 8 percent. This downward fluctuation can produce
   184→   373→suboptimal results while increasing the risk of injury.
   185→   374→
   186→   375→With VBT, however, we can measure movement velocity as a marker of
   187→   376→
   188→   377→intensity instead of the traditional 1RM. We can also use VBT to help prevent
   189→   378→failure, which as we all know, can be extremely taxing on the central nervous
   190→   379→system and equally difficult to recover from. These factors can slow down an
   191→   380→athlete’s progress considerably. But, let’s not get ahead of ourselves—predicting
   192→   381→a 1RM with VBT is covered in upcoming chapters.
   193→   382→
   194→   383→Using External Cueing
   195→   384→Providing feedback allows people to take that information and make better, more
   196→   385→educated decisions. This is especially true when talking about motor learning or
   197→   386→the refinement of an athletic skill. External cueing focuses athletes on the effect
   198→   387→of their movement through the use of outside information as opposed to internal
   199→   388→cueing, where athletes focus only on their body movements or their own inner
   200→   389→chatter. Many methods can be considered external cues—a coach’s feedback or
   201→   390→visual cues from an apparatus, such as a stopwatch or VBT, are two great
   202→   391→examples of external cueing.
   203→   392→Research has consistently demonstrated that enhanced motor performance
   204→   393→and learning extend across different types of tasks, skill levels, and age groups
   205→   394→when using an external focus relative to an internal focus (Wulf 2012). Further
   206→   395→studies show that when athletes are given external cueing relative to an internal
   207→   396→focus or no focus instructions at all, it not only increases jump height and speed
   208→   397→but also enhances neuromuscular coordination (Wulf 2012). Most good coaches
   209→   398→and experienced athletes probably already use external cueing when it comes to
   210→   399→coaching technique such as, “Extend your hip” or “Drive your feet through the
   211→   400→ground” (see figure 1.4). With VBT, we can also get feedback regarding speed or
   212→   401→velocity of the movement, whether it is from a bar or the body. Athletes who
   213→   402→receive this type of external feedback have shown improved performance results
   214→   403→because they were focused on the intent of the activity. VBT also helps to hold
   215→   404→athletes accountable for their performance.

   293→   482→Building Accountability
   294→   483→I must preface this section with this statement: It is my experience and belief that
   295→   484→athletes must have a solid base of absolute strength in order to be good
   296→   485→candidates to use VBT in their training programs. In working with both youth
   297→   486→and professional athletes, I have found that these younger, less mature athletes
   298→   487→who do not possess either the adequate strength, nor mobility possibly because
   299→   488→of existing growth plate issues, need to focus first and foremost on correct form
   300→   489→and gains in hypertrophy. However, as an athlete matures and their training age
   301→   490→increases, the athlete generally needs to be pushed harder to actually produce
   302→   491→maximum effort concentric movements and continue to create positive
   303→   492→adaptations. For such an athlete, VBT can be a game changer. Unfortunately, as
   304→   493→with anything, there are no absolutes. Unmotivated or undedicated athletes can
   305→   494→cheat the system with VBT. By purposely moving their bar or body slowly
   306→   495→during initial testing, athletes can attain lower baseline speeds in order to avoid
   307→   496→having to work as hard to match or increase those numbers and metrics later.
   308→   497→That is why I require an athlete to earn “the right” prior to allowing them to start
   309→   498→using VBT. To summarize, getting immediate feedback on speed makes the
   310→   499→intention of the movement clear to hold our athletes accountable.

   312→   501→Monitoring Fatigue
   313→   502→Everything that happens in our lives causes either an action or a reaction.
   314→   503→
   315→   504→Whether it is training, practice, relationship or family issues, or a lack of sleep,
   316→   505→all of these situations can profoundly affect an athlete’s central nervous system,
   317→   506→or recovery. As previously mentioned, research suggests that 1RM strength can
   318→   507→vary by 18 percent in either direction on any given day (Flanagan and Jovanovic
   319→   508→2014), meaning that prescribed percentages can be wildly inappropriate in either
   320→   509→direction depending on the amount of stress athletes have applied to their central
   321→   510→nervous system.
   322→   511→Autoregulation refers to a system that manages volume to regulate individual
   323→   512→differences in an athlete’s work capacity based on stress-related fatigue. This can
   324→   513→be a powerful tool for a coach helping an athlete to avoid over- or undertraining
   325→   514→in terms of the athlete’s long-term athletic development (LTAD). Athletes will
   326→   515→increase strength by progressing at their own pace based on daily and weekly
   327→   516→variations in performance parameters, unlike traditional linear periodization
   328→   517→(LP), during which there is a set increase in intensity from week to week. For
   329→   518→example, one study showed that the progressive resistance through
   330→   519→autoregulation was more effective than the LP model means of programming in
   331→   520→increasing the bench press and squat over a period of six weeks (Mann et al.
   332→   521→2010).
   333→   522→By using VBT, we can take these parameters into account by locking into a
   334→   523→percentage of a bar or body speed rather than a percentage of 1RM. By receiving
   335→   524→a number after each rep on a daily basis, we can see if the weight needs to be
   336→   525→decreased due to fatigue that particular day or increased because of new strength
   337→   526→gains. For example, assume an athlete has recorded a baseline measurement of
   338→   527→250 pounds (113 kg) for 1RM on a barbell bench press. If this athlete is in a
   339→   528→maximal strength phase and the program calls for 5 × 5 at 85 percent 1RM, it
   340→   529→would look something like this: 5 × 5 at 212 pounds (96 kg), or 85 percent of
   341→   530→250 pounds (113 kg). We also know that by monitoring this athlete’s bar speed

   411→   600→ECCENTRIC AND CONCENTRIC
   412→   601→CONTRACTIONS AND DECELERATION
   413→   602→To understand the different metrics associated with VBT, we must first review
   414→   603→eccentric and concentric muscle contractions as well as deceleration in order to
   415→   604→know why different methods of tracking velocity should be used. The ability to
   416→   605→get in and out of concentric and eccentric muscle contractions quickly and more
   417→   606→efficiently allows athletes to better use the stretch shortening cycle (SSC) to
   418→   607→produce force at fast rates (discussed in greater depth in chapter 6) and
   419→   608→ultimately produce more power. This ability is the gold standard in performance
   420→   609→and is a big part of what separates elite athletes from the rest of the athletic field.
   421→   610→During this discussion, keep in mind that VBT only measures the concentric
   422→   611→portion of the lift or movement. However, it’s vitally important to understand the
   423→   612→eccentric portion because it substantially boosts the acceleration of the
   424→   613→concentric portion when we monitor bar or body speeds. This makes the
   425→   614→eccentric portion of the movement a key performance indicator (KPI) as well
   426→   615→when playing sports.
   427→   616→
   428→   617→Eccentric Contraction
   429→   618→An eccentric muscle contraction is the motion of an active muscle while it is
   430→   619→lengthening under a load. When a muscle contracts eccentrically, it is absorbing
   431→   620→energy (as opposed to a concentric contraction which uses energy). The
   432→   621→lengthening of the muscle also puts the tendons on slack as shown in figure 2.1.
   433→   622→
   434→   623→An eccentric muscle contraction is the motion of an active
   435→   624→muscle while it is lengthening under a load.
   436→   625→During this process, a few things happen—the muscle absorbs energy
   437→   626→developed by the external load in order to support the weight of the body against
   438→   627→gravity, helping absorb shock and reducing the risk of injury. On the
   439→   628→performance side, the storage of this elastic energy helps the muscle to recoil in
   440→   629→preparation for the following concentric contraction (i.e., acceleration). For
   441→   630→example, a countermovement jump improves the eccentric strength of the
   442→   631→muscle and helps the athlete store energy more easily in the low position (see
   443→   632→figure 2.2a), increasing neural drive and switching quickly to the concentric, or
   444→   633→elastic recoil, position, of the jump (see figure 2.2b).
   445→   634→
   446→   635→Figure 2.1 Eccentric muscle contraction.
   447→   636→
   448→   637→In addition, eccentric contractions require far less motor unit activity
   449→   638→(approximately four times less) and consume much less oxygen for a given
   450→   639→muscle movement than concentric contractions. These reductions allow an
   451→   640→eccentric contraction to handle more force (load), with a lower energy
   452→   641→requirement, which may explain why eccentric overload training is essential in
   453→   642→the early stages of a strength training program: It enhances tissue prep and
   454→   643→makes athletes stronger in low positions. Although VBT is not typically used in
   455→   644→
   456→   645→this portion of the lift, eccentric overload training maximizes training in later
   457→   646→stages when heavier loads are used. It also helps athletes better absorb and store
   458→   647→force eccentrically to achieve a higher end result—to produce a more explosive
   459→   648→movement in their sport.

   462→   651→contraction, or the explosive portion of the jump.
   463→   652→
   464→   653→Concentric Contraction
   465→   654→Intention to move a barbell or the body as fast as possible is one of the most
   466→   655→important factors for increasing strength and power, and it relies on neural
   467→   656→adaptations to achieve this. The increase in neural drive allows the athlete to feel
   468→   657→
   469→   658→what true explosiveness is all about. It not only requires a great application of
   470→   659→force during the concentric portion but also involves selecting the correct
   471→   660→exercise to maximize the type of concentric contraction needed (explained in
   472→   661→greater detail in chapter 4’s section on exercise selection). First, let’s briefly
   473→   662→focus on what a concentric contraction actually is.
   474→   663→A concentric contraction uses energy and will result in acceleration of an
   475→   664→object. When a muscle is activated and required to lift a load that is less than the
   476→   665→maximum tension it can generate, the muscle begins to shorten. This is referred
   477→   666→to as a concentric contraction. Two good examples of concentric contractions are
   478→   667→raising the weight during a biceps curl (see figure 2.3a) and coming out of the
   479→   668→bottom of a bench press (see figure 2.3b).
   480→   669→
   481→   670→A concentric contraction uses energy and will result in
   482→   671→acceleration of an object.
   483→   672→
   484→   673→Figure 2.3 (a) Concentric contractions during a biceps curl and (b) a bench press.
   485→   674→
   486→   675→However, the speed at which we perform these concentric movements
   487→   676→
   488→   677→distinguishes whether we are focusing more on strength or power. To increase
   489→   678→power, we must train both force (strength) and velocity (the ability to apply this
   490→   679→strength). This requires using heavier loads (force) and lighter loads (velocity).
   491→   680→During the concentric portion of a movement, the body as a protected device,
   492→   681→must reduce the velocity and force of the concentric portion of the rep to guard
   493→   682→against jerking the tendons or creating undue stress on the joint when coming to
   494→   683→an abrupt stop at the end range of motion. On the other hand, when training the
   495→   684→force side of the power equation, there is little deceleration because we are using
   496→   685→loads greater than 60 percent of 1RM. Due to the heavier load and slower speed,
   497→   686→there is no need for a longer range of deceleration. However, when training with
   498→   687→loads less than 60 percent, a larger portion of this concentric portion must be
   499→   688→used to decelerate the movement in order to protect the body as mentioned.
   500→   689→Therefore, to most efficiently train the velocity side of the equation, athletes use
   501→   690→ballistic exercises such as jumps and throws. Because the body is being thrown
   502→   691→into the air, it does not need to decelerate the load. As a result, higher velocities
   503→   692→can be attained because there is no deceleration.
   504→   693→
   505→   694→Deceleration
   506→   695→During the concentric contraction, we must both accelerate and decelerate the
   507→   696→load. Existing data show that as resistances increase toward 100 percent 1RM,
   508→   697→velocity decreases. This act of purposely slowing down the speed of the load or
   509→   698→movement is a natural reflex in order to avoid joint, tendon, or muscle injuries—
   510→   699→otherwise known as deceleration. Deceleration depends on the intensity of the
   511→   700→load we are lifting and the speed at which we are lifting it.
   512→   701→Here is a brief example of how a lighter load requires greater deceleration and
   513→   702→vice versa. Lighter loads can involve deceleration of up to 50 percent of the
   514→   703→entire concentric contraction. In the bench press, for example, someone using a
   515→   704→light load of 40 to 50 percent 1RM could be only pushing during the first half of
   516→   705→the exercise while the second half of the movement is spent decelerating. This
   517→   706→long deceleration time will have a sizeable impact on the average, or mean,
   518→   707→concentric velocity we receive from our training device. On the other hand, loads
   519→   708→over 65 to 75 percent 1RM should involve minimal or no deceleration because
   520→   709→the load being lifted is not being moved fast enough to affect the average or
   521→   710→mean velocity. There are ways to alter the mechanical profile of an exercise such
   522→   711→as utilizing bands or chains to decrease the decelerative portion of the lift. For
   523→   712→example, when using band resistance, the more stretch the band gets, the more
   524→   713→resistance it will offer. This forces the athlete to continue accelerating toward the
   525→   714→
   526→   715→end of the concentric action in order to overcome that resistance. This, in turn,
   527→   716→minimizes deceleration. These higher loads also mean lower velocities, resulting
   528→   717→in lower power outputs. Therefore, it is vitally important to use the correct type
   529→   718→of exercise and the correct means for measuring the velocity of that exercise.
   530→   719→This brings us to the three metrics we use for determining percentages and
   531→   720→strength zones as well as which method to use for specific exercises.
   532→   721→
   533→   722→Lighter loads can have deceleration of up to 50 percent of the
   534→   723→entire concentric contraction.

   536→   725→METRICS FOR MEASURING
   537→   726→Traditionally, resistance exercise intensity has been shown to be the most vital
   538→   727→factor in producing adaptations of muscular strength. In recent years, however,
   539→   728→another underused factor to consider and measure is movement velocity. When
   540→   729→measuring velocities with VBT, we should first be cognizant of what our training
   541→   730→focus is. Are we training for strength or power? When distinguishing between
   542→   731→strength and power, we must take into consideration both the speed at which the
   543→   732→bar or body is being moved in relation to the load and the amount of deceleration
   544→   733→involved.
   545→   734→
   546→   735→When distinguishing between strength and power, we must take
   547→   736→into consideration both the speed at which the bar or body is
   548→   737→being moved in relation to the load and the amount of
   549→   738→deceleration involved.
   550→   739→One study found that when training for speed or power using loads between
   551→   740→20 and 100 percent 1RM with nonballistic exercises such as your standard bench
   552→   741→press or squat, the relative load at which the braking phase (deceleration) no
   553→   742→longer existed was 76 percent of 1RM (Sanchez-Medina, Perez, and GonzalezBadillo 2010). This tells us that we can get more accurate percentages of a 1RM
   554→   743→using loads above 75 percent because there is more deceleration when using
   555→   744→lighter loads (remember, the body is being protected as it comes to an abrupt
   556→   745→
   557→   746→stop at the end range of motion). In summary, when training for strength, using
   558→   747→loads greater than 60 percent of 1RM increases the time spent accelerating
   559→   748→through a larger range of motion and eliminates much of the deceleration
   560→   749→component found when using lighter loads and higher velocities. Knowing
   561→   750→which training adaptation to look for will help you choose not only the exercise
   562→   751→but also the method of measurement to use, ultimately affecting the accuracy of
   563→   752→calculated speeds while using VBT.
   564→   753→
   565→   754→Mean Concentric Velocity
   566→   755→Mean concentric velocity (MCV) is simply the average speed during the entire
   567→   756→concentric portion of the exercise, including the time spent decelerating through
   568→   757→that range of motion. Because strength-based exercises consist of both
   569→   758→acceleration and deceleration phases, the mean concentric velocity metric should
   570→   759→be used (see figure 2.4). This makes MCV a KPI for training absolute strength
   571→   760→exercises such as the squats, deadlifts, and bench presses. It is also the best
   572→   761→single velocity measurement to use at the beginning of the off-season when those
   573→   762→exercises are performed using loads greater than 60 percent 1RM. For sports
   574→   763→where upper-body actions are important and may require more capacity during
   575→   764→the second half of the movement, MCV is the way to go.
   576→   765→

   578→   767→greater than 60 percent 1RM because they consist of both acceleration and less time spent
   579→   768→decelerating (because of the higher load).

   582→   771→during the entire concentric portion of the exercise, including
   583→   772→the time spent decelerating through that range of motion.
   584→   773→
   585→   774→Remember that exercise selection matters. While traditional strength
   586→   775→exercises (squats, bench presses, deadlifts, pull-ups, etc.) are best for training
   587→   776→with medium resistances and higher reps for muscle hypertrophy, or for heavier
   588→   777→resistances and lower reps for neural activation adaptations, the slower velocities
   589→   778→involved with these exercises do not make them, by themselves, ideal choices for
   590→   779→power development. These more explosive power movements can be more
   591→   780→accurately measured through the use of peak concentric velocity (PCV), which is
   592→   781→discussed next.
   593→   782→
   594→   783→Peak Concentric Velocity
   595→   784→A key to continuing to progress and create performance adaptations is increasing
   596→   785→the rate of force development, or the speed at which we are applying this newly
   597→   786→acquired strength. After a good block(s) of strength (greater than 60 percent
   598→   787→1RM) and dynamic strength (40 to 60 percent 1RM) are attained, higher speeds
   599→   788→—depending on what is needed for the sport—can and should be used. This is
   600→   789→generally programmed in the final four-to-eight–week block (preseason period)
   601→   790→when PCV can be used. This means that the method of measurement will change
   602→   791→and the exercise selection will likely change as well.
   603→   792→
   604→   793→PCV is simply the peak speed during the concentric portion of
   605→   794→the exercise, and it is usually calculated every 5 to 10
   606→   795→milliseconds.
   607→   796→PCV is simply the peak speed during the concentric portion of the exercise,
   608→   797→and it is usually calculated every 5 to 10 milliseconds. This metric is used for
   609→   798→ballistic or power-based exercises. Ballistic exercises are movements where
   610→   799→force is produced for a very short amount of time before the implement or body
   611→   800→is projected into the air. During a large portion of these ballistic movements, the
   612→   801→athlete is not actually applying force to the bar, so using an average of the entire
   613→   802→movement is less efficient because no deceleration is produced from trying to
   614→   803→lock out the rep (see figure 2.5). Exercises such as power cleans, snatches, bench
   615→   804→press throws, and jump squats are generally used in this period. Because of the
   616→   805→ballistic nature of these exercises, it is important to remember that, although
   617→   806→MCV is still usable, it would not be as accurate or efficient, and therefore, PCV
   618→   807→may be a better option.
   619→   808→
   620→   809→Mean Propulsive Velocity
   621→   810→Mean propulsive velocity (MPV) is a bit more involved, but it is a great method
   622→   811→for measuring and assessing true muscular power. Unfortunately, MPV is not
   623→   812→available on many wearable devices such as linear transducers. Nonetheless, we
   624→   813→need to understand the importance of MPV to be able to apply it when devices
   625→   814→such as Tendo and GymAware units are available.
   626→   815→While MCV refers to and measures the entire upward portion of a lift
   627→   816→(including the braking phase), its accuracy begins to get compromised at or less
   628→   817→than 60 percent of 1RM. MPV refers to and measures only the portion of the
   629→   818→upward movement during which the measured acceleration is greater than the
   630→   819→acceleration related to gravity. In other words, it happens before deceleration
   631→   820→occurs.
   632→   821→
   633→   822→Figure 2.5 Using a PCV metric during ballistic movements, such as the hang clean.
   634→   823→
   635→   824→MPV may be a more valid measurement later in the off-season for both
   636→   825→traditional exercises when dynamic strength (loads between 40 to 60 percent
   637→   826→1RM are being used, and greater deceleration occurs due to the necessity to
   638→   827→brake at the end of the range of motion) and heavier ballistic jumps are
   639→   828→performed at or around 40 to 50 percent 1RM when the deceleration is not
   640→   829→significant because the upward phase occurs only after the implement has either
   641→   830→left the hands or the body has left the ground (i.e., takeoff).
   642→   831→
   643→   832→Mean propulsive velocity refers to and measures only the
   644→   833→portion of the upward movement during which the measured
   645→   834→
   646→   835→acceleration is greater than the acceleration related to gravity.
   647→   836→Based on this mechanical principle, the use of MCV related to the entire
   648→   837→upward portion of a given movement may be more biased and a less effective
   649→   838→means of measurement than the use of MPV with lighter loads. A training
   650→   839→system based on MPV may be a better option for measuring performance at both
   651→   840→ends of the force–velocity curve, whether it be a jump or maximum dynamic
   652→   841→strength ability, such as a 1RM for a back squat.
   653→   842→This information suggests that, by referring the MCVs to the propulsive
   654→   843→phase, we can avoid underestimating an athlete’s true neuromuscular potential
   655→   844→when lifting light and medium loads while training for power simply by taking
   656→   845→out the measurement of the deceleration phase. Therefore, many coaches and
   657→   846→athletes believe that using MPV in training and testing settings allows for a more
   658→   847→effective method of measuring training for strength or power than MCV.
   659→   848→Velocity is a useful parameter to measure during resistance training. However,
   660→   849→it is imperative to use the correct measuring protocol to factor in the amount of
   661→   850→time spent in the deceleration phase of the movement. Mean (average) velocity,
   662→   851→especially with resistances more than 60 percent 1RM, correlates well with
   663→   852→strength exercises such as squats, deadlifts, and bench presses and can be used
   664→   853→when training strength as well as for determining 1RMs. Dynamic strength and
   665→   854→power adaptations, on the other hand, typically occur using the same exercise at
   666→   855→more moderate resistances (approximately 40 to 60 percent) and require more
   667→   856→deceleration. This makes mean propulsive velocities a more efficient method
   668→   857→because the decelerated portion of the lift is eliminated when calculating average
   669→   858→speeds. Finally, more ballistic types of exercises, such as cleans, snatches,
   670→   859→weighted jumps, and throws, are used in training for explosiveness.
   671→   860→Peak power may be a better option to calculate the fastest 5 to 10 meters per
   672→   861→second—the closest to in-game speeds—of these lifts or jumps, making them
   673→   862→great options during preseason training (see figure 2.6). In a study by SanchezMedina et al. (2010), the relative load that maximized mechanical power output
   674→   863→was determined using three different parameters: mean concentric velocity and
   675→   864→power, mean power of the concentric portion, and peak power. The load at which
   676→   865→the braking phase no longer existed was (regardless of the method used) 76.1
   677→   866→percent (+/− 7.4) of 1RM. This is more than likely due to having to apply force
   678→   867→for a longer period of time because of the heavier load. Maximum mechanical
   679→   868→power output was dependent on the parameter used, making it imperative to
   680→   869→
   681→   870→select the appropriate parameter according to the type of exercise being done as
   682→   871→well as the load being used.
