   125‚Üí   149‚Üí   149‚ÜíWhen it comes to enhancing functional strength, your own body can provide you
   126‚Üí   150‚Üí   150‚Üíwith not only the most accessible and cost-effective training tool but in many

   132‚Üí   156‚Üí   156‚Üíing can be done by anyone, regardless of fitness level.

   150‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚Üístrength in several muscle groups at once, develops relative strength, and improves
   151‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üíbalance and coordination (Harrison 2011).

   231‚Üí   255‚Üí   255‚ÜíJust about any gym, regardless of the population it serves, has kettlebells in its

   236‚Üí   260‚Üí   260‚Üí(Cotter 2022). Kettlebell training offers a safer alternative to heavy-load lifting,
   237‚Üí   261‚Üí   261‚Üítargeting weaknesses and imbalances and correcting them through short, high-
   238‚Üí   262‚Üí   262‚Üíintensity workouts that increase hormonal production for muscle development
   239‚Üí   263‚Üí   263‚Üíand keep cortisol levels low.
   240‚Üí   264‚Üí   264‚ÜíKettlebells have been studied more recently by strength and conditioning
   241‚Üí   265‚Üí   265‚Üíresearchers interested in these tools‚Äô ability to enhance strength, power, and car-
   242‚Üí   266‚Üí   266‚Üídiorespiratory fitness in individuals ranging from novice exercisers to elite-level
   243‚Üí   267‚Üí   267‚Üíathletes and fitness enthusiasts (Beardsley and Contreras 2014). The benefits of
   244‚Üí   268‚Üí   268‚Üíkettlebell training are numerous. Kettlebell training has been shown to increase
   245‚Üí   269‚Üí   269‚Üíoverall strength and power production (Jay et al. 2011); improve core strength by
   246‚Üí   270‚Üí   270‚Üíemphasizing power, acceleration, and multiplanar and unilateral movement pat-
   247‚Üí   271‚Üí   271‚Üíterns (Lake and Lauder 2012); encourage the production of force and speed through
   248‚Üí   272‚Üí   272‚Üíthe hips (Otto et al. 2012); and promote the development of lean body mass through
   249‚Üí   273‚Üí   273‚Üíhigh-repetition sets combined with short rest periods. Often-neglected muscles of
   250‚Üí   274‚Üí   274‚Üíthe back of the body (core, shoulders, hips) are targeted, which improves posture

   253‚Üí   277‚Üí   277‚Üíand posterior-chain function (Jay et al. 2011). Kettlebell training has also been
   254‚Üí   278‚Üí   278‚Üíshown to improve grip strength, which enables other strength gains (Manocchia
   255‚Üí   279‚Üí   279‚Üíet al. 2013).
   256‚Üí   280‚Üí   280‚ÜíBecause of its emphasis on higher volume and shorter rest periods, kettlebell
   257‚Üí   281‚Üí   281‚Üítraining is associated with increased caloric expenditure per unit of time (20
   258‚Üí   282‚Üí   282‚Üícalories per minute), resulting in increased metabolism during and after training,
   259‚Üí   283‚Üí   283‚Üíincreased fat metabolism, increased muscular endurance, and better cardiorespi-
   260‚Üí   284‚Üí   284‚Üíratory function (Jay et al. 2011). Kettlebells provide the user with one piece of
   261‚Üí   285‚Üí   285‚Üíexercise equipment to train the entire body (Jay et al. 2011). If you are limited with
   262‚Üí   286‚Üí   286‚Üíspace at home or want to minimize your movement around the gym, kettlebells
   263‚Üí   287‚Üí   287‚Üíare the perfect tools to simplify your workout. Kettlebell training uses dynamic
   264‚Üí   288‚Üí   288‚Üímovement patterns that increase mobility, proprioception, flexibility, and body
   265‚Üí   289‚Üí   289‚Üíawareness by forcing the user to move through all three planes of motion in a
   266‚Üí   290‚Üí   290‚Üícontrolled, fluid manner. With kettlebells, stabilizing muscle groups are trained
   267‚Üí   291‚Üí   291‚Üíin combination with primary muscles, leading to improved joint stability, coor-
   268‚Üí   292‚Üí   292‚Üídination, and balance (Manocchia et al. 2013). Finally, kettlebells offer a fun and
   269‚Üí   293‚Üí   293‚Üíexciting way to train because the exercises are based on movement patterns and
   270‚Üí   294‚Üí   294‚Üírequire you to be more engaged (Jay et al. 2011).

   272‚Üí   296‚Üí   296‚Üípalms facing in; supinated grip, with palms facing up; or pronated grip, with palms

   281‚Üí   305‚Üí   305‚ÜíResistance bands have become a staple of just about every fitness environment.

   346‚Üí   370‚Üí   370‚ÜíMedicine Ball Training
   347‚Üí   371‚Üí   371‚ÜíMedicine balls can be traced back to ancient Persia, Greece, and Rome, where
   348‚Üí   372‚Üí   372‚Üíthey were used by athletes to increase strength, power, and athletic performance.
   349‚Üí   373‚Üí   373‚ÜíIt is believed that the ancient Greeks stuffed animal skins for patients to toss for
   350‚Üí   374‚Üí   374‚Üímedicinal purposes. A similar type of ball is believed to have been used in Persia
   351‚Üí   375‚Üí   375‚Üíin the 1700s. The term medicine ball can be dated back to 1876 (Roberts 2020). Hip-
   352‚Üí   376‚Üí   376‚Üípocrates believed them to be an essential tool for helping people regain normal body
   353‚Üí   377‚Üí   377‚Üímovement after injury, as well as a means for enhancing and maintaining overall
   354‚Üí   378‚Üí   378‚Üíhealth. Few, if any, pieces of equipment are versatile in the way that medicine
   355‚Üí   379‚Üí   379‚Üíballs are: not only can they be lifted, but they can also be thrown and slammed.
   356‚Üí   380‚Üí   380‚ÜíThe adaptability of medicine balls enables users to utilize nontraditional methods
   357‚Üí   381‚Üí   381‚Üíto expand training goals and objectives. In addition, nearly every medicine ball
   358‚Üí   382‚Üí   382‚Üíexercise can be combined with other exercises to create a continuous flow with
   359‚Üí   383‚Üí   383‚Üíminimal rest. This is beneficial for two reasons:
   360‚Üí   384‚Üí   384‚Üí1. It mimics functional movements used in everyday life.

   362‚Üí   386‚Üí   386‚Üítions.

   364‚Üí   388‚Üí   388‚Üíand cardiorespiratory fitness.
   365‚Üí   389‚Üí   389‚ÜíMedicine balls can be soft or firm, can come with or without handles, and can
   366‚Üí   390‚Üí   390‚Üívary in weight from 2 to 100 pounds (0.9 to 45.4 kilograms). Medicine ball training
   367‚Üí   391‚Üí   391‚Üíhas a wide spectrum of applications, ranging from injury rehabilitation to strength
   368‚Üí   392‚Üí   392‚Üíand sports-performance enhancement (Faigenbaum et al. 2006; Hagberg et al.

   370‚Üí   394‚Üí   394‚Üíexplosive power through functional movement makes medicine ball training an

   372‚Üí   396‚Üí   396‚Üíand Washburn 2013). When training with a medicine ball, perform 10 to 15 reps
   373‚Üí   397‚Üí   397‚Üíor perform reps for a designated time, provided that you can do so with good form

   375‚Üí   399‚Üí   399‚Üíone heavy enough to reduce movement speed but not so heavy that technique,
   376‚Üí   400‚Üí   400‚Üíform, and range of motion will be negatively impacted. Medicine balls weighing
   377‚Üí   401‚Üí   401‚Üíbetween 4 and 15 pounds (1.8 to 6.8 kilograms) are generally recommended as a

   381‚Üí   405‚Üí   405‚Üíditure. This results in an increased postexercise metabolic effect as well. Training

   384‚Üí   408‚Üí   408‚Üíto developing strength in the context of sports that require rotational movement.

   518‚Üí   542‚Üí   542‚ÜíMetabolic Training
   519‚Üí   543‚Üí   543‚ÜíPeriodization is the variation of training over a period of time in order to achieve
   520‚Üí   544‚Üí   544‚Üían optimal outcome. Or, in more detailed terms, ‚ÄúPeriodization is a theoretical
   521‚Üí   545‚Üí   545‚Üíand practical construct that allows for the systematic, sequential, and integrative
   522‚Üí   546‚Üí   546‚Üíprograming of training interventions into mutually dependent periods of time
   523‚Üí   547‚Üí   547‚Üíin order to induce specific physiological adaptations that underpin performance
   524‚Üí   548‚Üí   548‚Üíoutcomes‚Äù (Haff 2016). Another way to think of it is that periodization is the con-
   525‚Üí   549‚Üí   549‚Üícept, and programming (sets, reps, exercises, weight used, days per week, etc.)
   526‚Üí   550‚Üí   550‚Üíis the application.
   527‚Üí   551‚Üí   551‚ÜíComponents of Periodization
   528‚Üí   552‚Üí   552‚ÜíThere are three concepts central to understanding periodization: general adap-
   529‚Üí   553‚Üí   553‚Üítation syndrome, stimulus‚Äìfatigue‚Äìrecovery‚Äìadaptation (SFRA) theory, and the
   530‚Üí   554‚Üí   554‚Üífitness‚Äìfatigue paradigm. In the following, we explain what they are and how
   531‚Üí   555‚Üí   555‚Üíthey fit into metabolic training.
   532‚Üí   556‚Üí   556‚ÜíGeneral Adaptation Syndrome
   533‚Üí   557‚Üí   557‚ÜíThis theory is the most fundamental to physical training. When you perform a
   534‚Üí   558‚Üí   558‚Üítraining session that takes your body beyond its current state, you‚Äôre applying
   535‚Üí   559‚Üí   559‚Üía stress to your systems. The body initially responds to the stress in an alarm
   536‚Üí   560‚Üí   560‚Üístage where fatigue is experienced possibly in the form of soreness or stiffness
   537‚Üí   561‚Üí   561‚Üíin, generally, the first 24 to 48 hours following your training session. After this
   538‚Üí   562‚Üí   562‚Üíalarm stage the body transitions into the resistance stage and the body further
   539‚Üí   563‚Üí   563‚Üíadapts to the stress. In the final stage, the body experiences what is known as
   540‚Üí   564‚Üí   564‚Üísupercompensation. Upon reaching the supercompensation stage, the body is at a
   541‚Üí   565‚Üí   565‚Üíhigher level of adaptation which means you are becoming stronger or fitter than
   542‚Üí   566‚Üí   566‚Üíyou were before the training.
   543‚Üí   567‚Üí   567‚ÜíStimulus‚ÄìFatigue‚ÄìRecovery‚ÄìAdaptation Theory
   544‚Üí   568‚Üí   568‚ÜíThe SFRA theory suggests that training stimuli produce a general response (Haff
   545‚Üí   569‚Üí   569‚Üí2016). The greater the magnitude of the stimulus or workload, the greater the
   546‚Üí   570‚Üí   570‚Üí25
   547‚Üí   571‚Üí   571‚Üí--- PAGE 35 ---
   548‚Üí   572‚Üí   572‚Üí26 | Metabolic Training
   549‚Üí   573‚Üí   573‚Üífatigue response. In other words, there is a direct positive correlation between
   550‚Üí   574‚Üí   574‚Üítraining workload and fatigue. As you adapt to the training workload, however,
   551‚Üí   575‚Üí   575‚Üíyour fatigue will lessen. When a new training stimulus is introduced, the adap-
   552‚Üí   576‚Üí   576‚Üítation cycle continues. The SFRA theory emphasizes timing or scheduling of
   553‚Üí   577‚Üí   577‚Üíinterventions and is essential to optimizing training effects. Keep in mind that
   554‚Üí   578‚Üí   578‚Üíif you stop training after a single intervention, you will return to your untrained
   555‚Üí   579‚Üí   579‚Üístate. Consistency is key to maximizing your results.
   556‚Üí   580‚Üí   580‚ÜíComplete recovery is often not reached after training interventions. However,
   557‚Üí   581‚Üí   581‚Üípartial recovery does often occur, and training programs should have the fre-
   558‚Üí   582‚Üí   582‚Üíquency, intensity, and volume of training adjusted in such a manner to optimize
   559‚Üí   583‚Üí   583‚Üíresults. The general trend is to gradually decrease volume of training while
   560‚Üí   584‚Üí   584‚Üíincreasing intensity. This concept is central to periodization.
   561‚Üí   585‚Üí   585‚ÜíFitness‚ÄìFatigue Paradigm
   562‚Üí   586‚Üí   586‚ÜíThe fitness‚Äìfatigue paradigm posits a relationship between fitness and fatigue

   566‚Üí   590‚Üí   590‚Üíwhere volume load refers to amount of weight times the number of repetitions
