    39‚Üí    39‚ÜíFour Components of Physical
    40‚Üí    40‚ÜíFitness
    41‚Üí    41‚ÜíThe process of getting in shape involves the four components of fitness‚Äî
    42‚Üí    42‚Üíendurance, stamina, economy, and speed‚Äîand these are developed in
    43‚Üí    43‚Üíphases. This is also their order of progression as you train. Figure 1.1, the

    69‚Üí    69‚ÜíEndurance (Phase I)
    70‚Üí    70‚ÜíEndurance is our first phase of training, and endurance is the ability of a
    71‚Üí    71‚Üímuscle to repeat a movement aerobically, no matter how much it slows
    72‚Üí    72‚Üí--- PAGE 21 ---
    73‚Üí    73‚Üídown, until it runs out of fuel. Jogging for fun and fitness and long runs for
    74‚Üí    74‚Üía marathon are classic examples of endurance development exercises. To
    75‚Üí    75‚Üídevelop the endurance for a marathon, for example, requires lengthening
    76‚Üí    76‚Üíeach of the weekly long runs by a couple of miles. As runners reach their
    77‚Üí    77‚Üícurrent level of endurance, they approach the point where fuel supplies of
    78‚Üí    78‚Üíglycogen run low. To meet today‚Äôs mileage goal, they slow down to finally
    79‚Üí    79‚Üímake it to the end of the workout. Endurance improves as muscles learn
    80‚Üí    80‚Üíhow to improve their metabolism of fat supplies. Simply put, it‚Äôs going
    81‚Üí    81‚Üífrom point A to point B no matter how much you have to slow down to
    82‚Üí    82‚Üílower levels of intensity. In general, basic aerobic endurance is developed at
    83‚Üí    83‚Üíheart rates of less than 75 percent of the maximum heart rate (MHR). Think
    84‚Üí    84‚Üíof this phase of conditioning as getting into shape. If you wish to jog to
    85‚Üí    85‚Üísimply stay healthy, this is as hard as you have to work.
    86‚Üí    86‚ÜíStamina (Phase II)
    87‚Üí    87‚ÜíStamina is the second phase of training, and stamina is the ability of a
    88‚Üí    88‚Üímuscle to repeat the movement at faster rates without exceeding your
    89‚Üí    89‚Üíanaerobic threshold. Think of it as the ability to go at this faster pace, but
    90‚Üí    90‚Üíover a shorter point A to point B distance. The objective of stamina training
    91‚Üí    91‚Üíis to improve your tolerance of oxygen debts by minimizing the use of
    92‚Üí    92‚Üícarbohydrate fuels (glycogen and glucose) while maximizing the use of
    93‚Üí    93‚Üífatty fuel. Consider this phase of conditioning as getting ready to race. The
    94‚Üí    94‚Üífitness emphasis in this phase is on preparing the cardiovascular and
    95‚Üí    95‚Üírespiratory systems to work hard without overreaching. Steady-state
    96‚Üí    96‚Üíworkouts at 75 to 80 percent MHR for 45 to 60 minutes are good examples
    97‚Üí    97‚Üíof stamina workouts. Tempo workouts at 80 to 85 percent for 20 to 30
    98‚Üí    98‚Üíminutes are great for improving stamina to a slightly higher level. If you are
    99‚Üí    99‚Üía recreational athlete who wants to participate in events and finish smiling,
   100‚Üí   100‚Üíthis is as hard as you need to work. In general, stamina is developed in heart
   101‚Üí   101‚Üírate zones of 75 to 85 percent MHR.
   102‚Üí   102‚ÜíEconomy (Phase III)
   103‚Üí   103‚ÜíEconomy is our third phase of training, and economy is the ability of the
   104‚Üí   104‚Üímuscle to repeat movements at an even faster rate, albeit for a shorter time,
   105‚Üí   105‚Üíabove its anaerobic threshold. Economy training develops the ability to
   106‚Üí   106‚Üí--- PAGE 22 ---
   107‚Üí   107‚Üíhandle race paces while tolerating increasing levels of oxygen debt, which
   108‚Üí   108‚Üíleads to the accumulation of lactic acid in the muscles. Economy workouts
   109‚Üí   109‚Üítrain the muscle cells how to optimize the use of oxygen and carbohydrate
   110‚Üí   110‚Üífuel at this higher level of effort. Think of this phase of conditioning as
   111‚Üí   111‚Üíimproving your racing fitness by adding more horsepower to your engine
   112‚Üí   112‚Üíthrough workouts such as interval training, hill sprints, and fartlek running.
   113‚Üí   113‚Üí(Fartlek, which literally means ‚Äúspeed play,‚Äù is a Swedish system of
   114‚Üí   114‚Üíconditioning that features frequent changes in speed.) Good examples of
   115‚Üí   115‚Üíeconomy workouts are those at moderate to high intensity for continuous
   116‚Üí   116‚Üítempo paces, or interval workouts featuring longer repetitions. If you wish
   117‚Üí   117‚Üíto compete in athletic events, working this hard has to be included in your
   118‚Üí   118‚Üítraining program. In general, economy is developed in heart rate zones of
   119‚Üí   119‚Üí85 to 95 percent MHR.
   120‚Üí   120‚ÜíSpeed (Phase IV)
   121‚Üí   121‚ÜíSpeed is our fourth and final phase of training, and speed is the ability of a
   122‚Üí   122‚Üímuscle to repeat the movement at its maximum rate at full power under
   123‚Üí   123‚Üíanaerobic conditions. It is the ability to go at top effort for short periods of
   124‚Üí   124‚Üítime and to stay relaxed while tolerating increasing levels of lactic acid in
   125‚Üí   125‚Üíthe muscle tissue. The power training in this phase also will bring about
   126‚Üí   126‚Üífinal improvements in strength, flexibility, and coordination. Uphill sprints
   127‚Üí   127‚Üíand interval workouts of shorter, faster, and maximum-intensity repeats
   128‚Üí   128‚Üíwith long and full recoveries are the best examples of speed workouts.
   129‚Üí   129‚ÜíSome might describe this wish to compete at full capacity as joining the
   130‚Üí   130‚ÜíP.T.A. (pain, torture, and agony) Society as you learn how to withstand the
   131‚Üí   131‚Üímaximal exertion. In general, speed is developed in heart rate zones of 95 to
   132‚Üí   132‚Üí100 percent MHR and often at speeds in excess of race pace.
   133‚Üí   133‚ÜíWe fully recognize that these are not exactly dictionary definitions or
   134‚Üí   134‚Üístandard laboratory usage; however, they are commonly used in the popular

   210‚Üí   210‚ÜíRate of Perceived Exertion: Your
   211‚Üí   211‚ÜíBackup System
   212‚Üí   212‚ÜíIndividualizing workouts assumes that you understand the difference
   213‚Üí   213‚Üíbetween hard and easy effort. (The former makes you grimace and gasp for
   214‚Üí   214‚Üíair, whereas the latter allows you to laugh and talk all the way home.) Each
   215‚Üí   215‚Üíof us can make subjective distinctions between hard and easy tasks. Before
   216‚Üí   216‚Üítelemetric heart rate monitors made counting pulses so convenient, the rate
   217‚Üí   217‚Üíof perceived exertion (RPE), as illustrated by the Borg Scale, was the
   218‚Üí   218‚Üístandard for using one‚Äôs own capacity for subjective measurement of hard
   219‚Üí   219‚Üíand easy exercise effort. Dr. Borg‚Äôs 15-point scale could be used, for
   220‚Üí   220‚Üíexample, to describe how average people might feel as they progressed
   221‚Üí   221‚Üífrom walking a mile to trying to run it in 4 minutes. The scale described
   222‚Üí   222‚Üíresting or walking as a very, very light effort, whereas exhaustion is
   223‚Üí   223‚Üí--- PAGE 26 ---
   224‚Üí   224‚Üídescribed as beyond very, very hard. In addition, the Borg Scale also
   225‚Üí   225‚Üíintroduced a value system of points ranging from 6 (resting) to 20
   226‚Üí   226‚Üí(exhaustion). In short, Borg tried to correlate quantitative measures with his
   227‚Üí   227‚Üíqualitative system.
   228‚Üí   228‚ÜíAlthough the Borg Scale is useful, it is an arbitrary system because it
   229‚Üí   229‚Üíoffers no objective way to measure exertion. For the competitive athlete, a
   230‚Üí   230‚Üíheart rate monitor is a better way to quantify those measurements.
   231‚Üí   231‚ÜíProbably the most important reason for individualizing training is the
   232‚Üí   232‚Üífact that people have different workout objectives. What exactly, as a
   233‚Üí   233‚Üípercentage of your maximum, are your target heart rate zones? The need to
   234‚Üí   234‚Üícalculate these zones is the challenge that brought us to share our collective
   235‚Üí   235‚Üíwisdom with you. So, there are plenty of reasons for using the information
   236‚Üí   236‚Üíin this book to help you use your heart rate monitor intelligently. Your body
   237‚Üí   237‚Üíis not like anyone else‚Äôs, so why work out like anyone else? Whatever your
   238‚Üí   238‚Üíunique genetic gifts, current level of fitness, or goals are, this book will help

   280‚Üí   280‚ÜíUnderstanding Heart Rate
   281‚Üí   281‚ÜíThe simple beauty of monitoring heart rate is that it is based on your own
   282‚Üí   282‚Üíheart‚Äôs capacity and nobody else‚Äôs. The heart is a muscle, and it responds in
   283‚Üí   283‚Üímuch the same way as any other muscle by getting bigger and stronger as
   284‚Üí   284‚Üíyou work out. When you are not working out, the heart continues to pump
   285‚Üí   285‚Üíblood into your muscles to feed their repair and recovery. That‚Äôs why heart
   286‚Üí   286‚Üírate can inform you indirectly about the state of recovery of your muscles.
   287‚Üí   287‚ÜíIf you still have microtears or are replacing fuel, your metabolism will be
   288‚Üí   288‚Üíelevated, and your heart rate will reflect that by being slightly elevated.
   289‚Üí   289‚ÜíThat‚Äôs why monitoring and recording your heart rate each morning when
   290‚Üí   290‚Üí--- PAGE 28 ---
   291‚Üí   291‚Üíyou wake up will give you an idea of whether you‚Äôre still under a condition
   292‚Üí   292‚Üíof repair from a previous workout. This is why your morning resting heart
   293‚Üí   293‚Üírate is one of the two most important numbers. Your maximum heart rate
   294‚Üí   294‚Üí(MHR) is the other. Maximum heart rate is the fastest or highest number of
   295‚Üí   295‚Üítimes the heart can beat in a minute.
   296‚Üí   296‚ÜíMaximum heart rate doesn‚Äôt really change as a result of training, but all
   297‚Üí   297‚Üíyour training zones are calculated from that number. Therefore, you need an
   298‚Üí   298‚Üíaccurate maximum heart rate (see the Determining Maximum Heart Rate
   299‚Üí   299‚Üísection in chapter 2). Resting heart rate, on the other hand, does change
   300‚Üí   300‚Üíwith training and generally decreases with increasing fitness. At times,
   301‚Üí   301‚Üíresting heart rate can increase, which usually indicates fatigue, overtraining,
   302‚Üí   302‚Üíor sickness. Charting your resting heart rate can help identify these
   303‚Üí   303‚Üíconditions early.

   324‚Üí   324‚ÜíIntensity Relationship of Heart Rate
   325‚Üí   325‚Üíand Oxygen Consumption
   326‚Üí   326‚ÜíOur discussion so far has focused on heart rate. However, oxygen uptake, a
   327‚Üí   327‚Üíclose cousin of heart rate, is often mentioned in the popular literature.
   328‚Üí   328‚ÜíExperts seem to contradict each other or, at the very least, confuse readers
   329‚Üí   329‚Üías they recommend that training take place at various percentages of either
   330‚Üí   330‚Üíof these physical capacities. Furthermore, oxygen uptake capacity is
   331‚Üí   331‚Üíabbreviated different ways, sometimes as O or O uptake. At any rate,
   332‚Üí   332‚Üí2 2
   333‚Üí   333‚ÜíO refers to the volume of oxygen consumed, and O max refers to the
   334‚Üí   334‚Üí2 2
   335‚Üí   335‚Üí--- PAGE 30 ---
   336‚Üí   336‚Üígreatest amount of oxygen someone can consume, which usually occurs at
   337‚Üí   337‚Üímaximum exercise intensity. In general, the fitter you are, the higher your
   338‚Üí   338‚ÜíO max will be. Both heart rate and O are ways to measure how hard
   339‚Üí   339‚Üí2 2
   340‚Üí   340‚Üí(intensely) someone is working. Heart rate response is simply a measure of
   341‚Üí   341‚Üíhow hard the cardiovascular system is working to distribute oxygen,
   342‚Üí   342‚Üíwhereas O measures include heart rate and the work of the respiratory
   343‚Üí   343‚Üí2
   344‚Üí   344‚Üísystem‚Äôs and muscular system‚Äôs use of oxygen.
   345‚Üí   345‚ÜíDuring aerobic exercise, both heart rate and O increase as intensity
   346‚Üí   346‚Üí2
   347‚Üí   347‚Üíincreases. However, the patterns of response for both variables are different.
   348‚Üí   348‚ÜíIn science, terms such as linear and nonlinear are used to describe
   349‚Üí   349‚Üírelationships between two variables. The term linear means that as one
   350‚Üí   350‚Üívariable increases, the other variable increases in proportion to the change
   351‚Üí   351‚Üíin the first variable. If one variable changes and the other changes but not in
   352‚Üí   352‚Üíproportion, that is described as nonlinear.
   353‚Üí   353‚ÜíAlthough both heart rate and O increase in response to increasing
   354‚Üí   354‚Üí2
   355‚Üí   355‚Üíintensity, their responses are not entirely linear. For example, at lower
   356‚Üí   356‚Üíexercise intensities, both variables increase in a similar (linear) manner, but
   357‚Üí   357‚Üíat higher intensities, the heart rate response levels off (nonlinear) while the
   358‚Üí   358‚ÜíO response continues to climb. This does not detract from the value of the
   359‚Üí   359‚Üí2
   360‚Üí   360‚Üíinformation but instead shows that responses vary.
   361‚Üí   361‚ÜíAnother important difference in response patterns is that we see a
   362‚Üí   362‚Üídramatic and rapid increase in heart rate at the start of exercise regardless of
   363‚Üí   363‚Üíthe intensity. Someone who begins to jog slowly may see a 25- to 40-beat
   364‚Üí   364‚Üíincrease in heart rate right away that could go, for example, from 75 to 110
   365‚Üí   365‚Üíbpm. This heart rate will settle and may even decrease slightly after 5 to 10
   366‚Üí   366‚Üíminutes and eventually remain at 105 bpm. On the other hand, O
   367‚Üí   367‚Üí2
   368‚Üí   368‚Üíresponds slowly at the start of exercise, and we may see only a small
   369‚Üí   369‚Üíchange in the first 5 to 7 minutes that continues at a slow, steady pace, even
   370‚Üí   370‚Üíwith abrupt changes in intensity. Heart rate will continue to respond
   371‚Üí   371‚Üíabruptly at the lower intensities and then smooth out and change more
   372‚Üí   372‚Üíslowly at the higher intensities. It is important to understand this
   373‚Üí   373‚Üírelationship because it can help explain aspects of performance such as
   374‚Üí   374‚Üíwarm-up and recovery. Let‚Äôs look at the overall relationship of heart rate,
   375‚Üí   375‚ÜíO , and intensity in more detail.
   376‚Üí   376‚Üí2
   377‚Üí   377‚Üí--- PAGE 31 ---

   569‚Üí   569‚ÜíBetween Heart Rate and O

   573‚Üí   573‚Üí2
   574‚Üí   574‚Üívariables can change with appropriate training over time. An important
   575‚Üí   575‚Üípoint is that there is a subtle difference in the response of both heart rate
   576‚Üí   576‚Üíand O . As mentioned previously, heart rate is linear only up to 90 to 95
   577‚Üí   577‚Üí2
   578‚Üí   578‚Üípercent MHR, and then it smooths out and changes little in response to
   579‚Üí   579‚Üíincreasing workload. On the other hand, O continues to increase fairly
   580‚Üí   580‚Üí2
   581‚Üí   581‚Üísteadily all the way to maximum capacity. Figure 1.3 (see page 17) shows

   584‚Üí   584‚Üítest. If you take a close look at the data, you‚Äôll see that heart rate reaches
   585‚Üí   585‚Üímaximum before O does. This means that you can increase intensity and
   586‚Üí   586‚Üí2
   587‚Üí   587‚Üíenergy expenditure at maximum heart rate but not O max.
   588‚Üí   588‚Üí2
   589‚Üí   589‚ÜíAs intensity increases, so does energy expenditure, and O reflects
   590‚Üí   590‚Üí2
   591‚Üí   591‚Üíenergy expenditure better than heart rate does. When we test subjects in the
   592‚Üí   592‚Üílab, we always determine energy expenditure from the oxygen consumption
   593‚Üí   593‚Üírates or O uptake. To do this, we need to understand the basic relationship
   594‚Üí   594‚Üí2
   595‚Üí   595‚Üíbetween energy expenditure (calories burned) and O . When people get a
   596‚Üí   596‚Üí2

   598‚Üí   598‚Üí2
   599‚Üí   599‚Üíkilogram of body weight per minute (ml/kg/min). However, these data are
   600‚Üí   600‚Üíactually recorded in liters per minute (L/min) and then converted to
   601‚Üí   601‚Üímilliliters afterward. Consider this example of a 60-kilogram runner:
   602‚Üí   602‚ÜíRunner weight: 60 kg
   603‚Üí   603‚ÜíO max in L/min: 4.0 L/min
