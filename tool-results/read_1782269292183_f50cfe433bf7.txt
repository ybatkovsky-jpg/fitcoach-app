    35‚Üí    35‚ÜíCREATING STRENGTH TRAINING
    36‚Üí    36‚ÜíADAPTATIONS
    37‚Üí    37‚ÜíDeveloping strength involves more than just lifting heavy loads. The
    38‚Üí    38‚Üíreality is that force comes in many shapes and sizes and while we know
    39‚Üí    39‚Üíby definition strength is the ability of a muscle or muscle group to
    40‚Üí    40‚Üígenerate force, having a well-rounded approach to achieving strength
    41‚Üí    41‚Üígains is a smarter alternative than just lifting heavy. In this chapter,
    42‚Üí    42‚Üíwe‚Äôll discuss two methods of strength development that will be critical to
    43‚Üí    43‚Üíyour success in the gym. We‚Äôll discuss why you should consider utilizing
    44‚Üí    44‚Üíthese methods along with their benefits, their reasoning, and how they
    45‚Üí    45‚Üíplay a part in keeping your programming balanced (in order to avoid
    46‚Üí    46‚Üíthings like overtraining). For more information about other training
    47‚Üí    47‚Üímethods, see chapter 3.
    48‚Üí    48‚ÜíDynamic Effort Training
    49‚Üí    49‚ÜíIt‚Äôs a common misconception that you need to lift heavy in every workout.
    50‚Üí    50‚ÜíMany think that the effectiveness of training is judged by how hard it
    51‚Üí    51‚Üíis. But the human body isn‚Äôt a gumball machine‚Äîit doesn‚Äôt spit out
    52‚Üí    52‚Üícandy every time you put a quarter in. That‚Äôs because there is a point of
    53‚Üí    53‚Üídiminishing returns. If you‚Äôre not allowing for proper recovery between
    54‚Üí    54‚Üíhigher-threshold, or maximal lifting, sessions (sessions that are more
    55‚Üí    55‚Üídemanding on the central nervous system), you‚Äôll eventually overtrain
    56‚Üí    56‚Üíand start losing your progress or get injured. The reality is that hard
    57‚Üí    57‚Üítraining sessions need to be interspersed with easier training sessions.
    58‚Üí    58‚ÜíIt‚Äôs also important to incorporate strength methods that differ with
    59‚Üí    59‚Üíregard to bar velocity.
    60‚Üí    60‚ÜíWhat does this mean to the average individual who wants to get
    61‚Üí    61‚Üístronger? It means that there is low-hanging fruit to be picked with
    62‚Üí    62‚Üímethods such as dynamic effort (DE) training, which aims to improve
    63‚Üí    63‚Üístrength through nonmaximal measures that activate high-threshold
    64‚Üí    64‚Üímotor units to a high degree. The goal of this method is to use nonmaximal
    65‚Üí    65‚Üíloads with the highest attainable velocity (bar speed) to improve the rate
    66‚Üí    66‚Üíof force development (RFD) and increase the number of recruited and
    67‚Üí    67‚Üítrained motor units (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021).
    68‚Üí    68‚ÜíThese are the key features of DE training:
    69‚Üí    69‚Üí‚Ä¢ Uses high-threshold motor units and facilitates RFD
    70‚Üí    70‚Üí‚Ä¢ Places high demands on the nervous system
    71‚Üí    71‚Üí‚Ä¢ Works best with accommodating resistance (AR) to ensure proper
    72‚Üí    72‚Üíloading throughout full range of motion (ROM). AR is the use of
    73‚Üí    73‚Üíbands or chains to provide maximal tension through full ROM.
    74‚Üí    74‚Üí--- PAGE 223 ---
    75‚Üí    75‚ÜíStrength Programs 207
    76‚Üí    76‚Üí‚Ä¢ Must be separated from maximal effort training by at least 72 hours
    77‚Üí    77‚Üí‚Ä¢ Works the velocity portion of the force-velocity curve
    78‚Üí    78‚ÜíIn short, DE training is used in this book both for strength development
    79‚Üí    79‚Üíand athletic performance. This method maximizes the velocity component
    80‚Üí    80‚Üíof the force-velocity curve while creating balanced programming to
    81‚Üí    81‚Üíreduce the risk of overtraining.
    82‚Üí    82‚ÜíSubmaximal Effort Training
    83‚Üí    83‚ÜíThe repeated effort method, covered in chapter 3, is an integral aspect
    84‚Üí    84‚Üíof every program in this book. While there are some similarities between
    85‚Üí    85‚Üíit and the submaximal effort method, the major difference is that
    86‚Üí    86‚Üísubmaximal work is delivered exclusively through bilateral movements
    87‚Üí    87‚Üí(i.e., squats, presses, pulls). The submaximal effort method will be
    88‚Üí    88‚Üíused as a multiple-repetition maximum scenario (two- to six-rep max)
    89‚Üí    89‚Üíbecause true maximal effort training consists of a singular one-rep
    90‚Üí    90‚Üímaximal load set.
    91‚Üí    91‚ÜíThe goal with the submaximal effort method is twofold: (1) to develop
    92‚Üí    92‚Üíproper motor patterns with bilateral movements approaching heavier
    93‚Üí    93‚Üíloads and (2) to improve strength qualities via recruitment of high-
    94‚Üí    94‚Üíthreshold motor units and thus improve neuromuscular efficiency.
    95‚Üí    95‚ÜíFurthermore, this method is a prerequisite for maximal effort training,
    96‚Üí    96‚Üímeaning that one should have first developed efficient movement
    97‚Üí    97‚Üíbefore considering attempting maximal effort lifts. Moreover, the
    98‚Üí    98‚Üísubmaximal effort method can be used strategically for autoregulation.
    99‚Üí    99‚ÜíStress affects all individuals differently, and asking someone to adhere
   100‚Üí   100‚Üíto rigid percentages (five sets of three reps at 75 percent of one-rep
   101‚Üí   101‚Üímax) does not account for daily fluctuations. Using submaximal effort
   102‚Üí   102‚Üítraining allows the individual to build up in weight based on how they
   103‚Üí   103‚Üíare feeling on any given day. This method is also easier to navigate
   104‚Üí   104‚Üíbecause building to a heavy triple for the day is less confusing than
   105‚Üí   105‚Üíadhering to strict percentage-based guidelines. Submaximal effort
   106‚Üí   106‚Üítraining works both aspects of the force-velocity curve while creating
   107‚Üí   107‚Üíbalanced programming to reduce the risk of overtraining.
