  4572‚ÜíSpecificity, Overload, and Progression
  4573‚ÜíA resistance training program is planned around the needs, goals, and current
  4574‚Üícondition of the clients. Effective, long-term adaptation to a resistance training
  4575‚Üístimulus is guided by key principles that allow the body to adapt to the stress
  4576‚Üíresponse from resistance training. This stress response can be created by a
  4577‚Üímanipulation of the three primary principles of resistance training. These
  4578‚Üíthree principles are specificity, overload, and progression. The goal of exercise
  4579‚Üíprogram design is to manipulate the variables of specificity, overload, and
  4580‚Üíprogression for a desired outcome. This creates a training overload and causes
  4581‚Üíphysical adaptation.

  4587‚ÜíPrinciple of Specificity
  4588‚ÜíOur body adapts to the specific demands imposed on it. This principle of specificity is critical to understand! The specific adaptation to imposed demands
  4589‚Üí(SAID) principle of training means that, for example, when a client works with
  4590‚Üíbarbells, their body will adapt to that stimulus, then when the same exercises
  4591‚Üíare performed with dumbbells, they will readapt. Each change of equipment
  4592‚Üíis a different stimulus and each new exercise provides a different training
  4593‚Üíoverload, even when using the same muscle group.
  4594‚ÜíThis also means that when transitioning new clients from machine-based
  4595‚Üítraining into a small group with different equipment, there will be a whole new
  4596‚Üíset of adaptation challenges. Balance, coordination, body control, and stabilization are new constraints that the individual needs to develop in order to
  4597‚Üíperform skills correctly. For a client who‚Äôs been training on a machine, the pads
  4598‚Üíof the machine stabilize and hold the body in place, the arms of the machine
  4599‚Üíguide the limbs, and although strength is built, less control, stabilization, or
  4600‚Üíbalance is involved. Transitioning the client to standing exercises with elastic
  4601‚Üíresistance bands means that loads need to be lighter, communication needs to
  4602‚Üíbe clear and concise, and exercise progressions and regressions become critical.
  4603‚ÜíThe beauty of the specificity principle is that when training with simple exercises that represent functional movements found in daily life, such as squats,
  4604‚Üílunges, pushing, pulling, and rotational movements, an improvement in elements like coordination, balance, and body control is seen, thereby enhancing
  4605‚Üísports performance or activities of daily life. Clients who train functionally
  4606‚Üíwill naturally develop more coordination, body control, and balance, leading
  4607‚Üíto greater movement efficiency.
  4608‚Üí
  4609‚ÜíPrinciple of Overload
  4610‚ÜíTo produce desired adaptations to a training program, the variables of the exercise program must be manipulated to create an overload on the physiological
  4611‚Üísystems of the body. Overload can be achieved by increasing the load, increasing the volume (number of reps or sets), or decreasing rest between exercises
  4612‚Üíor sets. In other words, for training to be effective, it‚Äôs necessary to drive the
  4613‚Üíbody to work harder than what it‚Äôs used to doing. The level of overload must
  4614‚Üíbe appropriate to the training experience and fitness level of the clients. When
  4615‚Üíthis principle is not correctly applied, injuries are more likely to occur. Muscles
  4616‚Üígain strength more rapidly than tendons and ligaments, so appropriate loading
  4617‚Üíis an important consideration. For deconditioned and inexperienced clients,
  4618‚Üíless intensity can provide adequate overload.
  4619‚ÜíVolume is defined as the total amount of work performed (weight lifted)
  4620‚Üíduring an exercise session. It is generally expressed as a product of intensity √ó
  4621‚Üísets √ó number of repetitions. The total volume of a workout must be dictated
  4622‚Üíby training experience and goals of the clients. Volume increases time under
  4623‚Üítension (TUT), which provides the training stimulus for muscular hypertrophy.
  4624‚ÜíGreater volume also requires greater amounts of time and should therefore be
  4625‚Üíconsidered when planning your classes. Most critical is the fact that high-volume
  4626‚Üítraining is a natural progression and is unsuitable for novice exercisers. When
  4627‚Üítraining deconditioned individuals, lower workout volumes are more appropriate.
  4628‚ÜíIntensity is defined as the specific amount of resistance or external load
  4629‚Üíapplied to muscles. If the same resistive force is consistently applied, then
  4630‚Üí
  4631‚ÜíProgramming Components and Training Variables‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ 97
  4632‚Üí
  4633‚Üíthe muscle will not be stimulated and a training overload will not be created.
  4634‚ÜíIntensity is written as a percentage of the maximum amount of weight lifted
  4635‚Üífor one repetition, or one-repetition max (1RM). Greater intensities increase
  4636‚Üímotor unit recruitment and muscle force production, which translates into
  4637‚Üígains in strength and power. The external load must also be appropriately
  4638‚Üíapplied. Again, for deconditioned individuals, less intensity is more appropriate. For those groups, lower percentages of the 1RM are important to prevent
  4639‚Üíinjuries. As clients gain experience, greater levels of intensity can be tolerated
  4640‚Üíand are necessary to continue making gains. Experienced clients will benefit
  4641‚Üífrom training at higher percentages of the 1RM.

  4715‚ÜíPeriodization
  4716‚ÜíResistance training programs consist of numerous variables, including load
  4717‚Üí(intensity), volume (reps and sets), frequency, rest interval, exercise selection,
  4718‚Üíand exercise order (Williams et al., 2017). Periodization is defined as planned,
  4719‚Üícyclical (i.e., the program repeats) manipulation of these resistance training
  4720‚Üívariables to attain peak performance at specific times of the year (Evans, 2019).
  4721‚ÜíIn small-group training, an important consideration may be that load is not
  4722‚Üíalways a variable that can easily be manipulated. Bodyweight exercises and
  4723‚Üícommonly used equipment such as rubber resistance bands and suspension
  4724‚Üítraining systems limit how much load can be added; therefore other variables
  4725‚Üí(volume, rest interval, exercise selection, and exercise order) may need to be
  4726‚Üíadjusted for SGT programming when considering the objectives of the group.
  4727‚ÜíPeriodization training originated in sports with an annual competitive season,
  4728‚Üíwhich require different levels of training volume, intensity, and specificity;
  4729‚Üíthese variables are manipulated depending on the competition schedule and
  4730‚Üíthe athlete‚Äôs goal. The method was developed to prevent overtraining, help prevent plateaus, and build optimal performance and fitness for the competitive
  4731‚Üíseason. Evans (2019) cites research indicating that lengthy periods of training
  4732‚Üíthat lack variation results in stagnation and fatigue. Consequently, one of the
  4733‚Üímain purposes of periodization is to provide structure to the variability in
  4734‚Üíthe training components to decrease the likelihood of a plateau or decline in
  4735‚Üíphysical (and mental) performance. The success of periodization in sports has
  4736‚Üíbrought the protocol into mainstream fitness settings and with smart planning,
  4737‚Üícan be incorporated into SGT settings.
  4738‚ÜíThere are three basic periodization models: linear, block, and undulating or
  4739‚Üínonlinear. The two focused on here are linear and undulating. The training cycles
  4740‚Üíare broken into phases that focus on one or more goals. The phase length can
  4741‚Üívary from sport to sport, depending on the length of the general physical conditioning, sports-specific training, and competitive season. For general conditioning
  4742‚Üífor small-group training, the phases for linear periodization may be as follows:

  4909‚ÜíMovement-Based Training
  4910‚ÜíMovement-based training, also known as functional training, is useful for individuals of all ages and abilities. It‚Äôs important to understand how all the body‚Äôs
  4911‚Üísystems interact and work together, whether it‚Äôs finding a balance between
  4912‚Üíthe relationships of joint stability or appreciating how the body can create
  4913‚Üícompensations for weakness. Recognizing how each system can support the
  4914‚Üíenhancement of fitness and decrease the potential for injury assists trainers
  4915‚Üíin designing goal-specific programs.
  4916‚Üí
  4917‚ÜíElements of Human Movement
  4918‚ÜíWhen broken into its raw components, human
  4919‚Üímovement is a blend of multiple elements,
  4920‚Üíwith each intrinsically linked to the next‚Äîstability and mobility; coordination and balance;
  4921‚Üístrength, flexibility, and endurance; and a
  4922‚Üímotor program (see figure 9.4). This cyclical
  4923‚Üílinkage means that each element is dependent on the other. If any of these elements
  4924‚Üíare missing, movement efficiency decreases.
  4925‚ÜíMovement-based training builds whole-body
  4926‚Üícoordination, which in turn enhances the stability and mobility relationships, which improve
  4927‚Üístrength, flexibility, and endurance, which in
  4928‚Üíturn will develop balance and the motor program. Ultimately, this entire cycle boosts fitness
  4929‚Üíand well-being and decreases the potential for
  4930‚Üíinjury and movement deficiencies.

  5037‚Üíefficiency. These compensatory movement patterns become so ingrained that the
  5038‚Üíindividual may not be conscious of the habit. Think of someone walking with a limp.
  5039‚ÜíWithout being aware, they favor one side of the body and their gait changes to accommodate pain and the movement pattern then becomes habitual.
