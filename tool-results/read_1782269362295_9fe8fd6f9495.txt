   354→   354→Repeated Effort Method
   355→   355→The repeated effort method is a premier method of improving muscular
   356→   356→imbalance and muscular hypertrophy and providing rehabilitative or
   357→   357→prehabilitative work to ensure you’re constantly improving symmetry.
   358→   358→This work is done through single-joint exercises and isolation work to
   359→   359→target musculature limitations. It’s important to prioritize unilateral
   360→   360→assistance exercises because you can dedicate time to improving
   361→   361→limitations, and classic lifts such as the squat, press, and deadlift will
   362→   362→improve through establishing symmetry and strength in lagging muscle
   363→   363→groups. Because unilateral exercises are less demanding on the nervous
   364→   364→system, you are able to add volume and frequency with the overall
   365→   365→objective of improving deficiencies. The volume of assistance exercises
   366→   366→can be pushed to high levels and align with your training age and needs
   367→   367→quite well with less risk. Moreover, people often fail to take inventory of
   368→   368→where they struggle the most and experiment with exercises that will
   369→   369→potentially help improve their limiting factors. This is what assistance
   370→   370→exercises are for—to strategically improve limitations in a muscle or
   371→   371→muscle group that may be holding one back, or worse, increasing their risk
   372→   372→of injury in the future. A simple example of this is the glutes, which extend
   373→   373→the hips and abduct and externally rotate the legs. They are therefore
   374→   374→responsible for the finishing, hip extension–based portions of the squat
   375→   375→and deadlift. Performing exercises that duplicate these actions is key.
   376→   376→The Romanian deadlift (RDL) and the glute hip thrust are two excellent
   377→   377→choices to build glute strength around hip extension movements.

   379→   379→but many people tend to have the primary goal of improving body
   380→   380→composition. Luckily, assistance exercise work can be instrumental in
   381→   381→helping add lean muscle mass because the 8- to 12-rep range will facilitate
   382→   382→muscular hypertrophy (also known as sarcoplasmic hypertrophy), which
   383→   383→is an increase in the volume of sarcoplasmic fluid in the muscle cell

   390→   390→strength development. (Hypertrophy work prioritizes time under tension,
   391→   391→whereas DE work prioritizes bar velocity to improve RFD.) The ultimate
   392→   392→goal here is to improve and isolate lagging muscle groups, so single-joint
   393→   393→exercises like split squats, single-leg RDLs, and glute hip thrust variations

   395→   395→Benefits of Prioritizing
   396→   396→Assistance Exercises
   397→   397→The benefits of prioritizing assistance exercises (roughly 80 per-
   398→   398→cent of your training volume in a given session) are understated.

   400→   400→squat with two dumbbells, but the latter allows you to execute higher

   403→   403→synchronization. The benefits of prioritization are the following:
   404→   404→Focuses Lagging Muscle Groups
   405→   405→In an effort to target where you may be lacking, we can add a high
   406→   406→volume of work without the risk of exacerbating compensation
   407→   407→patterns—this will carry over to bigger movements like squats
   408→   408→and deadlifts by improving muscle function and coordination with
   409→   409→other muscles.
   410→   410→Safely Creates Higher Volume Through Unilateral
   411→   411→Movements
   412→   412→It is much safer to push the volume of a unilateral movement like a
   413→   413→split squat than a bilateral movement like a back squat. Pushing the
   414→   414→volume on compound movements carries inherent risks of overuse
   415→   415→injury and creating compensation patterns—with heavy, high-rep
   416→   416→bilateral work, there will almost always be a breakdown in the chain
   417→   417→as fatigue sets in. This can exacerbate your current asymmetries
   418→   418→and set you up for injury instead of making actual improvements.
   419→   419→Increases the Time Under Tension
   420→   420→Unilateral work requires less skill and neural demand, so we can
   421→   421→increase loading and volume commensurately, thereby increasing

   425→   425→phy. Sarcoplasmic hypertrophy is a product of time under tension,
   426→   426→unlike maximal strength development, where time under tension
   427→   427→is much lower.
   428→   428→Improves Bilateral Movement Motor Control and
   429→   429→Performance
   430→   430→To improve bilateral movements like squats, pulls, and Olympic lifts,
   431→   431→you have to have a continuous focus on isolating the areas where you
   432→   432→are weakest. For many, something as simple as adding more direct
   433→   433→posterior chain work is all it takes to produce noticeable gains in
   434→   434→all of the previously mentioned lifts (glute hip thrusts, back raises,
   435→   435→and RDL variations) and is a great vehicle to improve lifts where
   436→   436→the posterior chain predominates, such as squats, deadlifts, and
   437→   437→even the Olympic lifts.
   438→   438→Reduces the Risk of Injury
   439→   439→Assistance exercise work can effectively reduce the risk of injury
   440→   440→as well as rehabilitate current injuries. Advanced individuals must
   441→   441→be constantly assessing where they may be most limited; other-
   442→   442→wise they risk eventually incurring a setback or even detraining
   443→   443→effects. Strategically isolating weak muscle groups with unilateral
   444→   444→exercises can bring someone back from injury as well as prevent
   445→   445→future injuries.
   446→   446→Maximal Effort Method
   447→   447→The ME method is considered the superior method of improving both
   448→   448→intramuscular and intermuscular coordination; the muscles and CNS
   449→   449→adapt only to the load placed upon them, and this method brings forth
   450→   450→the greatest strength gains (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021). With
   451→   451→that said, what is meant by “maximal” can span a number of scenarios,
   452→   452→making the ME method a polarizing topic to say the least. Is training
   453→   453→maximally done with multiple repetitions such as a two-, three-, or even
   454→   454→five-rep max? Or are we talking about simply loading the bar with as much
   455→   455→weight as one can move for one repetition? In this book, we are referring
   456→   456→to maximal tension with the heaviest load for one repetition. The ME
   457→   457→method here is lifting a maximal load for one repetition performed with
   458→   458→bilateral movements such as squat, deadlift, and press variations. These
   459→   459→variations are included in the training programs provided in part III.

   462→   462→The adaptations occur in both intermuscular and intramuscular
   463→   463→coordination—in essence improving both the ability of muscles to fire
   464→   464→in synchronicity with one another and motor patterns. The ME method
   465→   465→brings forth the greatest gains in maximal strength with the most amount
   466→   466→of motor units activated (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021) and has the
   467→   467→highest-intensity strength measure (maximum load/resistance for one
   468→   468→repetition) but lowest-volume bilateral training measure (intensity and
   469→   469→volume should not intersect with this method) (Simmons 2015).
   470→   470→As previously mentioned, there are connections between maximal
   471→   471→strength and other biomotor abilities like strength endurance, but we
   472→   472→must first consider if the ME method can fit into your training plan,
   473→   473→allowing you to arrive at optimal results and a favorable risk-versus-
   474→   474→reward ratio. Exercise variations should be rotated weekly to reduce
   475→   475→risk of injury and to identify variations that are anthropometrically most
   476→   476→suited to the individual. Also, individuals using this method should be
   477→   477→fully comfortable building to one-rep maximum with any foundational
   478→   478→movement pattern such as a box squat or a floor press. Another safety
   479→   479→note is that this method requires the use of a spotter every time.
   480→   480→While this method does come with a stigma of being dangerous and is
   481→   481→not recommended for beginner or even intermediate individuals, it’s only
   482→   482→dangerous if the individual has not learned how to properly perform the
   483→   483→big lifts. Not having learned proper motor patterns can be a detriment
   484→   484→with any method, but it’s certainly not prudent to imagine working with
   485→   485→maximal loads without the proper movement patterns enforced first.
   486→   486→However, there are certainly other methods that will be a better match
   487→   487→for novices.
   488→   488→Submaximal Effort Method
   489→   489→The submaximal effort method differs from the ME method in the
   490→   490→number of repetitions executed, not necessarily the level of effort. While
   491→   491→the ME method results in the highest levels of motor-unit recruitment,
   492→   492→the submaximal effort method still results in high levels of motor-unit
   493→   493→recruitment that correspond with the size principle—recruitment order is
   494→   494→determined by the load placed upon the body; as the relative load increases
   495→   495→across multiple repetitions, large motor units will be recruited (Zatsiorsky,
   496→   496→Kraemer, and Fry 2021). With that said, the adaptations with this method
   497→   497→will be different from those with the ME method; however, a key takeaway
   498→   498→is that this method is optimal for individuals who have less experience.
   499→   499→This is because the emphasis is on building motor patterns with more
   500→   500→exposure to lighter loads. Individuals will not typically be asked to go
   501→   501→to one-rep maximum if they are still learning how to move with a given
   502→   502→pattern. Performing a multiple-rep max with a bilateral movement such

   505→   505→as a squat variation technically constitutes submaximal work—remember
   506→   506→that true ME work is trained with a maximum singular effort.
   507→   507→The adaptations for this method are intermuscular and intramuscular
   508→   508→coordination—in essence improving both the ability of muscles to fire
   509→   509→in synchronicity with one another and motor patterns. Also, time under
   510→   510→tension is higher and results in more hypertrophic adaptations. The
   511→   511→submaximal effort method presents moderate-intensity, moderate-volume
   512→   512→measure—it’s recommended that you do not attempt to ramp up to the
   513→   513→same degree one would expect with a method like the repeated effort
   514→   514→method. Exercise variations should be rotated every two to three weeks
   515→   515→to allow time to ingrain good motor patterns.
   516→   516→In short, the logic behind the submaximal effort method is twofold:
   517→   517→(1) improve motor patterns with bigger bilateral movements—this is
   518→   518→critical for newer individuals before attempting to progress to a method
   519→   519→such as the ME method; and (2) incur higher bouts of volume with bigger
   520→   520→lifts. Remember, you will still incur the largest volume with the repeated
   521→   521→effort method and smaller unilateral exercises, but that’s not to say that
   522→   522→including heavier multiple-rep maxes with bilateral movements does not
   523→   523→have a place in your training plan. You must be mindful of proper volume
   524→   524→prescriptions and how this method synergizes with other methods, which

   526→   526→Dynamic Effort Method
   527→   527→The DE method is used not to increase maximal strength but only to
   528→   528→improve the RFD and explosive strength (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry
   529→   529→2021). Working harder is often associated with making more gains in the
   530→   530→gym, but I’d urge you to consider working smarter. The different methods
   531→   531→introduced here will allow you to create a more well-rounded training
   532→   532→plan that covers all bases in terms of special strength development. The
   533→   533→DE method works with lighter loads and emphasizes bar velocity; only
   534→   534→working with submaximal or maximal loads does not offer the benefits
   535→   535→of dedicating time to work on bar velocity or power such as with the DE
   536→   536→method. Oftentimes people associate power training with the Olympic lifts,
   537→   537→and while there is certainly a power component to the Olympic lifts, for
   538→   538→many the Olympic lifts are not practical as they require years of experience
   539→   539→and coaching from a qualified coach. Instead, you can arrive at the same
   540→   540→improvements in RFD, in this case speed strength, by using the DE method
   541→   541→without having to learn the snatch or clean and jerk. Again, the focus is
   542→   542→on bar velocity against intermediate resistance, which will provide better
   543→   543→results than simply using ME or submaximal effort training alone.
   544→   544→The DE method is integral for improving RFD and explosive strength.
