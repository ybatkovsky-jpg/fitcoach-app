    26→    26→Oxygen Consumption
    27→    27→It is also worth considering the impact of excess postexercise oxygen consumption
    28→    28→(or EPOC). EPOC refers to the prolonged increase in O that may be observed
    29→    29→2
    30→    30→for hours after exercise. A comprehensive study conducted by Borsheim and Bahr
    31→    31→(2003) determined that heavy resistance training produces greater EPOC than
    32→    32→circuit weight training. In other words, EPOC is intensity dependent in response
    33→    33→to resistance training. This study also found that EPOC is intensity dependent in
    34→    34→relation to aerobic training, with the greatest EPOC values occurring when dura-
    35→    35→tion and intensity were high, and that individuals vary in their response. The
    36→    36→greatest EPOC values were recorded when the intensity of aerobic exercise was
    37→    37→greater than 50 to 60 percent of O max and the duration was equal to or greater
    38→    38→2
    39→    39→than 40 minutes. It would stand to reason that maximal effort toward the end of
    40→    40→a metabolic-training session could optimize EPOC. The benefit of EPOC in the
    41→    41→context of metabolic training is its tendency to increase caloric expenditure and
    42→    42→fat burning above resting levels following the completion of a workout.
    43→    43→The following physiological factors, among others, are responsible for EPOC
    44→    44→(Herda and Cramer 2016):
    45→    45→1. Restoring oxygen in the blood and muscles, which enables the body to return
    46→    46→to its pre-exercise state

    52→    52→recovery and reduces muscle soreness and fatigue

    55→    55→Short-Term Adaptations to Metabolic Training
    56→    56→Short-term adaptations are those that occur within one to four weeks and are spe-
    57→    57→cifically related to the composition of the training program. For example, heavy
    58→    58→strength training will yield greater increases in strength than low-intensity, high-
    59→    59→rep training programs. Short-term adaptations are relatively transient compared
    60→    60→to long-term adaptions, meaning that if you stop training after one to four weeks,
    61→    61→there is a relatively quick return to pre-training status. In practical terms, this
    62→    62→means that you should remain consistent in your training if you want to continue
    63→    63→to develop strength. It should be noted that nervous-system adaptations occur faster
    64→    64→than structural changes in skeletal muscle. This has led researchers and practi-
    65→    65→tioners to conclude that initial strength improvement is the result of neurological
    66→    66→changes. Even if you can’t see changes in your muscle size or composition early
    67→    67→on in your program, know that your body is adapting and building a foundation
    68→    68→for strength. Stick to your program, and the changes will be noticeable over time.

    75→    75→are employed. These improvements to your cardiorespiratory system will allow

   370→   370→Medicine Ball Training
   371→   371→Medicine balls can be traced back to ancient Persia, Greece, and Rome, where
   372→   372→they were used by athletes to increase strength, power, and athletic performance.
   373→   373→It is believed that the ancient Greeks stuffed animal skins for patients to toss for
   374→   374→medicinal purposes. A similar type of ball is believed to have been used in Persia
   375→   375→in the 1700s. The term medicine ball can be dated back to 1876 (Roberts 2020). Hip-
   376→   376→pocrates believed them to be an essential tool for helping people regain normal body
   377→   377→movement after injury, as well as a means for enhancing and maintaining overall
   378→   378→health. Few, if any, pieces of equipment are versatile in the way that medicine
   379→   379→balls are: not only can they be lifted, but they can also be thrown and slammed.
   380→   380→The adaptability of medicine balls enables users to utilize nontraditional methods
   381→   381→to expand training goals and objectives. In addition, nearly every medicine ball
   382→   382→exercise can be combined with other exercises to create a continuous flow with
   383→   383→minimal rest. This is beneficial for two reasons:
   384→   384→1. It mimics functional movements used in everyday life.
   385→   385→2. It enables users to program any number of exercise combinations and varia-
   386→   386→tions.
   387→   387→Today, medicine balls are used to develop power, strength, anaerobic endurance,
   388→   388→and cardiorespiratory fitness.
   389→   389→Medicine balls can be soft or firm, can come with or without handles, and can
   390→   390→vary in weight from 2 to 100 pounds (0.9 to 45.4 kilograms). Medicine ball training
   391→   391→has a wide spectrum of applications, ranging from injury rehabilitation to strength
   392→   392→and sports-performance enhancement (Faigenbaum et al. 2006; Hagberg et al.
   393→   393→2009). Its ability to recruit a significant number of muscle groups and to develop
   394→   394→explosive power through functional movement makes medicine ball training an
   395→   395→essential tool in fitness, rehabilitation, and sports performance (Vincent, Traywick,
   396→   396→and Washburn 2013). When training with a medicine ball, perform 10 to 15 reps
   397→   397→or perform reps for a designated time, provided that you can do so with good form
   398→   398→(Faigenbaum et al. 2006). When selecting a medicine ball, it is important to choose
   399→   399→one heavy enough to reduce movement speed but not so heavy that technique,
   400→   400→form, and range of motion will be negatively impacted. Medicine balls weighing
   401→   401→between 4 and 15 pounds (1.8 to 6.8 kilograms) are generally recommended as a
   402→   402→starting point (Faigenbaum et al. 2006; Vincent, Traywick, and Washburn 2013).
   403→   403→Medicine ball training can be effective as a fat-burning exercise when periods of
   404→   404→high-intensity, high-volume training are implemented to maximize caloric expen-

   433→   433→When developing metabolic-training programs, heavy ropes, suspension trainers,
   434→   434→weighted sleds, and sandbags can be valuable additions to traditional training tools.

   439→   439→along with your level of fitness. As with all exercise modalities, proper technique

   441→   441→look at each of these pieces of equipment and consider how they can be used to vary

   443→   443→Heavy ropes, also known as battle ropes, are thick ropes of varying lengths and
   444→   444→weights that can be linked together, anchored, or wrapped around a pole or training

   451→   451→lower-body lunging, jumping, and squatting while performing rope exercises.
   452→   452→• Participate in a lower-impact cardiorespiratory activity that greatly reduces the
   453→   453→impact to joints associated with some traditional cardiorespiratory activities.

   541→   541→Periodization for
   542→   542→Metabolic Training
   543→   543→Periodization is the variation of training over a period of time in order to achieve
   544→   544→an optimal outcome. Or, in more detailed terms, “Periodization is a theoretical
   545→   545→and practical construct that allows for the systematic, sequential, and integrative
   546→   546→programing of training interventions into mutually dependent periods of time
   547→   547→in order to induce specific physiological adaptations that underpin performance
   548→   548→outcomes” (Haff 2016). Another way to think of it is that periodization is the con-
   549→   549→cept, and programming (sets, reps, exercises, weight used, days per week, etc.)
   550→   550→is the application.
   551→   551→Components of Periodization
   552→   552→There are three concepts central to understanding periodization: general adap-
   553→   553→tation syndrome, stimulus–fatigue–recovery–adaptation (SFRA) theory, and the
   554→   554→fitness–fatigue paradigm. In the following, we explain what they are and how
   555→   555→they fit into metabolic training.
   556→   556→General Adaptation Syndrome
   557→   557→This theory is the most fundamental to physical training. When you perform a
   558→   558→training session that takes your body beyond its current state, you’re applying
   559→   559→a stress to your systems. The body initially responds to the stress in an alarm
   560→   560→stage where fatigue is experienced possibly in the form of soreness or stiffness
   561→   561→in, generally, the first 24 to 48 hours following your training session. After this
   562→   562→alarm stage the body transitions into the resistance stage and the body further
   563→   563→adapts to the stress. In the final stage, the body experiences what is known as
   564→   564→supercompensation. Upon reaching the supercompensation stage, the body is at a
   565→   565→higher level of adaptation which means you are becoming stronger or fitter than
   566→   566→you were before the training.
   567→   567→Stimulus–Fatigue–Recovery–Adaptation Theory
   568→   568→The SFRA theory suggests that training stimuli produce a general response (Haff
   569→   569→2016). The greater the magnitude of the stimulus or workload, the greater the
   570→   570→25
   571→   571→--- PAGE 35 ---
   572→   572→26 | Metabolic Training
   573→   573→fatigue response. In other words, there is a direct positive correlation between
   574→   574→training workload and fatigue. As you adapt to the training workload, however,
   575→   575→your fatigue will lessen. When a new training stimulus is introduced, the adap-
   576→   576→tation cycle continues. The SFRA theory emphasizes timing or scheduling of
   577→   577→interventions and is essential to optimizing training effects. Keep in mind that
   578→   578→if you stop training after a single intervention, you will return to your untrained
   579→   579→state. Consistency is key to maximizing your results.
   580→   580→Complete recovery is often not reached after training interventions. However,
   581→   581→partial recovery does often occur, and training programs should have the fre-
   582→   582→quency, intensity, and volume of training adjusted in such a manner to optimize
   583→   583→results. The general trend is to gradually decrease volume of training while
   584→   584→increasing intensity. This concept is central to periodization.
   585→   585→Fitness–Fatigue Paradigm
   586→   586→The fitness–fatigue paradigm posits a relationship between fitness and fatigue
   587→   587→known as the “state of readiness” (Haff 2016; see figure 4.1). This model was first
   588→   588→proposed by the sport scientist Vladimir Zatsiorsky (Zatsiorsky & Kraemer 2006).
   589→   589→There is a direct positive relationship between volume load of training and fatigue,
   590→   590→where volume load refers to amount of weight times the number of repetitions
   591→   591→assigned to an exercise. As an example, 10 reps at 100 pounds (45.4 kilograms)
   592→   592→performed for three sets results in a volume load of 3,000 pounds (1,360.7 kilo-
   593→   593→grams) for that exercise. The volume load of an exercise for a specific period of
   594→   594→time (a day, a week, a month, etc.) can be quickly calculated by multiplying reps
   595→   595→by weight by sets. The importance of accumulated fatigue as a result of volume
   596→   596→load cannot be overstated.
