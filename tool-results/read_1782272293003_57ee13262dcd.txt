  2161→The brain and spinal cord make up CNS (see figure 6.1). The somatic nervous
  2162→system transmits sensory and motor signals to and from the CNS. The efficient
  2163→routing of these motor signals must be in place to achieve optimal performance
  2164→and efficient technique. Famed performance coach Charlie Francis (1982)
  2165→defines the CNS as “the optimal transmitter of nervous signals and motor
  2166→pathways.”
  2167→Francis describes CNS fatigue as the “by-products of high-intensity exercise[s
  2168→that] build up to a point where the CNS impulses (necessary to contract the
  2169→muscle fibers) are handicapped.” Most studies involving CNS fatigue have been
  2170→done using endurance exercise rather than examining CNS function when

  2173→the following primarily causes this drop in function in explosive athletes:
  2174→High-intensity work occurring too frequently in a training cycle
  2175→Too much high-intensity volume in a single training session
  2176→Introducing high-intensity training too rapidly into a training program when
  2177→residual fatigue still exists

  2190→Traditionally, resistance-training intensity has been derived from a percentage
  2191→of an athlete’s 1RM. More recently, however, a study has shown that practically
  2192→perfect correlations (r = 0.95) were observed in the relationship between load–
  2193→velocity and a predicted 1RM (Jidovtseff et al. 2011). This makes VBT an
  2194→extremely reliable and more convenient alternative than the traditional
  2195→percentage-based 1RM. It enables both the coach and the athlete to match
  2196→established resisting training variables, such as the percentage of an athlete’s
  2197→1RM, to specific movement velocities. Doing so helps them objectively identify

  2713→Yearly Periodization Using VBT
  2714→The ultimate success of any training program centers on the ability to produce
  2715→specific physiological adaptations that will translate into increases in
  2716→performance (Poliquin 1988). This is achieved by using a concept called
  2717→periodization for yearly programming. Without being familiar with a few key
  2718→concepts of periodization, trying to apply bar and body speeds with VBT or any
  2719→method will be premature at best. In previous chapters, we discussed in detail
  2720→what VBT actually is, what its origins are, and how to read the data as well as
  2721→monitor fatigue. We now turn to two of the most frequently asked questions
  2722→regarding programming and the use of VBT.
  2723→1. How do I program and use VBT over a complete training year for an
  2724→athlete or a team?
  2725→2. How do I break up the year’s training into different phases?
  2726→To answer these questions, we must first have a discussion about
  2727→periodization. We cannot effectively program for an athlete until we have
  2728→reached a basic understanding of periodization. This chapter briefly explains
  2729→training cycles and periods, breaking them down into various phases as well as
  2730→the hierarchy (i.e., continuum) of these phases to reveal how and where the
  2731→special strength zones (from chapter 4) fit into them.
  2732→
  2733→PERIODIZATION: TRAINING CYCLES, PERIODS,
  2734→AND PHASES
  2735→Periodization is an organized approach to training with the intent of maximizing
  2736→recovery in order to elicit a performance effect. Periodization organizes a yearly
  2737→or seasonal plan by partitioning it into smaller training periods and phases
  2738→throughout the course of the athlete’s year or season in order to maximize
  2739→competitive performance.
  2740→In periodization, the structure (periods and phases) and timing (cycle) of these
  2741→periods and phases highly depend on the following:
  2742→The sport and type of athlete you are training
  2743→The team’s or athlete’s specific needs
  2744→The period or phase the team or athlete is currently in
  2745→
  2746→By partitioning an athlete’s yearly plan into smaller training periods, we can maximize recovery and
  2747→maximize in-season performance.
