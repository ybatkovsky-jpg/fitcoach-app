    33→Basic Principles of Metabolic Training
    34→The following principles of exercise are particularly applicable to metabolic train-
    35→ing and should always be considered when designing and implementing metabolic-
    36→training programs. Adhering to these principles will optimize training results.
    37→Measures such as heart rate and rating of perceived exertion (RPE) can be used
    38→to objectively evaluate how the body responds to the exercise stimuli associated
    39→with these training approaches.
    40→1. Overload. The principle of overload refers to the application of a greater
    41→amount of stress than an individual has previously experienced. Increased
    42→intensity, duration, frequency volume, as well as new types of exercise, can
    43→all overload an athlete. For instance, adding a fourth day of running per
    44→week from three days is an overload of training.

    47→2.Specificity. According to the principle of specificity, a specific method of train-
    48→ing produces a specific outcome. As an example, someone who consistently
    49→runs long distances will adapt physiologically to long-distance running.
    50→3.FITT. FITT stands for “frequency, intensity, time, and type. ”Frequency refers
    51→to how often you exercise. Intensity is the effort exerted to perform a specific
    52→exercise. Time is the duration of an exercise session. Type indicates the kind
    53→of exercise (cardiorespiratory, strength, or a combination of the two, as well
    54→as the exercise modality).
    55→4.Rest/recovery. Rest or recovery describes breaks taken during an exercise
    56→session—between exercises or between exercise sets—as well as between
    57→one exercise session and the next.
    58→Training intensity can be evaluated using heart-rate monitoring. The higher
    59→the heart rate, the more intense the training, and vice versa. You should know
    60→your resting heart rate and your average heart rate during light to heavy (or very
    61→heavy) exercise. Maximal heart rates can be reached during metabolic training.
    62→It is important to understand that each individual has his own maximal heart
    63→rate (and associated training intensities), and a higher heart rate during the train-
    64→ing session does not mean a greater effort or more intense training. World-class
    65→athletes can have low or high maximal heart rates; neither is a predictor of car-
    66→diorespiratory fitness.
    67→Rating of perceived exertion (RPE) is a subjective measure of how difficult an
    68→exercise is. The RPE scale is typically 1-10, with 1 being the lowest level of exer-
    69→tion and 10 being the highest. The RPE scale is a simple and effective tool that
    70→can be used to determine exercise intensity or the physical demands associated
    71→with a particular exercise.
    72→Workout Structure
    73→The following general guidelines should be followed when designing a workout or
    74→series of workouts over days, weeks, and months. Doing so will ensure a higher-
    75→quality workout and will optimize training.
    76→• Fatigue management. Properly managing fatigue during a workout involves
    77→close monitoring of exercise selection, rest intervals, exercise order, exercise
    78→grouping, training tempo, and workout length. As fatigue accumulates during the
    79→workout, biomechanics and exercise technique can be compromised, which can
    80→present safety issues.
    81→• Speed before strength exercises. Exercises that require higher velocity of move-
    82→ment, like plyometrics or jump squats, should be performed before slower move-
    83→ments. High-velocity movements can tax the nervous system to an extent that
    84→compromises subsequent exercises. In these scenarios, it is advisable to alternate
    85→ground-based speed exercises with upper-body exercises. Split-squat jumps, for
    86→example, could be followed by push-ups.

    89→• Technical before gross motor exercises. Metabolic training involves exercises
    90→ranging from low to high demand. Biceps dumbbell curls or jumping jacks, for
    91→example, are exercises that have low technical demands. On the other end of the
    92→spectrum, a power snatch from the floor or a burpee would present high techni-
    93→cal demands. Exercises with higher technical demands, like power movements,
    94→should be performed when fatigue is low. This can mean performing them earlier
    95→in the training session or after exercises that don’t bring about excessive fatigue.
    96→In the context of metabolic training, gross motor exercises should be performed
    97→after technical exercises. For instance, a hang power clean should be performed
    98→after back extensions.
    99→• Multijoint before single-joint exercises. Generally speaking, multijoint exercises
   100→should be performed before single-joint exercises. Multijoint exercises require
   101→more muscle mass and a higher degree of technique, and are more fatiguing, than
   102→single-joint exercises. A sequence of exercises might include a landmine squat to
   103→press followed by a supine triceps extension.
   104→• Exercise grouping. Exercises can be paired or combined to achieve various
   105→objectives, including, but not limited to, managing fatigue, promoting muscle
   106→growth, improving athletic performance, developing power, developing strength,
   107→and calibrating metabolic demand. Participant safety, available equipment, train-
   108→ing goals, and individual limitations are also important considerations when
   109→grouping exercises.
   110→• Exercise demand. Keep in mind that different exercises will have different
   111→levels of metabolic demand. Generally speaking, multijoint ground-based move-
   112→ments will have higher levels of metabolic demand than single-joint, isolated,
   113→machine-based exercises. Exercises that utilize more muscle mass, like squats
   114→and lunges, will have a greater metabolic demand than biceps curls or dumbbell
   115→side raises. For a training program to be successful, demand needs to be carefully
   116→calibrated.
   117→• Unstable and stable surfaces. The efficacy of unstable surfaces in developing
   118→athletic performance is debatable, and the primary goal of metabolic training
   119→is to improve metabolic efficiency and capacity. With that said, medicine balls,
   120→stability balls, balance pads, and balance boards are popular fitness tools. Should
   121→you choose to incorporate unstable surfaces into metabolic training, be aware of
   122→your surroundings and any safety issues they might present. Careful consider-
   123→ation should also be given to exercise selection. For instance, pre-fatiguing the
   124→lower body before performing one-leg squats on a balance pad will increase the
   125→difficulty of the exercise.
   126→• Rest intervals. Although metabolic training can be demanding, there are times
   127→when perceived effort is lower than expected. This is because neuromuscular
   128→fatigue is distributed through different areas of the body, not confined to one
   129→area or muscle group. One way to manage workout intensity or perceived effort
   130→is by changing the rest interval. Longer rest intervals may be warranted after

   166→Physiological Adaptations
   167→to Metabolic Training
   168→Physiological adaptation refers to how the body responds to, accommodates, or
   169→adjusts to exercise. To give an over-simplified example, if you lift heavy weights,
   170→your muscles get bigger. But there are actually a number of adaptations taking
   171→place in response to such a stimulus. These adaptations include a more coordi-
   172→nated neural signal to the activated muscles, uptake of nutrients, and the release
   173→of muscle-building hormones.
   174→Learning about the process of physiological adaptation will help you design
   175→more effective workouts, understand how your body will respond to training,
   176→and know what to expect with consistent and focused training. Two of the most
   177→common fitness goals are burning fat and enhancing muscular development. This
   178→chapter takes a deeper dive into some of the physiological adaptations to training
   179→that can turn these goals into realities.
   180→Adapting to Demands
   181→As discussed in chapter 1, in reference to the specificity principle, adaptations are
   182→specific to imposed demands. Adaptions can be morphological (related to muscle
   183→size), biomechanical (related to strength and power), anatomical (related to muscle,
   184→bone, or fat volumes), neurological (related to motor control), or metabolic (related
   185→to aerobic or anaerobic energy pathways) in nature. Workout parameters such
   186→as intensity level and rest duration can be set to elicit specific adaptations. For
   187→instance, short, high-intensity exercise intervals (up to six seconds in duration,
   188→focusing on quick movement with maximal intensity) paired with longer rest
   189→intervals (1:1 to 1:4 work:rest ratio) can target the short-duration phosphagen meta-
   190→bolic pathway. Conversely, lower-intensity activity paired with shorter-duration
   191→rest intervals can target the aerobic metabolic pathway. Also, keep in mind that
   192→exercises targeting multiple joints and muscle groups at one time are more meta-
   193→bolically challenging than exercises targeting individual joints and muscle groups.
   194→It should be pointed out that metabolic training utilizes both aerobic and anaero-
   195→bic metabolic pathways, while also placing demand on the neuromuscular system
   196→11
   197→--- PAGE 21 ---

   199→by way of strength and power exercises. In other situations, metabolic training
   200→can elicit more of an aerobic metabolic (endurance) response—for example, when
   201→lighter weights are used. This de-emphasizes the neuromuscular system. These
   202→two examples represent the various ways in which metabolic training can be pro-
   203→grammed. Neither approach is superior to the other; the optimal program is best
   204→determined by the goals of the program and the individual. With this concept in
   205→mind, both aerobic and anaerobic adaptations can be observed.
   206→Some metabolic-training programs implement strength-power and aerobic
   207→(endurance) methodologies together. This approach is called concurrent training.
   208→Research on the effects of concurrent training has yielded the following conclu-
   209→sions:
   210→• Concurrent training does not appear to compromise gains in aerobic capacity.
   211→• The incorporation of resistance training may have a beneficial effect on
   212→short-term endurance.
   213→• The incorporation of aerobic training may compromise maximal strength in
   214→experienced, resistance-trained individuals.
   215→Consider the intensity of training: Short bursts of high-intensity training target
   216→the anaerobic pathway. Longer-duration efforts, lasting more than three minutes,
   217→start to emphasize the aerobic pathway. Metabolic activity is elevated by anaerobic
   218→efforts of shorter duration if these efforts are performed at high intensity, which
   219→includes heavy resistance training exercise (Herda & Cramer 2016). This idea
   220→constitutes a fundamental element and benefit of metabolic training.

   251→Short-Term Adaptations to Metabolic Training
   252→Short-term adaptations are those that occur within one to four weeks and are spe-
   253→cifically related to the composition of the training program. For example, heavy
   254→strength training will yield greater increases in strength than low-intensity, high-
   255→rep training programs. Short-term adaptations are relatively transient compared
   256→to long-term adaptions, meaning that if you stop training after one to four weeks,
   257→there is a relatively quick return to pre-training status. In practical terms, this
   258→means that you should remain consistent in your training if you want to continue
   259→to develop strength. It should be noted that nervous-system adaptations occur faster
   260→than structural changes in skeletal muscle. This has led researchers and practi-
   261→tioners to conclude that initial strength improvement is the result of neurological
   262→changes. Even if you can’t see changes in your muscle size or composition early
   263→on in your program, know that your body is adapting and building a foundation
   264→for strength. Stick to your program, and the changes will be noticeable over time.
   265→There are some adaptations that you may notice soon after beginning a met-
   266→abolic-training program. Immediate cardiorespiratory responses to metabolic
   267→training include increases in heart rate, oxygen uptake, systolic blood pressure,
   268→blood flow to active muscles, the heart’s stroke volume, and cardiac output (French
   269→2016). Ventilation rate increases significantly during resistance training (Statler
   270→and Dubois 2016), and especially metabolic training, when short rest intervals
   271→are employed. These improvements to your cardiorespiratory system will allow
   272→you to breathe easier and work harder as you take on more-challenging workouts.

   344→Bodyweight Training
   345→When it comes to enhancing functional strength, your own body can provide you
   346→with not only the most accessible and cost-effective training tool but in many
   347→ways the most beneficial. Bodyweight-training exercises do not require a gym or
   348→specialized equipment to deliver an effective workout—they can be performed
   349→virtually anywhere. This training style provides you with a functionally based
   350→approach to increasing power, strength, endurance, and flexibility in the pursuit
   351→of muscle development and fat metabolism. Most importantly, bodyweight train-
   352→ing can be done by anyone, regardless of fitness level.
   353→The simplicity of bodyweight training allows you to quickly transfer from
   354→one exercise to another without having to adjust equipment or resistance. This
   355→significantly reduces rest time while keeping your heart rate in a target training
   356→zone and increasing caloric expenditure per unit of time. Bodyweight training is
   357→an excellent way to burn body fat and increase metabolic rate, not only during
   358→exercise but after exercise.
   359→Bodyweight training allows for natural progressions in intensity. Progressions
   360→commonly involve simply adding repetitions or time to an exercise for increased
   361→fitness or changing the movement pattern to increase the demand on strength.
   362→Focusing on volume eliminates the risk of utilizing resistances that may potentially
   363→result in injury while simultaneously improving flexibility by involving a full
   364→range of movement. The engagement of the core musculature required to perform
   365→bodyweight exercises naturally develops core strength and stability, which, in
   366→turn, appears to reduce injury risk (Haff, Berninger, and Caulfield 2016).
   367→In summary, the benefits of bodyweight training can be tied to the use of
   368→functional exercises that are adaptable to all levels of fitness. The use of these
   369→exercises in a workout provides a low-cost training method that increases muscle
   370→strength in several muscle groups at once, develops relative strength, and improves
   371→balance and coordination (Harrison 2011).

   457→targeting weaknesses and imbalances and correcting them through short, high-
   458→intensity workouts that increase hormonal production for muscle development

   460→Kettlebells have been studied more recently by strength and conditioning
   461→researchers interested in these tools’ ability to enhance strength, power, and car-
   462→diorespiratory fitness in individuals ranging from novice exercisers to elite-level
   463→athletes and fitness enthusiasts (Beardsley and Contreras 2014). The benefits of
   464→kettlebell training are numerous. Kettlebell training has been shown to increase
   465→overall strength and power production (Jay et al. 2011); improve core strength by
   466→emphasizing power, acceleration, and multiplanar and unilateral movement pat-
   467→terns (Lake and Lauder 2012); encourage the production of force and speed through
   468→the hips (Otto et al. 2012); and promote the development of lean body mass through
   469→high-repetition sets combined with short rest periods. Often-neglected muscles of
   470→the back of the body (core, shoulders, hips) are targeted, which improves posture

   473→and posterior-chain function (Jay et al. 2011). Kettlebell training has also been
   474→shown to improve grip strength, which enables other strength gains (Manocchia
   475→et al. 2013).
   476→Because of its emphasis on higher volume and shorter rest periods, kettlebell
   477→training is associated with increased caloric expenditure per unit of time (20
   478→calories per minute), resulting in increased metabolism during and after training,
   479→increased fat metabolism, increased muscular endurance, and better cardiorespi-
   480→ratory function (Jay et al. 2011). Kettlebells provide the user with one piece of
   481→exercise equipment to train the entire body (Jay et al. 2011). If you are limited with
   482→space at home or want to minimize your movement around the gym, kettlebells
   483→are the perfect tools to simplify your workout. Kettlebell training uses dynamic
   484→movement patterns that increase mobility, proprioception, flexibility, and body
   485→awareness by forcing the user to move through all three planes of motion in a
   486→controlled, fluid manner. With kettlebells, stabilizing muscle groups are trained
   487→in combination with primary muscles, leading to improved joint stability, coor-
   488→dination, and balance (Manocchia et al. 2013). Finally, kettlebells offer a fun and
   489→exciting way to train because the exercises are based on movement patterns and
   490→require you to be more engaged (Jay et al. 2011).
   491→When performing kettlebell exercises, use the grip position (neutral grip, with
   492→palms facing in; supinated grip, with palms facing up; or pronated grip, with palms
   493→facing down) and hold position (at your side, between your palms, in your palm,
   494→or upside down) that best suits each exercise (Cotter 2022). When performing an
   495→exercise in a standing position, place your feet slightly wider than shoulder-width
   496→apart. With training resistances between 80 and 100 percent of your repetition
   497→maximum (the maximum resistance you can utilize with a given volume and
   498→intensity), avoid holding your breath—you should continue to inhale and exhale

   500→Resistance-Band Training
   501→Resistance bands have become a staple of just about every fitness environment.
   502→Resistance bands come in three basic forms: tube, loop, and mini. Each of the
   503→three types provides unique benefits. Resistance bands come in a variety of
   504→colors often with handles. The color of a resistance band designates its tension
   505→level. What makes resistance-band training unique is that tension increases as
   506→the bands are stretched, meaning that resistance increases as more muscle groups
   507→are incorporated in a movement pattern (Haff, Berninger, and Caulfield 2016).
   508→Resistance bands can be used at any fitness level and any age. Youth, adults,
   509→seniors, competitive athletes, tactical professionals, and individuals with chronic
   510→conditions or disorders can all benefit from resistance-band training. Addition-
   511→ally, resistance bands are among the most inexpensive pieces of equipment and
   512→can easily be packed in a suitcase and utilized anywhere.
   513→Resistance bands are beneficial for metabolic training because they can be
   514→used safely and effectively at higher volumes with shorter rest periods. Training
   515→with resistance bands is an excellent way to reduce body fat and simultaneously

   520→Resistance bands are frequently used while warming up and cooling down.

   522→mobility through dynamic range-of-motion movements at reduced resistances.

   566→Medicine Ball Training
   567→Medicine balls can be traced back to ancient Persia, Greece, and Rome, where
   568→they were used by athletes to increase strength, power, and athletic performance.
   569→It is believed that the ancient Greeks stuffed animal skins for patients to toss for
   570→medicinal purposes. A similar type of ball is believed to have been used in Persia
   571→in the 1700s. The term medicine ball can be dated back to 1876 (Roberts 2020). Hip-
   572→pocrates believed them to be an essential tool for helping people regain normal body
   573→movement after injury, as well as a means for enhancing and maintaining overall
   574→health. Few, if any, pieces of equipment are versatile in the way that medicine
   575→balls are: not only can they be lifted, but they can also be thrown and slammed.
   576→The adaptability of medicine balls enables users to utilize nontraditional methods
   577→to expand training goals and objectives. In addition, nearly every medicine ball
   578→exercise can be combined with other exercises to create a continuous flow with
   579→minimal rest. This is beneficial for two reasons:
   580→1. It mimics functional movements used in everyday life.
   581→2. It enables users to program any number of exercise combinations and varia-
   582→tions.
   583→Today, medicine balls are used to develop power, strength, anaerobic endurance,
   584→and cardiorespiratory fitness.
   585→Medicine balls can be soft or firm, can come with or without handles, and can
   586→vary in weight from 2 to 100 pounds (0.9 to 45.4 kilograms). Medicine ball training
   587→has a wide spectrum of applications, ranging from injury rehabilitation to strength
   588→and sports-performance enhancement (Faigenbaum et al. 2006; Hagberg et al.
   589→2009). Its ability to recruit a significant number of muscle groups and to develop
   590→explosive power through functional movement makes medicine ball training an
   591→essential tool in fitness, rehabilitation, and sports performance (Vincent, Traywick,
   592→and Washburn 2013). When training with a medicine ball, perform 10 to 15 reps
   593→or perform reps for a designated time, provided that you can do so with good form
   594→(Faigenbaum et al. 2006). When selecting a medicine ball, it is important to choose
   595→one heavy enough to reduce movement speed but not so heavy that technique,
   596→form, and range of motion will be negatively impacted. Medicine balls weighing
   597→between 4 and 15 pounds (1.8 to 6.8 kilograms) are generally recommended as a
   598→starting point (Faigenbaum et al. 2006; Vincent, Traywick, and Washburn 2013).
   599→Medicine ball training can be effective as a fat-burning exercise when periods of
   600→high-intensity, high-volume training are implemented to maximize caloric expen-
   601→diture. This results in an increased postexercise metabolic effect as well. Training
   602→with medicine balls allows exercisers to utilize their full range of movement, which
   603→is important for developing total-body strength. Medicine balls also lend themselves
   604→to developing strength in the context of sports that require rotational movement.
