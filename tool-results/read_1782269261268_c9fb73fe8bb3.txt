   328→Programs
   329→Hypertrophy work, accessory work, special exercise work, single-joint
   330→work, unilateral work—whatever you’d like to call it, it’s a staple in any
   331→great training program. For some individuals, performing hypertrophy
   332→work alone as the crux of their training plan is prudent. Hypertrophy
   333→work is beneficial from a number of perspectives and can span all abilities,
   334→so whether you’re a seasoned lifter or a newbie, hypertrophy work will
   335→be an integral part of your training plan. Moreover, hypertrophy work
   336→can act as a primer for strength development while encouraging better
   337→motor-unit synchronization, symmetry, and resilience. Furthermore,
   338→hypertrophy work is a great way to add lean tissue in the beginning stages
   339→of one’s training. But I’d be remiss if I didn’t lay out why a hypertrophy-
   340→based training plan is effective for a wide array of individuals looking to
   341→improve numerous aspects of their training.
   342→BENEFITS OF HYPERTROPHY WORK
   343→Because hypertrophy-style training programs are an important form of
   344→training, particularly for individuals with low training ages, let’s discuss
   345→some of the benefits this work can have, as well as its carryover to other
   346→aspects of fitness.
   347→• Potential to increase calorie expenditure. Even though the
   348→increase in metabolic rate is not as large as we once thought it was,
   349→hypertrophy work can be a factor in caloric expenditure, fat loss,
   350→and blood sugar regulation when you add a significant amount of
   351→lean mass.
   352→• High amounts of time under tension. Just as a bodybuilder spends
   353→a significant amount of time isolating specific muscle groups, we
   354→193
   355→--- PAGE 210 ---
   356→194 Lower Body Training
   357→can employ a high volume of hypertrophy work. In an effort to
   358→target where we may be lacking, we can add a high volume of work
   359→without the risk of exacerbating faulty motor patterns. This work
   360→allows athletes of all levels to perform work without being limited
   361→by their lack of skill; because it’s low skill, just about everyone can
   362→derive benefits.
   363→• Reduced risk versus reward. It is safer to add volume to single-
   364→joint movements than to multijoint movements, just as it’s safer to
   365→increase the volume of a unilateral movement like a split squat than
   366→the volume of a bilateral movement like a back squat. Additionally,
   367→increasing the volume on compound movements carries a higher
   368→risk of overuse injury, which could hinder success even though you
   369→worked hard in a training session.
   370→• Reduced neural demand. Building on our last benefit, unilateral
   371→work requires less skill and neural demand, so we can increase
   372→loading and volume commensurately, thereby increasing the time
   373→under tension. This leads to an increase in muscular hypertrophy.
   374→Most who believe assistance exercises are unnecessary are simply
   375→uninformed about their influence on body composition. With lower
   376→neural demand comes less potential of central fatigue and thus lower
   377→risk of overtraining.
   378→• Increased potential to generate force. Just about any exercise
   379→physiology book or study will confirm this. While you may not see
   380→performing direct biceps work carry over to your pull-ups right
   381→away, you’ll see substantial improvements in as little as eight weeks
   382→(Haff 2016).
   383→• Improved performance of multijoint movements. If you want
   384→to make improvements when performing movements like squats,
   385→deadlifts, and Olympic lifts, it makes sense to prioritize training
   386→where you are weakest. Single-joint movements and isolation
   387→exercises allow you to add more direct posterior chain work to
   388→address weaknesses in all of these lifts.
   389→• Reduced risk of injury. You can effectively reduce the risk of injury
   390→as well as rehabilitate current injuries with hypertrophy work. It’s
   391→critical to constantly assess where you are most limited and take
   392→inventory of nagging aches and pains. For example, lower back
   393→disorder injuries are rising at an alarming rate, making direct gluteal
   394→and hip work a great place to focus hypertrophy efforts.
   395→Hypertrophy work has the power to help individuals reach their
   396→full potential, and performing this work alone can help one reset and
   397→reprogram faulty motor patterns as well as add lean muscle mass.
   398→Moreover, for individuals who are still learning how to move and perform
   399→--- PAGE 211 ---

   401→patterns like the squat and the hip hinge, exercises with less overall
   402→complexity and risk of technical error (think of a split squat versus a back
   403→squat) are a great place to start to improve motor control and facilitate
   404→long-term training sustainability.

   676→CREATING STRENGTH TRAINING
   677→ADAPTATIONS
   678→Developing strength involves more than just lifting heavy loads. The
   679→reality is that force comes in many shapes and sizes and while we know
   680→by definition strength is the ability of a muscle or muscle group to
   681→generate force, having a well-rounded approach to achieving strength
   682→gains is a smarter alternative than just lifting heavy. In this chapter,
   683→we’ll discuss two methods of strength development that will be critical to
   684→your success in the gym. We’ll discuss why you should consider utilizing
   685→these methods along with their benefits, their reasoning, and how they
   686→play a part in keeping your programming balanced (in order to avoid
   687→things like overtraining). For more information about other training
   688→methods, see chapter 3.
   689→Dynamic Effort Training
   690→It’s a common misconception that you need to lift heavy in every workout.
   691→Many think that the effectiveness of training is judged by how hard it
   692→is. But the human body isn’t a gumball machine—it doesn’t spit out
   693→candy every time you put a quarter in. That’s because there is a point of
   694→diminishing returns. If you’re not allowing for proper recovery between
   695→higher-threshold, or maximal lifting, sessions (sessions that are more
   696→demanding on the central nervous system), you’ll eventually overtrain
   697→and start losing your progress or get injured. The reality is that hard
   698→training sessions need to be interspersed with easier training sessions.
   699→It’s also important to incorporate strength methods that differ with
   700→regard to bar velocity.
   701→What does this mean to the average individual who wants to get
   702→stronger? It means that there is low-hanging fruit to be picked with
   703→methods such as dynamic effort (DE) training, which aims to improve
   704→strength through nonmaximal measures that activate high-threshold
   705→motor units to a high degree. The goal of this method is to use nonmaximal
   706→loads with the highest attainable velocity (bar speed) to improve the rate
   707→of force development (RFD) and increase the number of recruited and
   708→trained motor units (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021).
   709→These are the key features of DE training:
   710→• Uses high-threshold motor units and facilitates RFD
   711→• Places high demands on the nervous system
   712→• Works best with accommodating resistance (AR) to ensure proper
   713→loading throughout full range of motion (ROM). AR is the use of
   714→bands or chains to provide maximal tension through full ROM.
   715→--- PAGE 223 ---
   716→Strength Programs 207
   717→• Must be separated from maximal effort training by at least 72 hours
   718→• Works the velocity portion of the force-velocity curve
   719→In short, DE training is used in this book both for strength development
   720→and athletic performance. This method maximizes the velocity component
   721→of the force-velocity curve while creating balanced programming to
   722→reduce the risk of overtraining.
   723→Submaximal Effort Training
   724→The repeated effort method, covered in chapter 3, is an integral aspect
   725→of every program in this book. While there are some similarities between
   726→it and the submaximal effort method, the major difference is that
   727→submaximal work is delivered exclusively through bilateral movements
   728→(i.e., squats, presses, pulls). The submaximal effort method will be
   729→used as a multiple-repetition maximum scenario (two- to six-rep max)
   730→because true maximal effort training consists of a singular one-rep
   731→maximal load set.
   732→The goal with the submaximal effort method is twofold: (1) to develop
   733→proper motor patterns with bilateral movements approaching heavier
   734→loads and (2) to improve strength qualities via recruitment of high-
   735→threshold motor units and thus improve neuromuscular efficiency.
   736→Furthermore, this method is a prerequisite for maximal effort training,
   737→meaning that one should have first developed efficient movement
   738→before considering attempting maximal effort lifts. Moreover, the
   739→submaximal effort method can be used strategically for autoregulation.
   740→Stress affects all individuals differently, and asking someone to adhere
   741→to rigid percentages (five sets of three reps at 75 percent of one-rep
   742→max) does not account for daily fluctuations. Using submaximal effort
   743→training allows the individual to build up in weight based on how they
   744→are feeling on any given day. This method is also easier to navigate
   745→because building to a heavy triple for the day is less confusing than
   746→adhering to strict percentage-based guidelines. Submaximal effort
   747→training works both aspects of the force-velocity curve while creating
   748→balanced programming to reduce the risk of overtraining.

  1094→the risk versus reward ratio, so it’s not something that I use with most

  1556→BUILDING A HOME GYM
  1557→Home gyms have become quite popular due to the freedom and
  1558→convenience they provide. Moreover, former athletes are perfect
  1559→candidates for training on their own—most are familiar with how to
  1560→perform big compound movements and over the years have become self-
  1561→sufficient in terms of training. The questions that typically arise when
  1562→building a home gym center around what equipment one needs and why,
  1563→so I’ve compiled two lists: the “must-haves” and the “nice-to-haves.” Keep
  1564→in mind that you do not have to spend an exorbitant amount of money to
  1565→sufficiently train from home—quite the contrary—and considering the
  1566→cost of a gym membership, travel time to and from a gym, and associated
  1567→fuel costs, what you’ll save may make up for the initial investment. Of
  1568→course, nothing here is mandatory, and you can certainly get away with
  1569→a lot less. (Many of the programs in this chapter require less than what’s
  1570→recommended here.)
  1571→Must-Haves
  1572→• Squat rack. A rack can serve a number of purposes vital to your
  1573→training. For instance, you’ll want a rack that allows you to pull
  1574→rack deadlifts, perform multiple-grip pull-ups, perform a variety of
  1575→exercises off of pins (Anderson squats, pin presses, etc.), and set up
  1576→bands for accommodating resistance (AR).
  1577→• Barbell. This one goes without saying, but do yourself a favor and
  1578→invest in a good barbell because you’ll have it for the rest of your life.
  1579→• Dumbbells. The value of having multiple sizes of dumbbells on hand
  1580→is clear, but a few sets can go a long way in keeping your training
  1581→interesting and your progression consistent.
  1582→• Kettlebells. This is another piece of equipment where a few will likely
  1583→be all you need to keep your training interesting.
  1584→• Bands. Having a few sets of bands with different thicknesses will
  1585→allow you to train even when you’re away from home and also adds
  1586→the ability to use AR, which has a number of great benefits already
  1587→covered in chapter 3.
  1588→• Pulling sled. The sled is one of the most versatile tools known to
  1589→man for strength, conditioning, and recovery measures. For less than
  1590→100 dollars, you’ll have a training tool that will take your training to
  1591→the next level. (The sled is utilized in most of the programs in this
  1592→book for good reason.)
