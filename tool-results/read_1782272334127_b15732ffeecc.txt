  2280→strength, speed, and power may actually inhibit them. Two methods that have
  2281→captured the minds of athletes, coaches, and trainers are balance training and
  2282→overspeed training. Balance training is safe but also widely overused despite
  2283→the fact that it is not effective. Overspeed training—along with many training
  2284→devices used in an effort to enhance speed and power—jeopardizes an
  2285→athlete’s running technique and decreases the rate of force development.
  2286→It is important to have a good selection of exercises, but an exercise is
  2287→essential only if it targets the prime movers or the main muscle groups used
  2288→in performing an athletic skill—no more, no less. It is immaterial, for
  2289→example, whether the athlete uses a simple bench or a stability ball to perform
  2290→bench presses. The essential goal is to perform the exercise with continuous
  2291→
  2292→acceleration through the range of motion. At the beginning of a bench press,
  2293→fast-twitch muscle fibers are recruited to defeat inertia and the heavy load of
  2294→the barbell. As the athlete continues to press the barbell upward, he or she
  2295→should attempt to generate the highest possible acceleration. Under these
  2296→conditions, the discharge rate is increased in the same fast-twitch muscle
  2297→fibers. Maximum velocity, therefore, must be achieved toward the end of the
  2298→action to coincide with the instant of releasing a ball or other athletic
  2299→implement during sport performance.
  2300→Similarly, if a high level of strength adaptation is required in the leg muscles,
  2301→the athlete should squat, squat, squat to develop the greatest possible levels of
  2302→strength and adaptation—in other words, do what is necessary. Adding
  2303→variety by implementing different exercises is fine as long as they target the
  2304→same muscle group in the most specific way.
  2305→
  2306→Principles of Strength Training
  2307→The purpose of any strength training program is to produce a continual
  2308→increase in the athlete’s physical capacity. Strength training principles offer
  2309→methods for adapting the body to the various loads used in training; they also
  2310→provide guidelines for individualizing the program to the specific needs of the
  2311→athlete and the sport.
  2312→
  2313→Progressive Increase of Load
  2314→The principle of progressive increase of load is best illustrated by the Greek
  2315→myth about Milo of Croton. To become the world’s strongest man, Milo
  2316→lifted and carried a calf every day, beginning in his teenage years. As the calf
  2317→grew heavier, Milo grew stronger. By the time the calf was a full-grown bull,
  2318→Milo was the world’s strongest man, thanks to long-term progression.
  2319→In more specific terms, training progressively elicits adaptations in the
  2320→structure and functions of the athlete’s body, increasing his or her motor
  2321→potential and ultimately resulting in improved performance. Of course, the
  2322→body reacts both physiologically and psychologically to the increased training
  2323→load (that is, to the sum of the volume and intensity of all the training
  2324→stimuli). Therefore, training also produces gradual changes in nervous
  2325→reaction and functions, neuromuscular coordination, and psychological
  2326→capacity to cope with stress. The entire process requires time and competent
  2327→technical leadership.
  2328→Some coaches employ a consistent training load, called a standard load,
  2329→throughout the year. This approach may cause decreased performance during
  2330→the late competitive phase because the physiological basis of performance has
  2331→decreased and prevents consistent improvements (figure 3.1). Superior
  2332→adaptation and performance are produced only by steadily applying training
  2333→load increments.
  2334→Another traditional strength training approach uses the overload principle.
  2335→Early proponents of this principle claimed that strength and hypertrophy
  2336→increase only if muscles work at their maximum strength capacity against
  2337→workloads greater than those normally encountered (Hellebrand and Houtz
  2338→1956; Lange 1919). Contemporary advocates suggest that the load to
  2339→exhaustion in strength training should be increased throughout the program
  2340→
  2341→(Fox, Bowes, and Foss 1989). Therefore, the curve of load increment can rise
  2342→constantly (figure 3.2).
  2343→Proponents of overloading suggest two ways to increase strength: (1)
  2344→maximum loads to exhaustion, inducing strength gains, and (2) submaximal
  2345→loads to exhaustion, inducing hypertrophy (a popular approach among
  2346→bodybuilders). However, athletes cannot be expected to lift to exhaustion
  2347→every time they work out. This is especially true from the specific preparation
  2348→onward, when most of their energy must be directed to sport-specific
  2349→activities and their bodies must be well recovered in order to perform sportspecific skills optimally.
  2350→

  2358→tightness, impaired sport-specific technical proficiency, fatigue, exhaustion,
  2359→injury, or overtraining. To be effective, a strength training program must
  2360→follow the concept of periodization of strength, in which specific goals for
  2361→each phase lead up to either peak performance at the year’s major
  2362→competitions or the best possible performance throughout a championship.
  2363→To achieve these goals, a more effective approach is step-type loading
  2364→(figure 3.3). The athlete’s ability to tolerate heavy loads improves as the
  2365→result of adaptation to stressors applied in strength training (Councilman
  2366→1968; Harre 1982). The step-type method requires a training load increase
  2367→followed by an unloading phase during which the body adapts, regenerates,
  2368→and prepares for a new increase.
  2369→The frequency of such unloading microcycles is determined by each
  2370→athlete’s needs, the rate of adaptation, and the competitive calendar. Training
  2371→load increases are determined by the rate of the athlete’s performance
  2372→improvement; the intensity increase between steps (weeks) in a macrocycle
  2373→commonly falls between 2% and 5%. An abrupt increase in training load may
  2374→exceed the athlete’s capacity to adapt and therefore affect his or her
  2375→physiological balance.
  2376→The step-type approach does not necessarily mean increasing the load in
  2377→each training session in a linear fashion. Furthermore, a single training
  2378→session is insufficient to cause appreciable body adaptations. To achieve
  2379→adaptation, the same exercise must be repeated several times a week but at
  2380→different intensities, followed by an increase in the following week.
  2381→In figure 3.3, let’s say that each horizontal line represents a week, or
  2382→microcycle, of training and that the load is applied on Monday. This load
  2383→fatigues the body but is still within the capability of the athlete. The body
  2384→adjusts by Wednesday and adapts to the load over the next two days, and by
  2385→Friday the athlete feels stronger and capable of lifting heavier loads. Thus,
  2386→fatigue is followed by adaptation and then a physiological rebound or
  2387→improvement. This new level can be called a new ceiling of adaptation. By
  2388→the next Monday, the athlete is physiologically and psychologically
  2389→comfortable. This process is the reason why it is possible either to increase
  2390→the strength training load linearly throughout the microcycle (if the loading
  2391→parameters at the beginning of the macrocycle were well within the athlete’s
  2392→capacity) or to vary it (heavy on Monday, light on Wednesday, and medium-
  2393→
  2394→heavy on Friday).
  2395→The third step in figure 3.3 is followed by a lower step or unloading
  2396→microcycle. A reduction in overall demand allows the body to regenerate and
  2397→fully adapt. During the unloading week, the athlete recovers almost
  2398→completely from the fatigue accumulated in the first three steps, replenishes
  2399→energy stores, and relaxes psychologically. The body accumulates new
  2400→reserves in anticipation of further increases in training load. Training
  2401→performance usually improves following the unloading microcycle. Testing
  2402→can be planned at the end of the unloading microcycle.

  2407→The shorter the macrocycle (for instance, a 2+1 structure, which entails
  2408→two weeks of loading followed by an unloading week), the lower the increase
  2409→from the beginning load. Thus, a longer macrocycle may permit a larger
  2410→increase but generally starts at a lower intensity. Longer macrocycles (3+1 or
  2411→even 4+1 weeks) are used during general preparation when the intensity at
  2412→the beginning of the macrocycle is low, whereas shorter macrocycles are used
  2413→from the specific preparation onward, as the training intensifies. It is, in fact,
  2414→harder to sustain a prolonged increase of intensity when the intensity at the
  2415→beginning of the macrocycle was already high. Although training load
  2416→increases in steps, the load curve in the annual plan has a wavy shape that
  2417→represents the ongoing increases and decreases of loading to stimulate and
  2418→realize adaptations (see figure 3.4).
  2419→Although the step-loading method is applicable to every sport and athlete,
  2420→two variations are possible—reverse step loading and flat loading—and they
  2421→must be applied carefully and with discretion. In reverse step loading (figure
  2422→3.5), the load decreases rather than increases from step to step. Some Eastern
  2423→
  2424→European weightlifters maintain that this form of loading (planning the
  2425→heaviest loads immediately following a microcycle of low-intensity training)
  2426→is more specific to their physiological needs. Reverse step loading has been
  2427→used in weightlifting since the late 1960s but has not been accepted in any
  2428→other sport. The reason is simple: The goal of strength training for sport is
  2429→progressive adaptation—gradually increasing the athlete’s training
  2430→capabilities—and performance improvements are possible only when training
  2431→capabilities have increased. Reverse loading should be used only during the
  2432→peaking cycle prior to competition as a tapering method. Endurance
  2433→improvements are much better achieved by step loading because volume is
  2434→the main factor, and it is better increased in a step-loading fashion throughout
  2435→the year.

  2462→the lower-intensity microcycle, all the elements must be of lower demand in
  2463→order to facilitate relaxation and recovery.

  2465→function of the training phase and the type of desired training adaptation.
  2466→During the early part of the preparatory phase for all sports, the step loading
  2467→pattern prevails, ensuring better progression (figure 3.7). The flat loading
  2468→
  2469→pattern is better suited for the late preparatory phase, especially for power
  2470→sports and for athletes competing at or beyond the national level.
  2471→
  2472→Variety
  2473→Contemporary training requires the athlete to perform many hours of work.
  2474→The volume and intensity of training sometimes increase yearly, and
  2475→exercises are repeated numerous times. To reach high performance, an athlete
  2476→who is serious about training must dedicate two to four hours each week to
  2477→strength training, in addition to performing technical, tactical, and energy
  2478→systems training.
  2479→Under these conditions, boredom and monotony can become obstacles to
  2480→motivation and improvement. The best way to overcome these obstacles is to
  2481→incorporate variety into the training routines. Variety improves training
  2482→response and positively affects an athlete’s psychological well-being. In order
  2483→to implement variety effectively, however, instructors and coaches must be
  2484→well versed in strength training. They should not use variety for variety’s
  2485→sake. You should never forget, however, that specificity is the key to
  2486→physiological adaptation and performance improvements. Periodization of
  2487→strength naturally includes rational variations of means and methods
  2488→throughout the annual plan to elicit the best neuromuscular adaptations. The
  2489→following guidelines will help you design strength training programs with
  2490→sensible variations over the course of the annual plan.
  2491→
  2492→DEFINING AND UNDERSTANDING
  2493→STRENGTH TRAINING LOAD
  2494→PROGRESSION
  2495→Step loading is the favorite modality for eliciting morpho-functional adaptations through a
  2496→progressive increase of muscular, metabolic, or neural stress over time. There are many
  2497→ways to progress the load rationally, thereby eliciting the desired adaptations, such as
  2498→higher levels of hypertrophy, muscular endurance, maximum strength, or power. In order
  2499→to understand these options, we must analyze the loading variables and how they
  2500→influence the final training effect. Throughout a macrocycle, we can progress one or
  2501→more of these parameters according to the training effects (adaptations) we want to elicit.
  2502→The parameters are described in detail in the following sections.
  2503→
  2504→Repetitions
  2505→The number of repetitions per set is closely linked to the percentage of one repetition
  2506→maximum (1RM). Throughout a macrocycle, we can either increase repetitions in order
  2507→to increase endurance (more volume), keep repetitions the same while manipulating
  2508→other parameters, or decrease repetitions to increase intensity (percentage of 1RM) or to
  2509→unload or peak while maintaining or slightly reducing intensity.
  2510→
  2511→Sets
  2512→Throughout a macrocycle, we can increase the number of sets in order to increase work
  2513→capacity and endurance (more volume). We can also keep the same number of sets to
  2514→increase one of the other parameters or to decrease the number of sets to unload or
  2515→peak. The number of sets (volume) is the single most influential variable on the training’s
  2516→residual fatigue effect.
  2517→
  2518→Rest Interval
  2519→The rest interval directly influences the final training effect. We can increase the rest
  2520→interval if the macrocycle moves toward a decrease in reps and an increase in intensity
  2521→(percentage of 1RM). We can decrease the rest interval in order to increase endurance
  2522→(more density). Or, we can keep the rest interval the same while changing one or more
  2523→of the other variables. When performing a series of sets for power endurance and
  2524→muscle endurance, reducing the rest interval between sets (while maintaining their power
  2525→output) allows a densification that later translates into a higher mean power output for a
  2526→longer duration.
  2527→
  2528→Progress from full range of motion (ROM) in the general preparation
  2529→phase to sport-specific ROM in the late specific preparation phase and
  2530→the competitive phase. Be mindful of the fact that full-ROM exercises
  2531→cause more muscular tension than their partial counterparts; therefore, a
  2532→low volume of such exercises should always be used for maintaining
  2533→maximum strength (Bloomquist et al. 2013; Hartmann et al. 2012;
  2534→Bazyler et al. 2014).
  2535→Vary exercise selection, especially for children, by using more unilateral
  2536→and dumbbell exercises during anatomical adaptation and compensation
  2537→macrocycles.
  2538→Vary loading by using the principle of progressive increase of load in
  2539→training.
  2540→Vary the type and speed or tempo of muscle contractions. The usual
  2541→pattern goes from slow eccentrics (3 to 5 seconds) and controlled
  2542→concentrics (1 or 2 seconds) in anatomical adaptation to slow eccentrics
  2543→and fast concentrics (1 second or less) in the maximum strength
  2544→
  2545→macrocycles and then to fast eccentrics and explosive concentrics in
  2546→macrocycles for power, power endurance, or muscle endurance short.
  2547→This pattern is represented by a trio of numbers (King 1998); the first
  2548→number is the duration in seconds of the eccentric phase, the second
  2549→number is the length of the pause between the eccentric and concentric
  2550→phases, and the third number is the duration of the concentric phase (an
  2551→“X” means explosive).
  2552→Vary the method. Move from body weight, dumbbells, and machines
  2553→during macrocycles for anatomical adaptation and hypertrophy to mainly
  2554→barbells or strength training machines in macrocycles for maximum
  2555→strength, conversion to specific strength, and maintenance.
  2556→Variety in exercise selection keeps the athlete motivated and the
  2557→adaptation fresh. Problems can arise, however, when coaches and athletes
  2558→substitute an exercise or change a method solely for the sake of doing
  2559→something new. The principle of variety should be used only if the change or
  2560→substitution keeps the athlete on the path of adaptation.
  2561→When athletes reach a high level of competition and fitness, certain
  2562→exercises should always be included in their regimen. Coaches can alter the
  2563→load or method used in training but should always stick to the movements
  2564→that best work the kinetic chain prime movers used in the sport or best elicit
  2565→the threshold of stimulation needed for maximum gains.
  2566→Coaches and athletes should also remember that sport training is different
  2567→from fitness training and that fitness ideals rarely work in sport training. For
  2568→example, many strength training instructors preach that exercises should be
  2569→altered every other week. Although this approach may be beneficial when
  2570→training personal clients who require constant variety and excitement, it is not
  2571→appropriate for athletes. Alternating strength exercises for a given sport can
  2572→be done only if the new exercise addresses the prime movers in that sport.
  2573→Because adaptation is a physiological requirement for athletic
  2574→improvement, the same type of training and muscle groups must be targeted
  2575→repeatedly in order to produce the highest degree of adaptation. Without
  2576→constant increase in the adaptation of bodily systems, athletes see no
  2577→observable improvement in their performance. Yes, repeating the same type
  2578→of exercise day in and day out is very boring. But so is constantly repeating
  2579→the technical skills of running, swimming, cycling, and rowing, to name a
  2580→
  2581→few. Yet nobody suggests to runners, swimmers, cyclists, and rowers that
  2582→they alter their primary skill training because it is boring. Coaches should
  2583→choose a number of exercises that have the same functional purpose but add
  2584→variety to training. In this way, they can spice up the training program but
  2585→keep in mind the main focus: the athlete’s level of physiological adaptation.
  2586→
  2587→Individualization
  2588→Contemporary training requires individualization. Each athlete must be
  2589→treated according to his or her ability, potential, and strength training
  2590→background. Sometimes coaches are tempted to follow the training programs
  2591→of successful athletes, disregarding their particular athlete’s needs,
  2592→experience, and abilities. Even worse, they sometimes insert such programs
  2593→into the training schedules of junior athletes, who are not ready, either
  2594→physiologically or psychologically, for such high loads.
  2595→Before designing a training program, the coach should assess the athlete.
  2596→Even athletes who are equal in performance do not necessarily have the same
  2597→work capacity. Individual work abilities are determined by several biological
  2598→and psychological factors and must be considered in specifying the amount of
  2599→work (volume), the load (intensity), and the type of strength training an
  2600→athlete performs. Work capacity is also affected by training background.
  2601→Work demand should be based on experience. Even when an athlete exhibits
  2602→great improvement, the coach must still be cautious in estimating training
  2603→load (volume plus intensity). When assigning athletes of different
  2604→backgrounds and experiences to the same training group, the coach should
  2605→consider individual characteristics and potential.
  2606→Another factor to consider when planning a training program is the
  2607→athlete’s rate of recovery. When planning and evaluating the content and
  2608→stress of training, coaches should remember to assess demanding factors
  2609→outside of training. They should be aware of the athlete’s lifestyle and
  2610→emotional involvements. Rate of recovery can also be affected by schoolwork
  2611→and other activities. For help in monitoring the rate of recovery, coaches can
  2612→use a heart rate variability monitoring device.
  2613→
  2614→To reach the peak of his athletic potential, an athlete needs an individualized and periodized
  2615→training plan.

  2617→Sex-based differences also require consideration. Generally speaking, the
  2618→total body strength of women is 63.5% of that of men. More specifically, the
  2619→upper-body strength of women is, on average, 55.8% of that of men; the
  2620→lower-body strength of women, however, is much closer to that of men, at an
  2621→average of 71.9% (Laubach 1976). Women tend to have lower hypertrophy
  2622→levels and lower work capacity than men do, mostly because their
  2623→testosterone level is as much as 20 times lower (Wright 1980). Female
  2624→athletes can follow the same training programs as male athletes without
  2625→worrying about excessive bulky muscles. They can apply the same loading
  2626→pattern and use the same training methods as men without concern, except
  2627→when monitoring their recovery ability.
  2628→Strength training for women should be rigorously continuous, without
  2629→long interruptions. Plyometric training should progress carefully over a long
  2630→
  2631→period to allow for adaptation. Because women generally tend to be
  2632→physically weaker than men, improved and increased strength training can
  2633→produce more visible gains in performance (Lephart et al. 2002). Further
  2634→increases in strength from plyometric training promote greater power
  2635→capabilities. As for training the energy systems, women can use the same
  2636→training methods as men.
  2637→One major issue involving sex difference is injury in sport. Female
  2638→athletes often report a higher incidence of lower-body injuries, in particular to
  2639→the knee joint. Studies have been conducted in an effort to explain this fact
  2640→both physiologically and anatomically. For instance, when performing the
  2641→kinematics and electromyographic activity of the one-leg squat,
  2642→intercollegiate female athletes, as compared with their male counterparts,
  2643→demonstrated less lateral trunk flexion and more ankle dorsiflexion, ankle
  2644→pronation, hip adduction, hip flexion, and external rotation (Zeller et al.
  2645→2003). Furthermore, female athletes who participate in jumping and agility
  2646→exercises tend to exhibit less muscular-stiffness protection of the knee than
  2647→males do (Wojtys et al. 2003). Involuntarily, females allow their knees to
  2648→drift inward (knock knees), which places more stress on the knee joint and
  2649→can aggravate or strain the anterior cruciate ligament.
  2650→Although sex-specific planning is not entirely required, these differences
  2651→indicate that time should be dedicated to improving maximum strength, in
  2652→particular the strength of the lower body, in female athletes. In particular,
  2653→increased strengthening of the quadriceps and hamstrings at the end of the
  2654→early preparatory phase can physiologically prepare the athlete for gamespecific drills and power training, which place more stress on the knee joint
  2655→and can lead to injury.
  2656→
  2657→Specificity
  2658→To be effective and achieve greater adaptation, training must be designed to
  2659→develop sport-specific strength. Toward this end, a coach must make a simple
  2660→performance model analysis of the sport for which the strength training
  2661→program is being created. The analysis should take into account the
  2662→ergogenesis (i.e., the energy contribution to the sporting event of each of the
  2663→three energy systems), the specific joints’ range of motion, the planes of
  2664→movement, and the prime movers and their actions (eccentric, isometric,
  2665→
  2666→concentric). Training specificity is the most important mechanism for sportspecific neuromuscular adaptations.
  2667→
  2668→Specificity and the Dominant Energy System
  2669→The coach should carefully consider the dominant energy system in the
  2670→chosen sport. For instance, muscular endurance training is most appropriate
  2671→for endurance sports such as rowing, long-distance swimming, canoeing, and
  2672→speedskating. The coach must also consider the specific muscle groups
  2673→involved (the prime movers) and the movement patterns characteristic of the
  2674→sport. Exercises should use the sport’s key movement patterns. They must
  2675→also improve the power of the prime movers. Normally, gains in power
  2676→transfer to skill improvement.
  2677→
  2678→Specificity Versus a Methodical Approach
  2679→The principle of specificity sprang from the idea that the optimal strength
  2680→training program must be specific. According to this principle, an exercise or
  2681→type of training that is specific to the skills of a sport results in faster
  2682→adaptation and yields faster performance improvement. However, specificity
  2683→should be applied only to advanced athletes during the competitive phase.
  2684→These athletes devote a large portion of their annual strength training plan to
  2685→train the dominant strength in their selected sport.
  2686→
  2687→THE FALLACY OF FUNCTIONAL
  2688→TRAINING
  2689→Occasionally, certain authors advance a new term intended to sound like a scientific
  2690→novelty: functional training. This refers to exercises performed on different types of
  2691→equipment, such as balls, ABS (plastic) pipes with foam, and proprioceptive platforms, all
  2692→of which are designed to create a more difficult environment in order to increase
  2693→participation by small and deep stabilizing muscles (Staley 2005). But is it possible that
  2694→Olympic and world championship events have been won, and world records set, without
  2695→the athletes having worked on specific strength, or having done so in an optimal manner,
  2696→until the year 2000?
  2697→Specific strength and functional strength are not synonymous. Training for the specific
  2698→strength needed in a given sport involves replicating the specific modality of force
  2699→expression needed in the chosen event, both neurally and metabolically. This training is
  2700→achieved by using specific exercises that mimic the action of the kinetic chains in the
  2701→specific motor skills (including specific joints’ range of motion and force vector).
  2702→Particular emphasis is placed on the prime movers, without disturbing the motor patterns
  2703→
  2704→required for the sport’s technique.
  2705→In contrast, rather than referring to the physiological and biomechanical parameters of
  2706→the specific event or motor skill, the term functional strength is more commonly used to
  2707→describe how strength is trained—that is, its training means free weights or cables,
  2708→unilateral training, and possibly standing and moving through more than one plane of
  2709→motion. (Exceptions to this definition are found in propaedeutic exercises and some core
  2710→stability exercises.) In other words, in order to talk about specific strength training, the
  2711→essential starting points are the biomechanical—and in particular the physiological—
  2712→parameters of the event. Functional training, in contrast, is simply defined by the use of
  2713→exercises with the characteristics just listed.
  2714→To state that the exercise selection fully defines the degree of functionality of a
  2715→strength training program is, obviously, methodologically wrong, but it is also true that the
  2716→best functionalists apply the concept of periodization of strength to their planning. In
  2717→addition, not only do they consider biomechanics when selecting exercises, but they also
  2718→consider physiology when choosing load parameters, even if they prefer certain
  2719→exercises and methods. We should, however, ask ourselves to what extent certain
