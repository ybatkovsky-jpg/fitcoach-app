  2780→kayaking, and canoeing. From the late 1980s on, the cords also started to
  2781→be used on the North American continent, at first in fitness training and a
  2782→few years later for sport training. Once again, the producers of these
  2783→cords misunderstood their role and benefits. It was proposed that the
  2784→cords also be used for the development of power. This is an error in
  2785→understanding exercise equipment and its benefit in training. Elastic
  2786→cords are not conducive to the development of power for the simple
  2787→reason that as the cord is stretched, resistance increases. Every time you
  2788→apply force against a resistance, the movement is slowed down and you
  2789→cannot accelerate through the range of motion or perform an explosive
  2790→movement. That is why such an exercise does not accrue to the
  2791→development of power. In addition, at least in the case of exercises that
  2792→are performed in the shoulder region, you generate a strain in the
  2793→shoulder’s ligaments that may result in injury.
  2794→Medicine balls (MB) are among the most effective implements for
  2795→developing power. For the best adaptation to MB training you at first
  2796→progressively increase the weight of the balls. Then you should
  2797→progressively decrease the weight of the balls as you approach the
  2798→competitive phase. Lowering the weight facilitates a quicker force
  2799→application against the ball, which results in a forceful and powerful
  2800→throw and, ultimately, in increased distance. Do you know why these
  2801→balls are called medicine balls? Because in mid-1800 they were used in
  2802→medical clinics in Central Europe for rehabilitation purposes.
  2803→A takeoff is initiated first by flexing the ankles and the knees, followed
  2804→
  2805→by leg and arm upward actions to elevate the center of gravity, and
  2806→followed by an actual jump.
  2807→Strength training for increased takeoff in figure skating tends to be
  2808→incorrectly done. If skaters increase the power of the takeoff they will be
  2809→able to jump slightly higher and, as a result, have longer time to do the
  2810→rotations they want.
  2811→Strength training in Nordic skiing is more important for those who use
  2812→skating technique. If you look at the leg and arm actions (legs and arms
  2813→push-offs), you will see the need for better strength training programs.
  2814→Power skating in ice hockey requires a lower position of the trunk. In
  2815→this position the vertical projection of the center of gravity will be well
  2816→ahead of the feet and, consequently, be able to generate higher velocity.
  2817→However, this low position of the trunk cannot be properly maintained
  2818→unless your low back (lumbar) and intervertebral muscle are stronger.
  2819→Use trunk extensions or rotations against resistance to improve the
  2820→strength of these muscles.
  2821→During the turn in a slalom race in skiing, a centrifugal force is
  2822→generated. This force may take the athlete out of the race unless he or
  2823→she is able to load the downhill ski to apply centripetal force against the
  2824→slope. Strong legs, via MxS, will help the skier be competitive.
  2825→MxS does assist the athlete during many types of jumps, to overcome
  2826→gravitational force but also to absorb the shock of landing. In freestyle
  2827→snowboarding, ski jumping, spiking in volleyball, basketball, and so on,
  2828→eccentric strength must also be trained. Unfortunately, most athletes
  2829→rarely train eccentric strength.
  2830→MxS is essential for explosive, powerful starting in most sports. The
  2831→force applied against the starting block varies according to the maximum
  2832→force of the athlete, which is often between 285 and 375 pounds (130
  2833→and 170 kg), measured on a force plate. This is valid also for the start in
  2834→other sports, such as speedskating, bobsled, and the like.
  2835→MxS is equally important for post-start acceleration in many sports.
  2836→Why? Because during the acceleration an athlete has to apply high force
  2837→to overcome the force of gravity and to move the body forward.
  2838→The speed of a punch in boxing depends on the force of the athlete. Fear
  2839→of gaining additional weight makes MxS seem to be an undesirable
  2840→
  2841→athletic activity. But exercises with a load of 50% to 70%, performed
  2842→very fast, make the boxer faster without gaining body weight.
  2843→Do you remember Aeneas and his rowers? If you want to increase speed
  2844→in rowing, kayaking, canoeing, and swimming, you must train MxS. On
  2845→the other hand, if you want to increase the capacity to apply force
  2846→repetitively, you must use the muscle-endurance training method.
  2847→There is a continuous debate whether 1-leg squats are better than 2-leg
  2848→squats. People tend to forget the most important element for improving
  2849→MxS: the capacity to recruit the fast-twitch (FT) muscle fibers in action.
  2850→The higher the load, the higher the capacity to recruit more FT fibers. As
  2851→per the data collected by the author in electromyography testing, the
  2852→highest electrical activity was recorded in 2-leg squats (the level of
  2853→electrical activity demonstrates the level of stimulation on the action of
  2854→specific muscles). At the same time, the 2-leg squat ensures a better
  2855→equilibrium, and as a result, the athlete is able to lift heavier loads.
  2856→Some physiologists promote the idea that what is essential for the
  2857→development of strength is time under tension, or how long a contraction
  2858→lasts. While this is a good method to increase muscle size for
  2859→bodybuilders, it is not recommended for strength training for sports.
  2860→Remember what is determinant in strength training for sport: speed,
  2861→explosiveness, and quick changes of direction. Time under tension is not
  2862→conducive to speed and power development.
  2863→Have you ever asked yourself why many figure skaters fall when landing
  2864→from a jump? The answer is simple: they do not have good leg strength
  2865→to begin with, not to mention that landing requires a good eccentric force
  2866→to absorb the shock of landing. After a figure skater reaches the highest
  2867→point of a jump, the body accelerates downward, toward the ice. This
  2868→acceleration poses a real problem for the poorly trained athlete,
  2869→especially if a jump has to be followed by another jump. To be able to
  2870→jump again, the skater also needs to train reactive power, or the capacity
  2871→to land and immediately jump again.

  2883→Introduction to Part II
  2884→Planning takes place during the thousands of hours you spend behind your
  2885→desk and in the field, in the gym, or at the pool alongside your hardworking,
  2886→perspiring athletes. Nothing is possible in athletics unless you use your time
  2887→diligently to plan and periodize your athletes’ work to reach peak
  2888→performance.
  2889→The terms periodization, planning, and programming are often used as if
  2890→they were synonymous, but they are not. Planning-periodization incorporates
  2891→two basic concepts: periodization of biomotor abilities and short- and longterm planning, both of which are determinants for success.
  2892→Periodization of biomotor abilities, strength, speed, and endurance
  2893→facilitates the development of the sport-specific combination of motor
  2894→abilities to the highest level in order to reach peak performance for the time
  2895→of the main competitions of the year. Periodization of biomotor abilities is
  2896→based on the following premises.
  2897→Improvement in sport performance is based on your ability to increase
  2898→your athletes’ physiological potential to the highest level without
  2899→sacrificing the refinement of sport-specific skills.
  2900→Morpho-functional adaptations, the positive changes in body structure
  2901→and functions, require time to manifest themselves.
  2902→The development of biomotor abilities and the improvement of technical
  2903→and tactical factors require a progressive approach in which the intensity
  2904→and volume of training should result in improving athletes’ potential
  2905→beyond the previous level of adaptation.
  2906→Manipulation of training variables and the use of an appropriate planning
  2907→methodology facilitates peak performance.
  2908→An athlete cannot maintain peak performance for a prolonged period of
  2909→time.
  2910→Periodization of annual plan, as the foundation of comprehending
  2911→planning methodology, refers to dividing the training program into shorter,
  2912→easier-to-manage training phases. Periodization of annual plan is beneficial
  2913→for the following reasons.
  2914→The scope of planning specific training phases is to reach superior
  2915→
  2916→adaptation that facilitates peak performance.
  2917→It helps the coach to design a rational structure of training.
  2918→It enhances coaches’ awareness of the time available for each training
  2919→phase.
  2920→It integrates, at appropriate times, technical, tactical, physical, and
  2921→nutrition plans and psychological techniques to lead to the improvement
  2922→of athletes’ performance.
  2923→It facilitates the management of fatigue to help the coach plan a rational
  2924→alternation of loading with unloading periods (alternating training with
  2925→recovery), thus maximizing adaptation. This rational plan helps the
  2926→coach avoid the accumulation of residual fatigue and consequently helps
  2927→prevent overtraining.
  2928→While planning refers to the creation of a structure for the training process
  2929→via certain planning tools such as the long-term plan, the annual plan, the
  2930→macrocycles, and the microcycles, programming is the process of filling
  2931→those tools with the details of the actual training, such as the training methods
  2932→and training means to be used. Therefore, as a coach, you are not just a
  2933→psychologist and a nutritionist, but also a periodizer, a planner, and a training
  2934→programmer. Despite the many missions you have with your athletes, we are
  2935→certain you love what you are doing and the challenges you are faced with,
  2936→but you also enjoy the rewards the profession offers you. There is nothing
  2937→else you would rather do!

  2939→Planning-Periodization: A Clarification of
  2940→Terminologies
  2941→As examined in more detail in Periodization: Theory and Methodology of
  2942→Training (Bompa and Buzzichelli, 2018) sports planning is not a novel
  2943→creation. In simple forms it has existed since the Greek Olympic Games (776
  2944→BC–393 AD), but the sophistication of sports planning culminated with the
  2945→German planning for the 1936 Berlin Olympics, the work of Leonid
  2946→Matveyev and other East European specialists. While Matveyev’s book (The
  2947→Problem of Periodization of Sport Training, 1965), analyzed the training
  2948→plans and diaries of Russian athletes who competed in the 1952 Olympics,
  2949→Tudor Bompa was already applying periodization of training during the same
  2950→period with Mihaela Penes (gold medalist in javelin throw at the 1964 Tokyo
  2951→Olympic Games). His work in the early 1960s resulted in what would become
  2952→his concept of the periodization of strength. The popularity of periodization
  2953→has increased dramatically, especially in North America, after the first edition
  2954→of Bompa’s popular work (Bompa 1983).
  2955→The terminology Tudor Bompa uses is based on the original work of the
  2956→Greek author Flavius Philostratus (AD 170–245), the Roman physician
  2957→Claudius Galenus (Galen, AD 129–217), and the German specialists who
  2958→designed the German athletes’ training plans for the Berlin Olympic Games
  2959→(1936):
  2960→quadrennial, a four-year or Olympic training plan,
  2961→annu al plan, a training plan for a year, divided into smaller training
  2962→phases,
  2963→macrocycles (approximately 4 weeks),
  2964→microcycles, a training phase of 4-7 days, usually one full week, and
  2965→training unit, or training session.

  2981→The introduction of spurious terms into the theory and methodology of
  2982→training since the 1990s is not justified because no new information has been
  2983→added to the body of knowledge in that theory. In fact, some terms proposed
  2984→lately are mostly semantics, fake terms without scientific substance. A brief
  2985→analysis of contemporaneous terminologies might shed additional light on the
  2986→subject.
  2987→Periodization. When Leonid Matveyev borrowed this term from history,
  2988→he was referring to something new that was not yet part of the
  2989→vocabulary of that period. Nor was there much written information on
  2990→the subject. For better time management, the annual plan is divided into
  2991→periods of training phase; he called this planning concept periodization.
  2992→Some authors see Matveyev’s periodization model as the classical
  2993→periodization. But the true classical plans should be considered to be
  2994→those actually proposed first by Flavius Philostratus (AD 170–245) and
  2995→then by the German specialists who devised the training plans used for
  2996→the 1936 Berlin Olympics.
  2997→The annual plan has existed since the ancient Olympic Games (the
  2998→Olympic Year, referred to by Galen). Yet the best coaches of the late
  2999→1800s to early 1900s knew about it and used simple annual planning and
  3000→the breakdown of the annual plan into smaller training phases. In other
  3001→words, Matveyev’s work does not bring much new information about
  3002→
  3003→this subject. He was, however, the first author to use the term
  3004→periodization.
  3005→Matveyev proposed the term macrocycle to define an annual plan. But
  3006→the German specialists used a four-week plan (grosser, or large, plan) to
  3007→define what we call a macrocycle.
  3008→The microcycle (the term proposed by Matveyev for a weekly training
  3009→plan) has existed since the ancient Olympics as the tetra system, a fourday cycle mentioned by Philostratus.
  3010→Selected fallacies of training terminologies include
  3011→Block training. Instead of using the traditional term training phase, some
  3012→authors have adopted the term block training as if it brought something
  3013→new. What is the difference between training phase and block training?
  3014→Furthermore, the term block is often used incorrectly to indicate a phase
  3015→of training or a macrocycle.
  3016→Linear periodization. Some authors have introduced this term to refer to
  3017→traditional periodization, which is a series of training phases intended to
  3018→lead to and facilitate peak performance during the competitive phase.
  3019→But why is it necessary to call something linear that is already
  3020→recognized as being traditional? Besides, an annual plan has a linear
  3021→format!
  3022→Undulatory periodization is a hybrid term that has been proposed by
  3023→some authors without logic, without any base on the way the
  3024→periodization process is constructed. In fact, this term has been copied
  3025→from Bompa’s concept of the curve of rating training load (Bompa 1993)
  3026→and is a faulty interpretation of the load alternation between training
  3027→units within a microcycle. Someone unfamiliar with sport training and
  3028→with the intent of being original has presumably fabricated this term in
  3029→order to enlarge the terminology of periodization-planning.
  3030→Pendular development and complex development of biomotor abilities
  3031→are terms incorrectly taken from their original source (Bompa 1993). The
  3032→original term for pendular development was alternation of strength
  3033→training (maximum strength alternated with power training) that resulted
  3034→in better power development. In fact, pendular is exactly that: an
  3035→alternation of the various types of strength training. Complex
  3036→
  3037→development refers to complex training, which represents a strengthtraining phase in which both maximum strength and power are trained
  3038→simultaneously, mostly during the precompetitive phase. Originally
  3039→(Bompa 1993 and 1999), this method was called maxex training, or
  3040→maximum explosiveness training.
  3041→Sequential integration and simultaneous integration within periodization
  3042→of training also create confusion among many authors and practitioners.
  3043→Both of these planning tools are presented, totally inaccurately, as if they
  3044→are mutually exclusive, but this is not the case.
  3045→Sequential periodization proposes that certain biomotor abilities are
  3046→developed in a specific sequence, which is true. Take, for example,
  3047→periodization of strength of an annual plan (figure II.2), where
  3048→anatomical adaptation (AA), maximum strength (MxS), and power (P)
  3049→follow in a specific, physiologically justified sequence (neuromuscular
  3050→strategy). Yet all sports, for logical and physiological reasons, also
  3051→follow the simultaneous periodization model.
  3052→Simultaneous periodization. In designing the periodization of dominant
  3053→abilities for the majority of other sports, for physiological adaptation
  3054→reasons and the methodology of developing the dominant abilities in the
  3055→selected sport, coaches should follow the simultaneous periodization
  3056→model (figure II.3), or the simultaneous integration of the dominant
  3057→biomotor abilities of the selected sport.
  3058→If you take each ability separately, horizontally, particularly periodization
  3059→of strength, this is similar to sequential periodization. However, for the vast
  3060→majority of sports, where there are two or three biomotor abilities to periodize
  3061→at the same time, this process has to be performed simultaneously, not
  3062→independently. The dates of simultaneous periodization are dictated by the
  3063→competition calendar and the duration of a biomotor ability to reach the
  3064→desired standards of adaptation.
  3065→While a small number of authors have promoted the use of sequential
  3066→periodization only, this type of periodization cannot be taken seriously.
  3067→Physiologically and methodically you cannot train strength in one phase,
  3068→followed by endurance, and then return to strength training. It is
  3069→inappropriate, a physiological and methodological error, that does not result
  3070→in the best performance at the desired time of the year. What happens to the
  3071→
  3072→adaptation and improvement of strength, specifically the neuromuscular
  3073→strategy of power training while you are training endurance? How do you
  3074→retain the training effect of the previous abilities? In doing so you diminish
  3075→the early adaptation syndrome of strength, its training effect, and its influence
  3076→on the athlete’s improvement.
  3077→That is why the vast majority of sports should use simultaneous
  3078→periodization, in which you integrate the periodization of dominant abilities
  3079→of the selected sport, particularly team sports, for which the preparatory phase
  3080→is often much shorter than for some individual sports.

  3088→Manipulation of Training
  3089→Variables
  3090→To create successful strength training programs, coaches and athletes
  3091→manipulate several training variables, mainly volume and intensity. Both the
  3092→volume and the intensity of training, as well as its frequency, change
  3093→according to the competition schedule and the objective of training. More
  3094→specific factors within the categories of volume and intensity include load
  3095→(which is generally expressed as a percentage of one repetition maximum, or
  3096→1RM), repetitions, sets, tempo or speed of execution (only for muscle
  3097→endurance training), and rest intervals between sets. Manipulating these
  3098→specific variables alters the volume, intensity, degree of effort, and density of
  3099→training—and, consequently, the training effect.
  3100→Strength training programs must also include a mix of general and sportspecific exercises. As a rule of thumb, the early part of the yearly training
  3101→program, which can include two to three months of preparatory training,
  3102→should include a higher volume of training with a low proportion of sportspecific exercises. As the competitive season approaches, however, the
  3103→intensity of training is stressed, the volume is decreased, and sport-specific
  3104→exercises become a major part of the program.
  3105→
  3106→Training Volume
  3107→Volume, or the quantity of work performed, can be measured either in terms
  3108→of the weight lifted per training session, per microcycle, per macrocycle, or in
  3109→terms of the total number of sets or reps performed per training session, per
  3110→microcycle, per macrocycle, or per year. Instructors, coaches, and athletes
  3111→should keep records of the tonnage (total weight) lifted or the sets and reps
  3112→performed per session or per training phase to help them plan future training
  3113→volumes.
  3114→Training volume varies based on the sport’s particular physical demands,
  3115→the athlete’s strength training background, and the type of strength training
  3116→performed. For example, athletes attempting to develop muscular endurance
  3117→use a high volume of training because of the many reps they perform.
  3118→Maximum strength training, on the other hand, results in lower tonnage and
  3119→density, despite the high load, because of the lower number of total reps and
  3120→the longer rest intervals. A medium training volume is typical for athletes in
  3121→sports that require power, because the load is low to medium.
  3122→Overall training volume becomes more important as athletes approach
  3123→high performance. There are no shortcuts. Athletic performance requires high
  3124→weekly training frequency, which, in turn, may result in greater training
  3125→volume. As athletes adapt to a higher volume of training, they experience
  3126→better recovery and a higher level of structural and neural adaptation. This
  3127→increase in work capacity can later translate into better handling of the
  3128→intensification phases, as well as better performance overall.
  3129→Once optimal volume has been reached, the main stressor for mature
  3130→athletes should be intensity. Work capacity is acquired over time; therefore,
  3131→in order to increase training volume by increasing training frequency, it is
  3132→necessary at first to lower the volume per training unit. This reduction is
  3133→achieved by dividing the previous total volume of the microcycle by the new,
  3134→higher number of training units. Increasing the number of training units while
  3135→maintaining the same weekly training volume allows the work to be
  3136→intensified thanks to increased recovery as the volume and duration per unit
  3137→decrease. As a result, greater adaptations are possible (Bompa and
  3138→Buzzichelli 2018).
  3139→Later, the volume per session can be increased, if necessary. For example,
  3140→
  3141→suppose that your objective is to increase strength training from three to four
  3142→training units per microcycle and that your starting point is a microcycle with
  3143→three strength training sessions, each with 8 tons of volume (thus 24 tons of
  3144→total volume per microcycle). In this situation, here are an incorrect method
  3145→and a correct method:
  3146→Incorrect method. Add a training unit of 8 tons, thus abruptly increasing
  3147→the total volume of the microcycle from 24 to 32 tons (an increase of
  3148→25%).
  3149→Correct method. Divide the total volume of 24 tons by the new total of
  3150→four training units. The total microcycle volume remains unchanged at
  3151→24 tons, but the volume of the single sessions is reduced to 6 tons each (a
  3152→decrease of 25%), thus allowing a greater average intensity and better
  3153→recovery. The volume per session can be increased later if need be.
  3154→Strength training volume depends on the athlete’s biological makeup, the
  3155→specifics of the sport, and the importance of strength in that sport. Mature
  3156→athletes with a good strength training background can tolerate higher
  3157→volumes, but volume should not be increased for its own sake. Rather, it
  3158→should be increased if the specific situation requires it—and never at the
  3159→expense of the quality of sport-specific training.
  3160→Because biomotor abilities training (increasing the athlete’s motor
  3161→potential) must be integrated with sport-specific training (specific
  3162→performance), our starting point should be the least training volume that is
  3163→effective at increasing the indexes of a certain biomotor ability. During
  3164→general preparation, the volume of biomotor abilities training can be such as
  3165→to temporarily affect the specific performance. During specific preparation,
  3166→there should a correlation between the increase of a biomotor ability’s
  3167→indexes and the specific performance. And during the competitive phase,
  3168→biomotor abilities training should be such as to allow maintenance, slight
  3169→improvement, or peaking of the specific performance.
  3170→A dramatic or abrupt increase in volume can be detrimental, irrespective
  3171→of the athlete’s sport or ability, resulting in fatigue, uneconomical muscular
  3172→work, and possibly injury. These pitfalls can be avoided by implementing a
  3173→progressive plan with an appropriate method of monitoring load increments.
  3174→Here are a few rules of thumb.
  3175→
  3176→A session dedicated to strength training should not last longer than 75
  3177→minutes unless it is a high-volume, maximum-strength session with long
  3178→rest intervals, or a muscle-endurance-long session for an ultra-endurance
  3179→athlete.
  3180→The volume of an anatomical adaptation session should fall between 16
  3181→and 32 total sets; a hypertrophy session between 16 and 24 (and less than
  3182→one hour in duration); a maximum strength session between 16 and 24; a
  3183→power session between 10 and 16; and a power endurance or muscleendurance-short session between 4 and 12.
  3184→Once the sets’ volume is established, it should not vary by more than
  3185→50% within a macrocycle—for example, 2 sets per exercise in the first
  3186→microcycle, 3 sets per exercise in the second and third microcycles, 2
  3187→sets in the fourth (unload) microcycle.
  3188→The total volume depends on several factors, and the determinant is the
  3189→importance of strength to the sport. For instance, international-class
  3190→weightlifters often plan 33 short tons (30 tonnes) per training session and
  3191→approximately 44,000 short tons (40,000 tonnes) per year. For other sports,
  3192→the volume differs drastically (table 4.1). Power and speed sports require a
  3193→much higher volume than boxing does; in sports in which muscular
  3194→endurance is dominant, such as rowing and canoeing, the volume of strength
  3195→per year can be three to six times higher.

  3197→Training Intensity
  3198→In strength training, intensity is expressed as a percentage of load or 1RM
  3199→maximum. It is an indicator of the strength of the nervous stimuli employed
  3200→in training, and it is determined by the degree to which the central nervous
  3201→system (CNS) is called into action. Stimulus strength depends on load, speed
  3202→of movement, and variation of rest intervals between reps. Training load,
  3203→expressed as intensity percentage of 1RM, refers to the mass or weight lifted.
  3204→Strength training employs the intensity zones and loads presented in table 4.2.
  3205→
  3206→A supermaximum load exceeds one’s maximum strength (1RM). In most
  3207→cases, loads between 100% and 120% of 1RM can be used by applying the
  3208→eccentric (yielding to the force of gravity) or isometric (maximal contraction
  3209→without joint movement) method. Only a few athletes with a robust strength
  3210→training background should use supermaximum loads. Such loads should be
  3211→employed for limited time periods and only for some muscle groups, in
  3212→particular those muscle groups whose eccentric loading is high during the
  3213→sport-specific activity (for instance, the hamstrings during sprinting or the
  3214→quads during landing or changing directions). Most other athletes should be
  3215→restricted to loads of no more than 100% of 1RM.
  3216→Maximum load can range from 90% to 100% of 1RM, heavy load from
  3217→80% to 90%, medium load from 60% to 80%, and low load from 30% to 60%
  3218→percent. Each intensity zone elicits slightly different neuromuscular
  3219→adaptations and necessitates a precise progression. Intensities above 90%
  3220→percent should be used sparingly, especially to concentric failure, for their
  3221→
