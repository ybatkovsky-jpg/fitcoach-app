   208‚Üí   257‚Üí   281‚Üí   281‚ÜíThe simple beauty of monitoring heart rate is that it is based on your own
   209‚Üí   258‚Üí   282‚Üí   282‚Üíheart‚Äôs capacity and nobody else‚Äôs. The heart is a muscle, and it responds in

   276‚Üí   325‚Üí   349‚Üí   349‚Üírelationships between two variables. The term linear means that as one
   277‚Üí   326‚Üí   350‚Üí   350‚Üívariable increases, the other variable increases in proportion to the change
   278‚Üí   327‚Üí   351‚Üí   351‚Üíin the first variable. If one variable changes and the other changes but not in
   279‚Üí   328‚Üí   352‚Üí   352‚Üíproportion, that is described as nonlinear.
   280‚Üí   329‚Üí   353‚Üí   353‚ÜíAlthough both heart rate and O increase in response to increasing
   281‚Üí   330‚Üí   354‚Üí   354‚Üí2
   282‚Üí   331‚Üí   355‚Üí   355‚Üíintensity, their responses are not entirely linear. For example, at lower
   283‚Üí   332‚Üí   356‚Üí   356‚Üíexercise intensities, both variables increase in a similar (linear) manner, but
   284‚Üí   333‚Üí   357‚Üí   357‚Üíat higher intensities, the heart rate response levels off (nonlinear) while the
   285‚Üí   334‚Üí   358‚Üí   358‚ÜíO response continues to climb. This does not detract from the value of the
   286‚Üí   335‚Üí   359‚Üí   359‚Üí2
   287‚Üí   336‚Üí   360‚Üí   360‚Üíinformation but instead shows that responses vary.
   288‚Üí   337‚Üí   361‚Üí   361‚ÜíAnother important difference in response patterns is that we see a
   289‚Üí   338‚Üí   362‚Üí   362‚Üídramatic and rapid increase in heart rate at the start of exercise regardless of
   290‚Üí   339‚Üí   363‚Üí   363‚Üíthe intensity. Someone who begins to jog slowly may see a 25- to 40-beat
   291‚Üí   340‚Üí   364‚Üí   364‚Üíincrease in heart rate right away that could go, for example, from 75 to 110
   292‚Üí   341‚Üí   365‚Üí   365‚Üíbpm. This heart rate will settle and may even decrease slightly after 5 to 10
   293‚Üí   342‚Üí   366‚Üí   366‚Üíminutes and eventually remain at 105 bpm. On the other hand, O
   294‚Üí   343‚Üí   367‚Üí   367‚Üí2
   295‚Üí   344‚Üí   368‚Üí   368‚Üíresponds slowly at the start of exercise, and we may see only a small
   296‚Üí   345‚Üí   369‚Üí   369‚Üíchange in the first 5 to 7 minutes that continues at a slow, steady pace, even
   297‚Üí   346‚Üí   370‚Üí   370‚Üíwith abrupt changes in intensity. Heart rate will continue to respond
   298‚Üí   347‚Üí   371‚Üí   371‚Üíabruptly at the lower intensities and then smooth out and change more
   299‚Üí   348‚Üí   372‚Üí   372‚Üíslowly at the higher intensities. It is important to understand this
   300‚Üí   349‚Üí   373‚Üí   373‚Üírelationship because it can help explain aspects of performance such as
   301‚Üí   350‚Üí   374‚Üí   374‚Üíwarm-up and recovery. Let‚Äôs look at the overall relationship of heart rate,
   302‚Üí   351‚Üí   375‚Üí   375‚ÜíO , and intensity in more detail.
