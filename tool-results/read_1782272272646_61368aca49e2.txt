   592→Understanding the Metrics
   593→Not all velocities are created equal. This chapter discusses the three types of
   594→measuring protocols used for implementing VBT. It explains the significance of
   595→each and why one metric may be more suitable than another depending on which
   596→adaptation—strength, speed, or power—an athlete is looking to improve. This
   597→chapter also reviews what eccentric and concentric contractions are, why they
   598→are important, and how they play an integral role in VBT.
   599→
   600→ECCENTRIC AND CONCENTRIC
   601→CONTRACTIONS AND DECELERATION
   602→To understand the different metrics associated with VBT, we must first review
   603→eccentric and concentric muscle contractions as well as deceleration in order to
   604→know why different methods of tracking velocity should be used. The ability to
   605→get in and out of concentric and eccentric muscle contractions quickly and more
   606→efficiently allows athletes to better use the stretch shortening cycle (SSC) to
   607→produce force at fast rates (discussed in greater depth in chapter 6) and
   608→ultimately produce more power. This ability is the gold standard in performance
   609→and is a big part of what separates elite athletes from the rest of the athletic field.
   610→During this discussion, keep in mind that VBT only measures the concentric
   611→portion of the lift or movement. However, it’s vitally important to understand the
   612→eccentric portion because it substantially boosts the acceleration of the
   613→concentric portion when we monitor bar or body speeds. This makes the
   614→eccentric portion of the movement a key performance indicator (KPI) as well
   615→when playing sports.
   616→
   617→Eccentric Contraction
   618→An eccentric muscle contraction is the motion of an active muscle while it is
   619→lengthening under a load. When a muscle contracts eccentrically, it is absorbing
   620→energy (as opposed to a concentric contraction which uses energy). The
   621→lengthening of the muscle also puts the tendons on slack as shown in figure 2.1.
   622→
   623→An eccentric muscle contraction is the motion of an active
   624→muscle while it is lengthening under a load.
   625→During this process, a few things happen—the muscle absorbs energy
   626→developed by the external load in order to support the weight of the body against
   627→gravity, helping absorb shock and reducing the risk of injury. On the
   628→performance side, the storage of this elastic energy helps the muscle to recoil in
   629→preparation for the following concentric contraction (i.e., acceleration). For
   630→example, a countermovement jump improves the eccentric strength of the
   631→muscle and helps the athlete store energy more easily in the low position (see
   632→figure 2.2a), increasing neural drive and switching quickly to the concentric, or
   633→elastic recoil, position, of the jump (see figure 2.2b).
   634→
   635→Figure 2.1 Eccentric muscle contraction.
   636→
   637→In addition, eccentric contractions require far less motor unit activity
   638→(approximately four times less) and consume much less oxygen for a given
   639→muscle movement than concentric contractions. These reductions allow an
   640→eccentric contraction to handle more force (load), with a lower energy
   641→requirement, which may explain why eccentric overload training is essential in
   642→the early stages of a strength training program: It enhances tissue prep and
   643→makes athletes stronger in low positions. Although VBT is not typically used in
   644→
   645→this portion of the lift, eccentric overload training maximizes training in later
   646→stages when heavier loads are used. It also helps athletes better absorb and store
   647→force eccentrically to achieve a higher end result—to produce a more explosive
   648→movement in their sport.

   653→Concentric Contraction
   654→Intention to move a barbell or the body as fast as possible is one of the most
   655→important factors for increasing strength and power, and it relies on neural
   656→adaptations to achieve this. The increase in neural drive allows the athlete to feel
   657→
   658→what true explosiveness is all about. It not only requires a great application of
   659→force during the concentric portion but also involves selecting the correct
   660→exercise to maximize the type of concentric contraction needed (explained in
   661→greater detail in chapter 4’s section on exercise selection). First, let’s briefly
   662→focus on what a concentric contraction actually is.
   663→A concentric contraction uses energy and will result in acceleration of an
   664→object. When a muscle is activated and required to lift a load that is less than the
   665→maximum tension it can generate, the muscle begins to shorten. This is referred
   666→to as a concentric contraction. Two good examples of concentric contractions are
   667→raising the weight during a biceps curl (see figure 2.3a) and coming out of the
   668→bottom of a bench press (see figure 2.3b).
   669→
   670→A concentric contraction uses energy and will result in
   671→acceleration of an object.
   672→
   673→Figure 2.3 (a) Concentric contractions during a biceps curl and (b) a bench press.
   674→
   675→However, the speed at which we perform these concentric movements
   676→
   677→distinguishes whether we are focusing more on strength or power. To increase
   678→power, we must train both force (strength) and velocity (the ability to apply this
   679→strength). This requires using heavier loads (force) and lighter loads (velocity).
   680→During the concentric portion of a movement, the body as a protected device,
   681→must reduce the velocity and force of the concentric portion of the rep to guard
   682→against jerking the tendons or creating undue stress on the joint when coming to
   683→an abrupt stop at the end range of motion. On the other hand, when training the
   684→force side of the power equation, there is little deceleration because we are using
   685→loads greater than 60 percent of 1RM. Due to the heavier load and slower speed,
   686→there is no need for a longer range of deceleration. However, when training with
   687→loads less than 60 percent, a larger portion of this concentric portion must be
   688→used to decelerate the movement in order to protect the body as mentioned.
   689→Therefore, to most efficiently train the velocity side of the equation, athletes use
   690→ballistic exercises such as jumps and throws. Because the body is being thrown
   691→into the air, it does not need to decelerate the load. As a result, higher velocities
   692→can be attained because there is no deceleration.
   693→
   694→Deceleration
   695→During the concentric contraction, we must both accelerate and decelerate the
   696→load. Existing data show that as resistances increase toward 100 percent 1RM,
   697→velocity decreases. This act of purposely slowing down the speed of the load or
   698→movement is a natural reflex in order to avoid joint, tendon, or muscle injuries—
   699→otherwise known as deceleration. Deceleration depends on the intensity of the
   700→load we are lifting and the speed at which we are lifting it.
   701→Here is a brief example of how a lighter load requires greater deceleration and
   702→vice versa. Lighter loads can involve deceleration of up to 50 percent of the
   703→entire concentric contraction. In the bench press, for example, someone using a
   704→light load of 40 to 50 percent 1RM could be only pushing during the first half of
   705→the exercise while the second half of the movement is spent decelerating. This
   706→long deceleration time will have a sizeable impact on the average, or mean,
   707→concentric velocity we receive from our training device. On the other hand, loads
   708→over 65 to 75 percent 1RM should involve minimal or no deceleration because
   709→the load being lifted is not being moved fast enough to affect the average or
   710→mean velocity. There are ways to alter the mechanical profile of an exercise such
   711→as utilizing bands or chains to decrease the decelerative portion of the lift. For
   712→example, when using band resistance, the more stretch the band gets, the more
   713→resistance it will offer. This forces the athlete to continue accelerating toward the
   714→
   715→end of the concentric action in order to overcome that resistance. This, in turn,
   716→minimizes deceleration. These higher loads also mean lower velocities, resulting
   717→in lower power outputs. Therefore, it is vitally important to use the correct type
   718→of exercise and the correct means for measuring the velocity of that exercise.
   719→This brings us to the three metrics we use for determining percentages and
   720→strength zones as well as which method to use for specific exercises.
   721→
   722→Lighter loads can have deceleration of up to 50 percent of the
   723→entire concentric contraction.

   725→METRICS FOR MEASURING
   726→Traditionally, resistance exercise intensity has been shown to be the most vital
   727→factor in producing adaptations of muscular strength. In recent years, however,
   728→another underused factor to consider and measure is movement velocity. When
   729→measuring velocities with VBT, we should first be cognizant of what our training
   730→focus is. Are we training for strength or power? When distinguishing between
   731→strength and power, we must take into consideration both the speed at which the
   732→bar or body is being moved in relation to the load and the amount of deceleration
   733→involved.
   734→
   735→When distinguishing between strength and power, we must take
   736→into consideration both the speed at which the bar or body is
   737→being moved in relation to the load and the amount of
   738→deceleration involved.
   739→One study found that when training for speed or power using loads between
   740→20 and 100 percent 1RM with nonballistic exercises such as your standard bench
   741→press or squat, the relative load at which the braking phase (deceleration) no
   742→longer existed was 76 percent of 1RM (Sanchez-Medina, Perez, and GonzalezBadillo 2010). This tells us that we can get more accurate percentages of a 1RM
   743→using loads above 75 percent because there is more deceleration when using
   744→lighter loads (remember, the body is being protected as it comes to an abrupt
   745→
   746→stop at the end range of motion). In summary, when training for strength, using
   747→loads greater than 60 percent of 1RM increases the time spent accelerating
   748→through a larger range of motion and eliminates much of the deceleration
   749→component found when using lighter loads and higher velocities. Knowing
   750→which training adaptation to look for will help you choose not only the exercise
   751→but also the method of measurement to use, ultimately affecting the accuracy of
   752→calculated speeds while using VBT.

   754→Mean Concentric Velocity
   755→Mean concentric velocity (MCV) is simply the average speed during the entire
   756→concentric portion of the exercise, including the time spent decelerating through
   757→that range of motion. Because strength-based exercises consist of both
   758→acceleration and deceleration phases, the mean concentric velocity metric should
   759→be used (see figure 2.4). This makes MCV a KPI for training absolute strength
   760→exercises such as the squats, deadlifts, and bench presses. It is also the best
   761→single velocity measurement to use at the beginning of the off-season when those
   762→exercises are performed using loads greater than 60 percent 1RM. For sports
   763→where upper-body actions are important and may require more capacity during
   764→the second half of the movement, MCV is the way to go.
   765→

   767→greater than 60 percent 1RM because they consist of both acceleration and less time spent
   768→decelerating (because of the higher load).

   773→
   774→Remember that exercise selection matters. While traditional strength
   775→exercises (squats, bench presses, deadlifts, pull-ups, etc.) are best for training
   776→with medium resistances and higher reps for muscle hypertrophy, or for heavier
   777→resistances and lower reps for neural activation adaptations, the slower velocities
   778→involved with these exercises do not make them, by themselves, ideal choices for
   779→power development. These more explosive power movements can be more
   780→accurately measured through the use of peak concentric velocity (PCV), which is
   781→discussed next.
   782→
   783→Peak Concentric Velocity
   784→A key to continuing to progress and create performance adaptations is increasing
   785→the rate of force development, or the speed at which we are applying this newly
   786→acquired strength. After a good block(s) of strength (greater than 60 percent
   787→1RM) and dynamic strength (40 to 60 percent 1RM) are attained, higher speeds
   788→—depending on what is needed for the sport—can and should be used. This is
   789→generally programmed in the final four-to-eight–week block (preseason period)
   790→when PCV can be used. This means that the method of measurement will change
   791→and the exercise selection will likely change as well.
   792→
   793→PCV is simply the peak speed during the concentric portion of
   794→the exercise, and it is usually calculated every 5 to 10
   795→milliseconds.
   796→PCV is simply the peak speed during the concentric portion of the exercise,
   797→and it is usually calculated every 5 to 10 milliseconds. This metric is used for
   798→ballistic or power-based exercises. Ballistic exercises are movements where
   799→force is produced for a very short amount of time before the implement or body
   800→is projected into the air. During a large portion of these ballistic movements, the
   801→athlete is not actually applying force to the bar, so using an average of the entire
   802→movement is less efficient because no deceleration is produced from trying to
   803→lock out the rep (see figure 2.5). Exercises such as power cleans, snatches, bench
   804→press throws, and jump squats are generally used in this period. Because of the
   805→ballistic nature of these exercises, it is important to remember that, although
   806→MCV is still usable, it would not be as accurate or efficient, and therefore, PCV
   807→may be a better option.
   808→
   809→Mean Propulsive Velocity
   810→Mean propulsive velocity (MPV) is a bit more involved, but it is a great method
   811→for measuring and assessing true muscular power. Unfortunately, MPV is not
   812→available on many wearable devices such as linear transducers. Nonetheless, we
   813→need to understand the importance of MPV to be able to apply it when devices
   814→such as Tendo and GymAware units are available.
   815→While MCV refers to and measures the entire upward portion of a lift
   816→(including the braking phase), its accuracy begins to get compromised at or less
   817→than 60 percent of 1RM. MPV refers to and measures only the portion of the
   818→upward movement during which the measured acceleration is greater than the
   819→acceleration related to gravity. In other words, it happens before deceleration
   820→occurs.

   823→
   824→MPV may be a more valid measurement later in the off-season for both
   825→traditional exercises when dynamic strength (loads between 40 to 60 percent
   826→1RM are being used, and greater deceleration occurs due to the necessity to
   827→brake at the end of the range of motion) and heavier ballistic jumps are
   828→performed at or around 40 to 50 percent 1RM when the deceleration is not
   829→significant because the upward phase occurs only after the implement has either
   830→left the hands or the body has left the ground (i.e., takeoff).
   831→
   832→Mean propulsive velocity refers to and measures only the
   833→portion of the upward movement during which the measured
   834→
   835→acceleration is greater than the acceleration related to gravity.
   836→Based on this mechanical principle, the use of MCV related to the entire
   837→upward portion of a given movement may be more biased and a less effective
   838→means of measurement than the use of MPV with lighter loads. A training
   839→system based on MPV may be a better option for measuring performance at both
   840→ends of the force–velocity curve, whether it be a jump or maximum dynamic
   841→strength ability, such as a 1RM for a back squat.
   842→This information suggests that, by referring the MCVs to the propulsive
   843→phase, we can avoid underestimating an athlete’s true neuromuscular potential
   844→when lifting light and medium loads while training for power simply by taking
   845→out the measurement of the deceleration phase. Therefore, many coaches and
   846→athletes believe that using MPV in training and testing settings allows for a more
   847→effective method of measuring training for strength or power than MCV.
   848→Velocity is a useful parameter to measure during resistance training. However,
   849→it is imperative to use the correct measuring protocol to factor in the amount of
   850→time spent in the deceleration phase of the movement. Mean (average) velocity,
   851→especially with resistances more than 60 percent 1RM, correlates well with
   852→strength exercises such as squats, deadlifts, and bench presses and can be used
   853→when training strength as well as for determining 1RMs. Dynamic strength and
   854→power adaptations, on the other hand, typically occur using the same exercise at
   855→more moderate resistances (approximately 40 to 60 percent) and require more
   856→deceleration. This makes mean propulsive velocities a more efficient method
   857→because the decelerated portion of the lift is eliminated when calculating average
   858→speeds. Finally, more ballistic types of exercises, such as cleans, snatches,
   859→weighted jumps, and throws, are used in training for explosiveness.
   860→Peak power may be a better option to calculate the fastest 5 to 10 meters per
   861→second—the closest to in-game speeds—of these lifts or jumps, making them
   862→great options during preseason training (see figure 2.6). In a study by SanchezMedina et al. (2010), the relative load that maximized mechanical power output
   863→was determined using three different parameters: mean concentric velocity and
   864→power, mean power of the concentric portion, and peak power. The load at which
   865→the braking phase no longer existed was (regardless of the method used) 76.1
   866→percent (+/− 7.4) of 1RM. This is more than likely due to having to apply force
   867→for a longer period of time because of the heavier load. Maximum mechanical
   868→power output was dependent on the parameter used, making it imperative to
   869→
   870→select the appropriate parameter according to the type of exercise being done as
   871→well as the load being used.

   920→Tools of the Trade
   921→Not long ago, the linear position transducer (LPT) was the only way to measure
   922→the velocity of the body or a bar. Now, with the addition of accelerometers,
   923→coaches and trainers have various ways to monitor velocity and power output.
   924→No matter the tool you choose, all calculate velocity by measuring the rate of
   925→change in the body’s or bar’s position over time. Because each method measures
   926→velocity a little differently, it is imperative to perform a velocity profile before
   927→you begin training. By doing so, we are using the athlete’s specific means of
   928→measurement in order to keep data consistent. (Velocity profiling is discussed in
   929→upcoming chapters.)
   930→
   931→OVERVIEW OF VBT MEASURING TOOLS
   932→Some coaches may prefer one type of measuring tool over another, but the
   933→bottom line is each kind measures velocity by a different means. This section
   934→discusses what makes these devices different from each other and the pros and
   935→cons to consider when choosing the tool that may be most practical for you.
   936→
   937→Linear Position Transducers
   938→LPTs such as GymAware or Tendo units are devices capable of measuring
   939→displacement in a linear plane (see figure 3.1). An LPT consists of a hardwired
   940→measuring cable—otherwise known as a tether, spool, or spring—and a sensor,
   941→such as a potentiometer or rotary encoder. The sensor converts the change in
   942→distance of the cable to voltage and, ultimately, velocity and acceleration. The
   943→LPT also calculates average force using the summed mass of the barbell and the
   944→mass of the lifter and multiplying by acceleration due to gravity (g = 9.81 m × s
   945→
   946→– 2). The average power may be calculated for each movement using the average
   947→force and average velocity. Using a hardwired cable makes it easy to transition
   948→from exercise to exercise without having to restart the device whenever changing
   949→angles within the exercise selection. The advantage of using an encoder is simple
   950→to see: The data is always a direct measure rather than a calculated estimate.
   951→
   952→Figure 3.1 (a) The GymAware and (b) Tendo unit are the two main LPTs.
   953→(a) Courtesy of GymAware; (b) Courtesy of Tendo Sport
   954→
   955→In an attempt to validate LPT technology, a study was conducted using an
   956→LPT that used three jumping conditions (squats, countermovements, and drops)
   957→and compared the force plate’s values of average force, peak force, and time–
   958→peak force to the LPT’s values. The study concluded that LPT technology is a
   959→useful and accurate tool for measuring movement velocity (Cronin, Hing, and
   960→McNair 2004). In another study that also examined the reliability of
   961→measurement between the force plate and LPT technology, each subject
   962→completed two testing sessions that were separated by one week and included
   963→three rebound jump squats with an 88-pound (40 kg) external load. Again, the
   964→force plate and the LPT were both found to be reliable in the measurement of
   965→peak force (Hansen, Cronin, and Newton 2011).
   966→While the price of Tendo units and GymAware might not be considered as
   967→expensive as other measuring equipment, such as force plates, they do present
   968→some budgetary concerns for many training facilities, as well as strength and
   969→
   970→conditioning coaches and personal trainers. Coaches who train athletes either
   971→individually, or small in groups, might want to consider different options for
   972→their day-to-day monitoring purposes. For this reason, all VBT examples shown
   973→will use an accelerometer because it is currently the most practical application
   974→used in the United States.
   975→
   976→Accelerometers
   977→More recently accelerometers, such as the PUSH Band (see figure 3.2) and Bar
   978→Sensei, which are able to accurately calculate velocity, have been making their
   979→way into facilities much like mine. Accelerometers differ from LPTs because
   980→they use an algorithm to determine where the body or bar is being positioned and
   981→when each movement starts and stops. This algorithm adjusts according to the
   982→exercise being performed; for example, with jumps, it adjusts through the change
   983→in angles when a person moves from a vertical position to a wide position.
   984→Accelerometers do not have the hardwired cable to calculate angles; instead,
   985→they are preprogrammed to a built-in specific exercise suite. This feature works,
   986→but its readings can be slightly more skewed than those of an LPT.
   987→
   988→Figure 3.2 The PUSH Band is one of a number of accelerometers currently available on the
   989→market.
   990→
   991→Accelerometers are great for measuring peak velocity (PV), but they are
   992→inferior at detecting where they are in time and space. They are usually included
   993→
   994→in other systems to assist the calculations, but again, the algorithms are usually a
   995→step behind the technology. Small, subtle idiosyncrasies, such as gripping the
   996→barbell and small micromovements, will throw off calculations with
   997→accelerometers’ algorithms.
   998→Another disadvantage of accelerometers is they currently lack the capability
   999→to measure force (in real time) of rep-to-rep sets, which, as we know, is the other
  1000→half of the power equation. These units only measure velocity. While some
  1001→companies provide access to force readings via a company portal, there are
  1002→downsides to this. Users are required to purchase expensive yearly subscriptions,
  1003→and the readings are not immediate. There are, however, many benefits of using
  1004→an accelerometer in a group setting. First and foremost is affordability:
  1005→Accelerometers have a much lower price point than LPTs. Despite being slightly
  1006→less accurate at pinpointing specific velocities, in a team setting where wider
  1007→ranges are often used, accelerometers work just fine. Other benefits worth
  1008→pointing out are that accelerometers are designed to be worn on the body or on
  1009→the bar, do not require a hardwired cable, can be used anywhere, and require
  1010→much less space in the weight room. I have about 15 of them in my facility and
  1011→have been getting great results for years. I do, however, also have one LPT when
  1012→individual profiles are necessary.
  1013→While LPTs and accelerometers both show great accuracy and testing when
  1014→compared to a 1RM, LPT technology in the Tendo unit and GymAware still
  1015→seems to be the gold standard and the more valid and reliable way to measure

  1052→
  1053→This section provides a brief overview of the basic setup for using both an LPT
  1054→and accelerometer. This is not a how-to instruction manual; it is merely a
  1055→glimpse of the step-by-step procedure from attaching the devices to reading the
  1056→information. The setup is slightly different for each device.
  1057→As with any device that incorporates the use of data, the best place to begin is
  1058→by simply starting to use the device. I recommend getting used to the feedback
  1059→of the unit by reading velocities from rep to rep and seeing if they improve or
  1060→worsen. Even in these initial stages of learning to use and apply VBT, you will
  1061→begin to see results.
  1062→As discussed in the previous section, many different types of technology on
  1063→the market are used for measuring VBT. Refer back to table 3.1 for a few of the
  1064→more popular and most dependable (in my opinion) options for LPTs and

  1067→Basic Setup for LPTs
  1068→For LTPs, such as the GymAware and Tendo, a thin cable spool attaches to the
  1069→barbell to measure distance and speed of the bar. The sensors sample not only
  1070→the rate and distance the cable line is traveling but also the angle. Data can be
  1071→stored locally on the device or through an online portal in the cloud. The basic
  1072→setup is as follows:
  1073→1. Set up the device.
  1074→
  1075→This step is easy and self-explanatory. Some coaches get
  1076→concerned with the reliability when bar whip associated with the
  1077→Olympic lifts causes cable slack. While every unit will have
  1078→some issues with readings, absolute difference is so low that it
  1079→should be of little concern. Most of the bar whip comes from
  1080→very heavy competitive loads, not the typical ones used in sports
  1081→training.
  1082→2. Pair the device to an iPad.
  1083→
  1084→The first step before pairing anything is to ensure that the Wi-Fi
  1085→is strong and secure. If you train in a basement or an
  1086→underground weight room, test the saturation of the Wi-Fi
  1087→connection to each iPad and lock it down.
  1088→3. Place the iPad in a convenient and safe location.
  1089→
  1090→For the best functionality, iPads should be placed where athletes
  1091→can get feedback conveniently but out of the way of weights.
  1092→Make sure to use a mounting option that allows the tablet to be
  1093→mobile. Each unit pairs within a specific iPad, but the data are
  1094→universal, so, technically, any athlete can train off of any iPad or
  1095→station.
  1096→4. Synchronize with the unit’s portal through the cloud (optional).
  1097→
  1098→Most of the time, I record data locally (stored on the device);
  1099→however, there is a great option for collecting data for teams to
  1100→use. After each iPad is securely connected to Wi-Fi, connect the
  1101→associated app to the cloud to access a user portal. Connecting is
  1102→very simple and only requires a username and password. After
  1103→that, the rest is basically done for you.
  1104→5. Set target zones and timers for workflow (optional).
  1105→
  1106→These are great options that only LPTs offer. Refer to the user
  1107→manual for more information on how set up these functions.
  1108→6. Select your exercise.
  1109→
  1110→To get the most accurate readings, all VBT devices come with a
  1111→library of exercises; however, most research and information
  1112→refer to the big lifts (squats; d-lifts; and bench and body-weight
  1113→exercises such as countermovement jumps, push-ups, and pullups).
  1114→7. Select your load and start your set.
  1115→
  1116→Get into the habit of pushing every rep with maximal voluntary
  1117→concentric (MVC) action. This just means pushing every single
  1118→velocity measured rep as hard and as fast as you are physically
  1119→capable. Doing so ensures readings are as accurate as possible.
  1120→Slowing down concentric muscle action greatly skews the
  1121→readings of the velocity measuring technology.
