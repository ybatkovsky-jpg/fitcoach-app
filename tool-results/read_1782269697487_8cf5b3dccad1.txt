    15→objective of maximizing performance, minimizing injury, optimizing rest,
    16→and ensuring adequate recovery during exercise sessions such as interval
    17→workouts. Collectively, this allows for better overall progress and avoidance
    18→of cases of overtraining that so easily lead to injuries and illnesses.
    19→Of the many benefits of 24-hour monitoring, here are four:
    20→1. It allows an athlete to gauge the overall stress load outside of the
    21→exercise routine. Often an athlete’s daily routine can add significantly
    22→to his or her daily exertion, necessitating nutritional or recovery
    23→intervention.

    26→4. It gives more real-time feedback about the body’s response to an

    39→Four Components of Physical
    40→Fitness
    41→The process of getting in shape involves the four components of fitness—
    42→endurance, stamina, economy, and speed—and these are developed in
    43→phases. This is also their order of progression as you train. Figure 1.1, the
    44→basic model for training, shows these components schematically. Each is
    45→developed at a specific intensity, and in the early stages of training is
    46→optimally developed within a very specific intensity range. Exercising
    47→below the target intensity zones will not deliver the stimulus required to get
    48→in better shape. Exercising above the intensity range will result in
    49→maladaptations such as increased risk of injury, premature peaking, or
    50→staleness from overtraining, all of which result in poor performance. Each
    51→component has a zone with upper and lower limits. Heart rate is the
    52→simplest and most effective way to monitor intensity and therefore ensure
    53→training in the correct zone of effort.

    56→Here, we are trying to carefully mix both lay language and lab language
    57→to get our concepts across. We will use the terms aerobic and anaerobic
    58→quite freely to relate to these components of fitness. Oxygen is the common
    59→currency of both the circulatory and respiratory systems, and while we don’t
    60→count respiratory rates, these descriptions of your breathing will associate
    61→well with aerobic and anaerobic work. The breathing involved with aerobic
    62→work is so verbal it is like reading aloud one of Faulkner’s endless run-on
    63→sentences that may last a whole page before coming to a period. The
    64→breathing involved in anaerobic threshold work is less verbal, more like a
    65→classic Hemingway sentence of just several trenchant words. The breathing
    66→involved in anaerobic work is almost wordless with listeners hearing only
    67→very heavy, fast panting. Review the following definitions and explanations
    68→of these key components of fitness.
    69→Endurance (Phase I)
    70→Endurance is our first phase of training, and endurance is the ability of a
    71→muscle to repeat a movement aerobically, no matter how much it slows
    72→--- PAGE 21 ---
    73→down, until it runs out of fuel. Jogging for fun and fitness and long runs for
    74→a marathon are classic examples of endurance development exercises. To
    75→develop the endurance for a marathon, for example, requires lengthening
    76→each of the weekly long runs by a couple of miles. As runners reach their
    77→current level of endurance, they approach the point where fuel supplies of
    78→glycogen run low. To meet today’s mileage goal, they slow down to finally
    79→make it to the end of the workout. Endurance improves as muscles learn
    80→how to improve their metabolism of fat supplies. Simply put, it’s going
    81→from point A to point B no matter how much you have to slow down to
    82→lower levels of intensity. In general, basic aerobic endurance is developed at
    83→heart rates of less than 75 percent of the maximum heart rate (MHR). Think
    84→of this phase of conditioning as getting into shape. If you wish to jog to
    85→simply stay healthy, this is as hard as you have to work.
    86→Stamina (Phase II)
    87→Stamina is the second phase of training, and stamina is the ability of a
    88→muscle to repeat the movement at faster rates without exceeding your
    89→anaerobic threshold. Think of it as the ability to go at this faster pace, but
    90→over a shorter point A to point B distance. The objective of stamina training
    91→is to improve your tolerance of oxygen debts by minimizing the use of
    92→carbohydrate fuels (glycogen and glucose) while maximizing the use of
    93→fatty fuel. Consider this phase of conditioning as getting ready to race. The
    94→fitness emphasis in this phase is on preparing the cardiovascular and
    95→respiratory systems to work hard without overreaching. Steady-state
    96→workouts at 75 to 80 percent MHR for 45 to 60 minutes are good examples
    97→of stamina workouts. Tempo workouts at 80 to 85 percent for 20 to 30
    98→minutes are great for improving stamina to a slightly higher level. If you are
    99→a recreational athlete who wants to participate in events and finish smiling,
   100→this is as hard as you need to work. In general, stamina is developed in heart
   101→rate zones of 75 to 85 percent MHR.
   102→Economy (Phase III)
   103→Economy is our third phase of training, and economy is the ability of the
   104→muscle to repeat movements at an even faster rate, albeit for a shorter time,
   105→above its anaerobic threshold. Economy training develops the ability to
   106→--- PAGE 22 ---
   107→handle race paces while tolerating increasing levels of oxygen debt, which
   108→leads to the accumulation of lactic acid in the muscles. Economy workouts
   109→train the muscle cells how to optimize the use of oxygen and carbohydrate
   110→fuel at this higher level of effort. Think of this phase of conditioning as
   111→improving your racing fitness by adding more horsepower to your engine
   112→through workouts such as interval training, hill sprints, and fartlek running.
   113→(Fartlek, which literally means “speed play,” is a Swedish system of
   114→conditioning that features frequent changes in speed.) Good examples of
   115→economy workouts are those at moderate to high intensity for continuous
   116→tempo paces, or interval workouts featuring longer repetitions. If you wish
   117→to compete in athletic events, working this hard has to be included in your
   118→training program. In general, economy is developed in heart rate zones of
   119→85 to 95 percent MHR.
   120→Speed (Phase IV)
   121→Speed is our fourth and final phase of training, and speed is the ability of a
   122→muscle to repeat the movement at its maximum rate at full power under
   123→anaerobic conditions. It is the ability to go at top effort for short periods of
   124→time and to stay relaxed while tolerating increasing levels of lactic acid in
   125→the muscle tissue. The power training in this phase also will bring about
   126→final improvements in strength, flexibility, and coordination. Uphill sprints
   127→and interval workouts of shorter, faster, and maximum-intensity repeats
   128→with long and full recoveries are the best examples of speed workouts.
   129→Some might describe this wish to compete at full capacity as joining the
   130→P.T.A. (pain, torture, and agony) Society as you learn how to withstand the
   131→maximal exertion. In general, speed is developed in heart rate zones of 95 to
   132→100 percent MHR and often at speeds in excess of race pace.
   133→We fully recognize that these are not exactly dictionary definitions or
   134→standard laboratory usage; however, they are commonly used in the popular
   135→literature when discussing exercise and fitness. For the purpose of this
   136→book, we feel that they are useful in understanding the proper heart rate
   137→ranges so you can safely progress up the training triangle all the way to
   138→championship shape, depending on your goal. We start by identifying these
   139→concepts because they tie in well with the concept of oxygen use, which, of
   140→course, is conveniently estimated (albeit indirectly) by your heart rate.
   141→--- PAGE 23 ---
   142→Figure 1.1 on page 5 is our version of the classic training triangle and offers
   143→a graphic representation of this approach.
   144→Activities in the lower, easier zone result in specific physiological and
   145→biomechanical adaptations, whereas those in the upper, harder zone have
   146→their own important, yet different, biochemical and neurological
   147→adaptations. Developing speed requires fairly sophisticated training
   148→methods at even narrower effort zones within the anaerobic section of the
   149→triangle. These will be discussed in more detail in later chapters.

   152→Consider the categories in table 1.1 with open-mindedness because the
   153→zones range as wide as 15 percent. The reason for this is that, in our

   156→might be closer to the 70 to 75 percent mark; whereas when swimming, you
   157→may be closer to the 60 to 65 percent mark. When you become well trained,

   161→activity. Therefore, you need a true maximum heart rate for each activity,
   162→especially if you’re a triathlete. We will talk more about this in later

   164→Personal Considerations
   165→--- PAGE 24 ---
   166→Essentially, all people are made from the same parts. However, important
   167→differences, such as training objectives and genetics, affect their responses
   168→and adaptations to exercise.
   169→Do you want to improve your cardiovascular health and control your
   170→weight? If so, then we suggest that you exercise frequently and extensively
   171→at the very low levels of aerobic endurance effort, but don’t expect dramatic
   172→short-term gains. You need to make exercise a lifestyle habit.
   173→Does simply participating (but not really competing) in a 10K road race
   174→with the goal of just finishing with a smile seem like a good way to enjoy
   175→social recreation? If so, then for several months to a year, patiently increase
   176→the volume of your endurance training, but substitute a couple days of
   177→training in the stamina zone of the triangle.
   178→Do you want to compete and work hard at improving and setting
   179→personal records (PRs)? If so, you need to add at least a day per week of
   180→high-intensity, anaerobic economy or speed workouts at the very difficult
   181→and mentally challenging level.
   182→The preceding are general ideas to get you thinking about your training
   183→goals. Your reasons will affect your target heart rate choices. Your responses
   184→and adaptations, however, will also be affected by something else: your
   185→genetic makeup.
   186→Everyone understands that an easy effort results in a low heart rate and a
   187→harder effort raises it. Although this is true for the most part, the
   188→relationship is not uniformly predictable, especially in terms of absolute
   189→numbers. What this means is that two people exercising at the same
   190→absolute heart rate (145 beats per minute [bpm], for example) may have
   191→very different comfort levels and different physiological responses. Genetic
   192→differences such as the composition of fast- and slow-twitch muscle fibers
   193→can result in puzzling heart rate responses. Fast-twitch fibers (think
   194→sprinters) have different oxygen consumption rates than slow-twitch fibers
   195→do (think marathoners). Another factor that can result in widely different
   196→heart rates in two people of similar ability and fitness level is the anatomy
   197→and size of the heart. These differences may result in heart rates that differ
   198→from 35 to 70 bpm in people running at the same pace. Target heart rates are
   199→a lot like salaries—it’s best not to discuss them. Having a higher heart rate
   200→than someone else does not mean that you are less fit.
   201→--- PAGE 25 ---
   202→Your personal training goals and genetics will affect your responses to heart rate
   203→training.
   204→One last genetic issue to point out is significant, but far less remarkable
   205→—the difference in the heart size of men and women. This difference is
   206→revealed by the average resting heart rates of 72 bpm for men versus 84
   207→bpm for women. This book will help you monitor your training by showing
   208→you how to measure your cardiac response to your program designed

   210→Rate of Perceived Exertion: Your
   211→Backup System
   212→Individualizing workouts assumes that you understand the difference
   213→between hard and easy effort. (The former makes you grimace and gasp for
   214→air, whereas the latter allows you to laugh and talk all the way home.) Each
   215→of us can make subjective distinctions between hard and easy tasks. Before
   216→telemetric heart rate monitors made counting pulses so convenient, the rate
   217→of perceived exertion (RPE), as illustrated by the Borg Scale, was the
   218→standard for using one’s own capacity for subjective measurement of hard
   219→and easy exercise effort. Dr. Borg’s 15-point scale could be used, for
   220→example, to describe how average people might feel as they progressed
   221→from walking a mile to trying to run it in 4 minutes. The scale described
   222→resting or walking as a very, very light effort, whereas exhaustion is
   223→--- PAGE 26 ---
   224→described as beyond very, very hard. In addition, the Borg Scale also
   225→introduced a value system of points ranging from 6 (resting) to 20
   226→(exhaustion). In short, Borg tried to correlate quantitative measures with his
   227→qualitative system.
   228→Although the Borg Scale is useful, it is an arbitrary system because it
   229→offers no objective way to measure exertion. For the competitive athlete, a
   230→heart rate monitor is a better way to quantify those measurements.
   231→Probably the most important reason for individualizing training is the
   232→fact that people have different workout objectives. What exactly, as a
   233→percentage of your maximum, are your target heart rate zones? The need to
   234→calculate these zones is the challenge that brought us to share our collective
   235→wisdom with you. So, there are plenty of reasons for using the information
   236→in this book to help you use your heart rate monitor intelligently. Your body
   237→is not like anyone else’s, so why work out like anyone else? Whatever your
   238→unique genetic gifts, current level of fitness, or goals are, this book will help
   239→you determine your target heart rates and individualize your training.
   240→Monitoring the heart rate of members of soccer, rugby, football, and
   241→lacrosse teams is growing as well as in athletes in other nonaerobic settings
   242→such as wrestling, weightlifting, and boxing. However, because of the start-
   243→and-stop nature of these sports, the use of heart rate to monitor intensity is
   244→somewhat limited; what it actually provides is information about energy
   245→expenditure and recovery rather than overall intensity. This doesn’t mean
   246→that you cannot use heart rate if you participate in these sports. Like aerobic
   247→athletes, you can use heart rate to monitor your training status and intensity
   248→during the aerobic training portion of your conditioning program or with
   249→interval training (a conditioning system that consists of repeated efforts over
   250→a time shorter than the length of the competition and done at fairly high
   251→intensities of 85 to 95 percent MHR), during which knowing the recovery
   252→heart rate is valuable. It is also a useful recovery tool.
   253→Using heart rate during higher-intensity anaerobic activity involves
   254→other, more complex physiological responses. Toward the end of the book,
   255→we provide more advice for using heart rate in the nonaerobic setting, but
   256→for now our focus is on the typical endurance athlete—the runner, cyclist,
   257→rower, cross-country skier, or triathlete.

   260→Heart rate provides a lot of information, but you must have an accurate
   261→monitor that provides reliable data. Accurate data will allow you to evaluate
   262→your own responses, adaptations, energy expenditure, training programs,
   263→and a lot more. The point to keep in mind is that you are embarking on a
   264→quest for knowledge that is highly individualized and therefore more likely
   265→to help you yield the results you want. Here is the information you can learn
   266→from heart rate monitoring, provided you have an accurate heart rate

   268→Correct intensity for aerobic system development
   269→Correct intensity for anaerobic system development
   270→Correct durations for time spent in appropriate training zones
   271→Appropriate recovery periods during interval training
   272→Appropriate recovery periods between exercise sessions
   273→Effective evaluation of adaptations to training programs
   274→Early warning signs of overtraining
   275→Early indications of heat stress
   276→Early indications of energy depletion
   277→Race pace strategy for longer competitions

   279→worthwhile (and these are only some of the benefits!).
   280→Understanding Heart Rate
   281→The simple beauty of monitoring heart rate is that it is based on your own
   282→heart’s capacity and nobody else’s. The heart is a muscle, and it responds in
   283→much the same way as any other muscle by getting bigger and stronger as
   284→you work out. When you are not working out, the heart continues to pump
   285→blood into your muscles to feed their repair and recovery. That’s why heart
   286→rate can inform you indirectly about the state of recovery of your muscles.
   287→If you still have microtears or are replacing fuel, your metabolism will be
   288→elevated, and your heart rate will reflect that by being slightly elevated.
   289→That’s why monitoring and recording your heart rate each morning when
   290→--- PAGE 28 ---
   291→you wake up will give you an idea of whether you’re still under a condition
   292→of repair from a previous workout. This is why your morning resting heart
   293→rate is one of the two most important numbers. Your maximum heart rate
   294→(MHR) is the other. Maximum heart rate is the fastest or highest number of
   295→times the heart can beat in a minute.
   296→Maximum heart rate doesn’t really change as a result of training, but all
   297→your training zones are calculated from that number. Therefore, you need an
   298→accurate maximum heart rate (see the Determining Maximum Heart Rate
   299→section in chapter 2). Resting heart rate, on the other hand, does change
   300→with training and generally decreases with increasing fitness. At times,
   301→resting heart rate can increase, which usually indicates fatigue, overtraining,
   302→or sickness. Charting your resting heart rate can help identify these
   303→conditions early.
   304→Take a look at the resting heart rate response of a runner over a one-
   305→month period depicted in figure 1.2. Notice what happens on the days
   306→following a high-intensity sprint workout or a long run. Each time the
   307→athlete performed a hard session or a long run, the resting heart rate was
   308→elevated on the following day. This information is useful because it can help
   309→the athlete adjust his or her workouts when he or she is not fully recovered
   310→and possibly prevent overtraining and injury. This is an example of how
   311→heart rate monitoring can help plan recovery, but it is equally useful in
   312→determining exercise intensity.

   660→Facts and Myths About Heart Rate
   661→Numbers
   662→What do heart rate numbers mean? Before interpreting the numbers, we’d
   663→like to share our experience in understanding heart rate responses to
   664→exercise. This is one of the most important benefits of this book because
   665→this information is seldom included in the instruction manuals that come
   666→with heart rate monitors. Knowing some basic truths and common
   667→--- PAGE 42 ---
   668→misunderstandings can help you avoid getting false feedback from your
   669→monitor. To see how well you understand heart rate, read each statement
   670→and determine if you believe it is true or false.
   671→1. The popular formula of subtracting your age from 220 bpm for
   672→predicting MHR is reliable for everyone.
   673→2. Treadmill or bike testing for MHR is not a learned skill and takes no
   674→experience.
   675→3. Treadmill or bike cardiac stress tests administered in physicians’
   676→offices often fall far short of revealing actual true MHR.
   677→4. A heart rate number is a heart rate number. In other words, once
   678→you’ve got a heart rate training zone for any sport, you can apply it
   679→across a range of sports.
   680→All in all, what you see on a heart rate monitor will be reliable and valid.
   681→But too many people who have spent money on monitors bury them in the
   682→back of their sock drawer and don’t use them because they have become
   683→frustrated by irrational numbers. So, let’s see how you did on the quiz.
   684→Myth 1: Predicting Maximum Heart Rate
   685→The idea that anyone can subtract his or her age from 220 bpm to predict a
   686→reliable MHR is a myth. That is only helpful for 65 percent of the
   687→population. Here’s a real-life example from Bob Johnson, who doesn’t
   688→realize that he has an exceptional heart. When he bought a monitor at age
   689→55, he had no idea that his MHR was way above predicted average. By
   690→using the age-adjusted formula suggested in the instructions that came with
   691→the monitor, he calculated his MHR to be 165. Using the standard target
   692→heart rate zone of 60 to 70 percent for an easy aerobic workout, he set his
   693→receiver to beep when he was outside the zone of 99 to 115 bpm. Bob went
   694→out for a jog and within minutes was exceeding 115 bpm. He slowed down
   695→to keep the alarm from sounding. In another few minutes, he was reduced to
   696→walking and then to a crawl to keep his monitor quiet. This was not the
   697→monitor’s fault. It was merely doing its job by warning him that he was out
   698→of his target zone. Bob knew that he was not in bad shape for his age, but
   699→this was shocking. After a few more workouts, Bob’s puzzlement over his
   700→new purchase was growing into a good case of frustration.
   701→--- PAGE 43 ---
   702→What was wrong? Bob didn’t realize that he had a smaller-than-average
   703→heart and that it could compensate by being able to reach a much higher
   704→MHR. In fact, his actual MHR turned out to be an even 200 bpm, 35 beats
   705→higher than predicted by the conventional formula.
   706→So what’s the story? Let’s start with this fact: The average MHR of a
   707→brand-new baby, who has a heart the size of a walnut, is 220 bpm. And we
   708→know that as a person grows up, the heart also grows bigger. At adulthood,
   709→the heart is about the size of the person’s fist. Naturally, it has more
   710→capacity for holding blood in its chambers. This means that more blood is
   711→pumped with each stroke (greater stroke volume), and hence, fewer beats
   712→are required. By age 20, growth of the heart brings the average MHR down
   713→to about 195 bpm. From that age, the aging process causes a further decline
   714→at the convenient rate of about one beat per year. This explains the logic of
   715→the formula 220 minus age equals predicted MHR.
   716→Now here is the key to understanding why Bob could hit 200 at age 55.
   717→Maximum heart rates are naturally spread across the standard distribution of
   718→a bell-shaped curve. As with intelligence, there is a wide range from one
   719→end of the curve to the other for people of the same age. On each of the plus
   720→and minus sides of the mean is a standard distribution of MHR of 12 bpm.
   721→This distribution means that only those at the mean can reliably predict
   722→MHR by using the age-adjusted formulas. Depending on where you fall on
   723→the curve, your MHR may be as high as 36 bpm above or below what the
   724→formula predicts.
   725→Trust us on this one. Long before telemetric heart rate monitors, when
   726→we used our fingers to palpate carotid arteries (the jugular vein in the neck),
   727→we counted maximum heart rates as high as 200 and as low as 140 bpm for
   728→50-year-olds. People with high maximum heart rates simply have smaller
   729→but much quicker hearts. Some young adults whose hearts are as big as
   730→grapefruits have maximum heart rates of 140 to 160 bpm at truly drop-dead
   731→levels of fatigue, and this is normal. Admittedly, these examples are few and
   732→far between, but they do exist, and maybe you’re one of them. The bottom
   733→line: You need an accurate measure of maximum heart rate.
   734→Myth 2: Enduring Fitness Testing
   735→--- PAGE 44 ---
   736→It’s true that someone not experienced with exhaustive levels of athletic
   737→effort might not do well on a first max treadmill stress test. So the idea that
   738→treadmill or bike testing for MHR is not a learned skill and takes no
   739→experience is a myth. People often bail out long before they reach their true
   740→maximum values because they don’t know how to endure the pain of full
   741→fatigue or they simply are unfamiliar with this level of effort and are
   742→uncomfortable with it. True MHR is not reached until the beats per minute
   743→plateau and refuse to rise any higher no matter how much harder, faster, or
   744→longer the subject moves. Most rookies have to survive several close
   745→brushes with exhaustion before they realize how hard they can push
   746→themselves and what levels of fatigue they can endure and live to tell about.
   747→If you have suspicions about your MHR, keep trying by repeating the tests
   748→we recommend, or checking your monitor at the end of really hard
   749→workouts or races.
