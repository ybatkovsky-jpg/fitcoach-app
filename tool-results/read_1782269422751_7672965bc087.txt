    33→    33→Basic Principles of Metabolic Training
    34→    34→The following principles of exercise are particularly applicable to metabolic train-
    35→    35→ing and should always be considered when designing and implementing metabolic-
    36→    36→training programs. Adhering to these principles will optimize training results.
    37→    37→Measures such as heart rate and rating of perceived exertion (RPE) can be used
    38→    38→to objectively evaluate how the body responds to the exercise stimuli associated
    39→    39→with these training approaches.
    40→    40→1. Overload. The principle of overload refers to the application of a greater
    41→    41→amount of stress than an individual has previously experienced. Increased
    42→    42→intensity, duration, frequency volume, as well as new types of exercise, can
    43→    43→all overload an athlete. For instance, adding a fourth day of running per
    44→    44→week from three days is an overload of training.

    47→    47→2.Specificity. According to the principle of specificity, a specific method of train-
    48→    48→ing produces a specific outcome. As an example, someone who consistently
    49→    49→runs long distances will adapt physiologically to long-distance running.
    50→    50→3.FITT. FITT stands for “frequency, intensity, time, and type. ”Frequency refers
    51→    51→to how often you exercise. Intensity is the effort exerted to perform a specific
    52→    52→exercise. Time is the duration of an exercise session. Type indicates the kind
    53→    53→of exercise (cardiorespiratory, strength, or a combination of the two, as well
    54→    54→as the exercise modality).
    55→    55→4.Rest/recovery. Rest or recovery describes breaks taken during an exercise
    56→    56→session—between exercises or between exercise sets—as well as between
    57→    57→one exercise session and the next.
    58→    58→Training intensity can be evaluated using heart-rate monitoring. The higher
    59→    59→the heart rate, the more intense the training, and vice versa. You should know
    60→    60→your resting heart rate and your average heart rate during light to heavy (or very
    61→    61→heavy) exercise. Maximal heart rates can be reached during metabolic training.
    62→    62→It is important to understand that each individual has his own maximal heart
    63→    63→rate (and associated training intensities), and a higher heart rate during the train-
    64→    64→ing session does not mean a greater effort or more intense training. World-class
    65→    65→athletes can have low or high maximal heart rates; neither is a predictor of car-
    66→    66→diorespiratory fitness.
    67→    67→Rating of perceived exertion (RPE) is a subjective measure of how difficult an
    68→    68→exercise is. The RPE scale is typically 1-10, with 1 being the lowest level of exer-
    69→    69→tion and 10 being the highest. The RPE scale is a simple and effective tool that
    70→    70→can be used to determine exercise intensity or the physical demands associated
    71→    71→with a particular exercise.
    72→    72→Workout Structure
    73→    73→The following general guidelines should be followed when designing a workout or
    74→    74→series of workouts over days, weeks, and months. Doing so will ensure a higher-

    76→    76→• Fatigue management. Properly managing fatigue during a workout involves
    77→    77→close monitoring of exercise selection, rest intervals, exercise order, exercise
    78→    78→grouping, training tempo, and workout length. As fatigue accumulates during the
    79→    79→workout, biomechanics and exercise technique can be compromised, which can
    80→    80→present safety issues.
    81→    81→• Speed before strength exercises. Exercises that require higher velocity of move-
    82→    82→ment, like plyometrics or jump squats, should be performed before slower move-
    83→    83→ments. High-velocity movements can tax the nervous system to an extent that
    84→    84→compromises subsequent exercises. In these scenarios, it is advisable to alternate
    85→    85→ground-based speed exercises with upper-body exercises. Split-squat jumps, for
    86→    86→example, could be followed by push-ups.

   166→   166→Physiological Adaptations
   167→   167→to Metabolic Training
   168→   168→Physiological adaptation refers to how the body responds to, accommodates, or
   169→   169→adjusts to exercise. To give an over-simplified example, if you lift heavy weights,
   170→   170→your muscles get bigger. But there are actually a number of adaptations taking
   171→   171→place in response to such a stimulus. These adaptations include a more coordi-
   172→   172→nated neural signal to the activated muscles, uptake of nutrients, and the release
   173→   173→of muscle-building hormones.
   174→   174→Learning about the process of physiological adaptation will help you design
   175→   175→more effective workouts, understand how your body will respond to training,
   176→   176→and know what to expect with consistent and focused training. Two of the most
   177→   177→common fitness goals are burning fat and enhancing muscular development. This
   178→   178→chapter takes a deeper dive into some of the physiological adaptations to training
   179→   179→that can turn these goals into realities.
   180→   180→Adapting to Demands
   181→   181→As discussed in chapter 1, in reference to the specificity principle, adaptations are
   182→   182→specific to imposed demands. Adaptions can be morphological (related to muscle
   183→   183→size), biomechanical (related to strength and power), anatomical (related to muscle,
   184→   184→bone, or fat volumes), neurological (related to motor control), or metabolic (related
   185→   185→to aerobic or anaerobic energy pathways) in nature. Workout parameters such
   186→   186→as intensity level and rest duration can be set to elicit specific adaptations. For
   187→   187→instance, short, high-intensity exercise intervals (up to six seconds in duration,
   188→   188→focusing on quick movement with maximal intensity) paired with longer rest
   189→   189→intervals (1:1 to 1:4 work:rest ratio) can target the short-duration phosphagen meta-
   190→   190→bolic pathway. Conversely, lower-intensity activity paired with shorter-duration
   191→   191→rest intervals can target the aerobic metabolic pathway. Also, keep in mind that
   192→   192→exercises targeting multiple joints and muscle groups at one time are more meta-
   193→   193→bolically challenging than exercises targeting individual joints and muscle groups.
   194→   194→It should be pointed out that metabolic training utilizes both aerobic and anaero-
   195→   195→bic metabolic pathways, while also placing demand on the neuromuscular system
   196→   196→11
   197→   197→--- PAGE 21 ---

   199→   199→by way of strength and power exercises. In other situations, metabolic training
   200→   200→can elicit more of an aerobic metabolic (endurance) response—for example, when
   201→   201→lighter weights are used. This de-emphasizes the neuromuscular system. These
   202→   202→two examples represent the various ways in which metabolic training can be pro-
   203→   203→grammed. Neither approach is superior to the other; the optimal program is best
   204→   204→determined by the goals of the program and the individual. With this concept in
   205→   205→mind, both aerobic and anaerobic adaptations can be observed.

   451→   451→Just about any gym, regardless of the population it serves, has kettlebells in its
   452→   452→strength-training or group-exercise areas. While the use of kettlebells in the

   455→   455→Schoenfeld 2011). A kettlebell consists of an iron handle attached to an iron ball
   456→   456→(Cotter 2022). Kettlebell training offers a safer alternative to heavy-load lifting,
   457→   457→targeting weaknesses and imbalances and correcting them through short, high-
   458→   458→intensity workouts that increase hormonal production for muscle development

   461→   461→researchers interested in these tools’ ability to enhance strength, power, and car-

   468→   468→the hips (Otto et al. 2012); and promote the development of lean body mass through
