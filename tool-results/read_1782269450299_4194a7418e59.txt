    55→Short-Term Adaptations to Metabolic Training
    56→Short-term adaptations are those that occur within one to four weeks and are spe-
    57→cifically related to the composition of the training program. For example, heavy
    58→strength training will yield greater increases in strength than low-intensity, high-
    59→rep training programs. Short-term adaptations are relatively transient compared
    60→to long-term adaptions, meaning that if you stop training after one to four weeks,
    61→there is a relatively quick return to pre-training status. In practical terms, this
    62→means that you should remain consistent in your training if you want to continue
    63→to develop strength. It should be noted that nervous-system adaptations occur faster
    64→than structural changes in skeletal muscle. This has led researchers and practi-
    65→tioners to conclude that initial strength improvement is the result of neurological
    66→changes. Even if you can’t see changes in your muscle size or composition early
    67→on in your program, know that your body is adapting and building a foundation
    68→for strength. Stick to your program, and the changes will be noticeable over time.
    69→There are some adaptations that you may notice soon after beginning a met-
    70→abolic-training program. Immediate cardiorespiratory responses to metabolic
    71→training include increases in heart rate, oxygen uptake, systolic blood pressure,
    72→blood flow to active muscles, the heart’s stroke volume, and cardiac output (French
    73→2016). Ventilation rate increases significantly during resistance training (Statler
    74→and Dubois 2016), and especially metabolic training, when short rest intervals
    75→are employed. These improvements to your cardiorespiratory system will allow
    76→you to breathe easier and work harder as you take on more-challenging workouts.

   148→Bodyweight Training
   149→When it comes to enhancing functional strength, your own body can provide you
   150→with not only the most accessible and cost-effective training tool but in many
   151→ways the most beneficial. Bodyweight-training exercises do not require a gym or
   152→specialized equipment to deliver an effective workout—they can be performed
   153→virtually anywhere. This training style provides you with a functionally based
   154→approach to increasing power, strength, endurance, and flexibility in the pursuit
   155→of muscle development and fat metabolism. Most importantly, bodyweight train-
   156→ing can be done by anyone, regardless of fitness level.
   157→The simplicity of bodyweight training allows you to quickly transfer from
   158→one exercise to another without having to adjust equipment or resistance. This
   159→significantly reduces rest time while keeping your heart rate in a target training
   160→zone and increasing caloric expenditure per unit of time. Bodyweight training is
   161→an excellent way to burn body fat and increase metabolic rate, not only during
   162→exercise but after exercise.
   163→Bodyweight training allows for natural progressions in intensity. Progressions
   164→commonly involve simply adding repetitions or time to an exercise for increased
   165→fitness or changing the movement pattern to increase the demand on strength.
   166→Focusing on volume eliminates the risk of utilizing resistances that may potentially
   167→result in injury while simultaneously improving flexibility by involving a full
   168→range of movement. The engagement of the core musculature required to perform
   169→bodyweight exercises naturally develops core strength and stability, which, in
   170→turn, appears to reduce injury risk (Haff, Berninger, and Caulfield 2016).
   171→In summary, the benefits of bodyweight training can be tied to the use of
   172→functional exercises that are adaptable to all levels of fitness. The use of these
   173→exercises in a workout provides a low-cost training method that increases muscle
   174→strength in several muscle groups at once, develops relative strength, and improves
   175→balance and coordination (Harrison 2011).
   176→Dumbbell Training
   177→Developed over 2,500 years ago, no single piece of fitness equipment has stood
   178→the test of time like the dumbbell. Dumbbells can be found in almost any exercise
   179→environment, be it a high-tech fitness center, boutique fitness center, hard-core
   180→gym, school, hotel, or home gym. Dumbbells offer you the ability to train your
   181→entire body and can be utilized regardless of your level of experience, level of
   182→fitness, age, or training proficiency. They remain a staple for adults looking to
   183→get in shape, youth and professional athletes, tactical professionals, elite fitness
   184→participants, and bodybuilders.
   185→Believed to date back to the ancient Greeks, dumbbells (halteres or alteres, as
   186→they were termed) came in various shapes and sizes and were made from mate-
   187→rials that could be molded in different ways. Other ancient cultures, including
   188→the Indians, Egyptians, and Chinese, are believed to have utilized dumbbell-like
   189→objects as well, although the Greeks are recognized as their creators.

   192→The ancient Greek physician Galen is regarded as the first person to write a
   193→text (ca. 200 AD) on the benefits of exercising with dumbbells (Ford 1955; Ber-
   194→ryman 2012). Other significant exercise-associated works, such as Dialogues des
   195→Gymnastica in 1544 (Todd 2003) and De Arte Gymnastica Aput Ancientes in 1569
   196→(Ford 1955; Todd 2003), discuss dumbbell training as well. The term dumbbell was
   197→believed to have been coined in England during the 16th century, when athletes
   198→trained with handheld bells from which the clappers had been removed. Once
   199→the clappers were removed, the bells became silent, which gave rise to the name
   200→“dumbbells.” In 1864, with the release of John Blundell’s book The Muscles and
   201→Their Story (Todd 2003), dumbbells became even more popular. Ranging from 3
   202→to 100 pounds (1.4 to 45.4 kilograms), they became increasingly commonplace
   203→among fitness enthusiasts everywhere and established themselves as standard
   204→pieces of gym equipment.
   205→On February 14, 1865, the first plate-loaded dumbbell was patented. Today,
   206→there are three different types of dumbbells, each of which offers benefits. Adjust-
   207→able dumbbells consist of a short metal bar that is slightly knurled in the middle
   208→to improve hand grip. Weight plates can be slid onto each end and secured with
   209→collars. Adjustable dumbbells are quickly adaptable to weight changes and can be
   210→utilized to exercise all muscle groups. Fixed-weight dumbbells are often referred
   211→to as gym-style dumbbells because they are most often found in gyms. While
   212→they do not offer the versatility of adjustable dumbbells, a complete set of fixed
   213→dumbbells allows you to change weights quickly. Selectorized dumbbells are
   214→designed to maximize your training time by offering a single pair of dumbbells
   215→that can be quickly adjusted using a selector pin or dial to set the desired weight.
   216→Training with dumbbells produces favorable alterations in the muscular,
   217→nervous, endocrine, and cardiorespiratory systems, as well as connective-tissue
   218→improvements (Ratames and Izquierdo 2008).

   220→length, muscle size, muscle fiber type transition (Type I to Type II), pre-
   221→exercise energy-substrate levels, protein concentration, fiber number, oxida-
   222→tive capacity (with higher-volume training), and fatigue resistance.
   223→• Neural alterations include increased muscle-fiber recruitment and potentia-
   224→tion, as well as fatigue resistance, which leads to a higher training capacity.
   225→• Benefits to connective tissue include increases in bone-mineral density, tendon
   226→and ligament size, and tendon and ligament strength.
   227→• Cardiorespiratory improvements encompass acute cardiac output and stroke
   228→volume increases.

   237→When training with dumbbells, safety precautions should be taken to reduce the poten-
   238→tial for injury. Complete adequate movement preparation and a dynamic warm-up prior
   239→to a training session. Start with lighter weights and progress to heavier weights when
   240→performing multiple sets. Select a weight that will provide maximum training benefits
   241→without forcing you to sacrifice correct technique and risk getting injured.
   242→Maintain an erect, firm torso and lift the dumbbells from a rack or the floor using your
   243→legs, bending at the knees and maintaining a flat back. Enlist the assistance of a spotter
   244→when needed to ensure correct form and lift safety. Consult with a medical professional
   245→(physician or physical therapist) if you experience persistent or recurring pain or any pain
   246→prior to or after starting a lifting program.
   247→3. Oxidative energy system. Provides energy for exercise of 2 minutes or
   248→greater (Herda and Cramer 2016).
   249→Dumbbells offer an operative means to focus on major muscle groups of the
   250→entire body. They can be utilized in a gym, at home, or outdoors with no required
   251→installation or setup. Exercising with dumbbells necessitates greater stabilization,
   252→recruiting a greater number of secondary muscle groups than machine exercises
   253→and forcing limbs to work individually, which helps to prevent imbalances.
   254→Kettlebell Training
   255→Just about any gym, regardless of the population it serves, has kettlebells in its
   256→strength-training or group-exercise areas. While the use of kettlebells in the
   257→United States is new, training for strength and fitness with kettlebells can be
   258→traced back centuries to their Eastern European roots (Harrison, Schoenfeld, and
   259→Schoenfeld 2011). A kettlebell consists of an iron handle attached to an iron ball
   260→(Cotter 2022). Kettlebell training offers a safer alternative to heavy-load lifting,
   261→targeting weaknesses and imbalances and correcting them through short, high-
   262→intensity workouts that increase hormonal production for muscle development
   263→and keep cortisol levels low.
   264→Kettlebells have been studied more recently by strength and conditioning
   265→researchers interested in these tools’ ability to enhance strength, power, and car-
   266→diorespiratory fitness in individuals ranging from novice exercisers to elite-level
   267→athletes and fitness enthusiasts (Beardsley and Contreras 2014). The benefits of
   268→kettlebell training are numerous. Kettlebell training has been shown to increase
   269→overall strength and power production (Jay et al. 2011); improve core strength by
   270→emphasizing power, acceleration, and multiplanar and unilateral movement pat-
   271→terns (Lake and Lauder 2012); encourage the production of force and speed through
   272→the hips (Otto et al. 2012); and promote the development of lean body mass through
   273→high-repetition sets combined with short rest periods. Often-neglected muscles of
   274→the back of the body (core, shoulders, hips) are targeted, which improves posture
   275→--- PAGE 28 ---
   276→Metabolic-Training Tools | 19
   277→and posterior-chain function (Jay et al. 2011). Kettlebell training has also been
   278→shown to improve grip strength, which enables other strength gains (Manocchia
   279→et al. 2013).
   280→Because of its emphasis on higher volume and shorter rest periods, kettlebell
   281→training is associated with increased caloric expenditure per unit of time (20
   282→calories per minute), resulting in increased metabolism during and after training,
   283→increased fat metabolism, increased muscular endurance, and better cardiorespi-
   284→ratory function (Jay et al. 2011). Kettlebells provide the user with one piece of
   285→exercise equipment to train the entire body (Jay et al. 2011). If you are limited with
   286→space at home or want to minimize your movement around the gym, kettlebells
   287→are the perfect tools to simplify your workout. Kettlebell training uses dynamic
   288→movement patterns that increase mobility, proprioception, flexibility, and body
   289→awareness by forcing the user to move through all three planes of motion in a
   290→controlled, fluid manner. With kettlebells, stabilizing muscle groups are trained
   291→in combination with primary muscles, leading to improved joint stability, coor-
   292→dination, and balance (Manocchia et al. 2013). Finally, kettlebells offer a fun and
   293→exciting way to train because the exercises are based on movement patterns and
   294→require you to be more engaged (Jay et al. 2011).
   295→When performing kettlebell exercises, use the grip position (neutral grip, with
   296→palms facing in; supinated grip, with palms facing up; or pronated grip, with palms
   297→facing down) and hold position (at your side, between your palms, in your palm,
   298→or upside down) that best suits each exercise (Cotter 2022). When performing an
   299→exercise in a standing position, place your feet slightly wider than shoulder-width
   300→apart. With training resistances between 80 and 100 percent of your repetition
   301→maximum (the maximum resistance you can utilize with a given volume and
   302→intensity), avoid holding your breath—you should continue to inhale and exhale
   303→throughout the movement (Haff, Berninger, and Caulfield 2016).
   304→Resistance-Band Training
   305→Resistance bands have become a staple of just about every fitness environment.
   306→Resistance bands come in three basic forms: tube, loop, and mini. Each of the
   307→three types provides unique benefits. Resistance bands come in a variety of
   308→colors often with handles. The color of a resistance band designates its tension
   309→level. What makes resistance-band training unique is that tension increases as
   310→the bands are stretched, meaning that resistance increases as more muscle groups
   311→are incorporated in a movement pattern (Haff, Berninger, and Caulfield 2016).
   312→Resistance bands can be used at any fitness level and any age. Youth, adults,
   313→seniors, competitive athletes, tactical professionals, and individuals with chronic
   314→conditions or disorders can all benefit from resistance-band training. Addition-
   315→ally, resistance bands are among the most inexpensive pieces of equipment and
   316→can easily be packed in a suitcase and utilized anywhere.
   317→Resistance bands are beneficial for metabolic training because they can be
   318→used safely and effectively at higher volumes with shorter rest periods. Training
   319→with resistance bands is an excellent way to reduce body fat and simultaneously

   322→develop muscle. In conjunction with dietary changes, resistance bands can bring
   323→about significant physiological changes when used three to five times per week.
   324→Resistance bands are frequently used while warming up and cooling down.

   326→mobility through dynamic range-of-motion movements at reduced resistances.
   327→Following this approach ensures that there is adequate blood flow to the exer-

   329→use of resistance bands as part of the postworkout cool-down can improve blood
   330→flow, reduce muscle soreness, and prevent injuries by enhancing stretches and

   332→Resistance bands are excellent tools for increasing muscle strength and endur-

   370→Medicine Ball Training
   371→Medicine balls can be traced back to ancient Persia, Greece, and Rome, where
   372→they were used by athletes to increase strength, power, and athletic performance.
   373→It is believed that the ancient Greeks stuffed animal skins for patients to toss for
   374→medicinal purposes. A similar type of ball is believed to have been used in Persia
   375→in the 1700s. The term medicine ball can be dated back to 1876 (Roberts 2020). Hip-
   376→pocrates believed them to be an essential tool for helping people regain normal body
   377→movement after injury, as well as a means for enhancing and maintaining overall
   378→health. Few, if any, pieces of equipment are versatile in the way that medicine
   379→balls are: not only can they be lifted, but they can also be thrown and slammed.
   380→The adaptability of medicine balls enables users to utilize nontraditional methods
   381→to expand training goals and objectives. In addition, nearly every medicine ball
   382→exercise can be combined with other exercises to create a continuous flow with
   383→minimal rest. This is beneficial for two reasons:
   384→1. It mimics functional movements used in everyday life.
   385→2. It enables users to program any number of exercise combinations and varia-
   386→tions.
   387→Today, medicine balls are used to develop power, strength, anaerobic endurance,
   388→and cardiorespiratory fitness.
   389→Medicine balls can be soft or firm, can come with or without handles, and can
   390→vary in weight from 2 to 100 pounds (0.9 to 45.4 kilograms). Medicine ball training
   391→has a wide spectrum of applications, ranging from injury rehabilitation to strength
   392→and sports-performance enhancement (Faigenbaum et al. 2006; Hagberg et al.
   393→2009). Its ability to recruit a significant number of muscle groups and to develop
   394→explosive power through functional movement makes medicine ball training an
   395→essential tool in fitness, rehabilitation, and sports performance (Vincent, Traywick,
   396→and Washburn 2013). When training with a medicine ball, perform 10 to 15 reps
   397→or perform reps for a designated time, provided that you can do so with good form
   398→(Faigenbaum et al. 2006). When selecting a medicine ball, it is important to choose
   399→one heavy enough to reduce movement speed but not so heavy that technique,
   400→form, and range of motion will be negatively impacted. Medicine balls weighing
   401→between 4 and 15 pounds (1.8 to 6.8 kilograms) are generally recommended as a
   402→starting point (Faigenbaum et al. 2006; Vincent, Traywick, and Washburn 2013).
   403→Medicine ball training can be effective as a fat-burning exercise when periods of
   404→high-intensity, high-volume training are implemented to maximize caloric expen-
   405→diture. This results in an increased postexercise metabolic effect as well. Training
   406→with medicine balls allows exercisers to utilize their full range of movement, which
   407→is important for developing total-body strength. Medicine balls also lend themselves
   408→to developing strength in the context of sports that require rotational movement.
   409→--- PAGE 31 ---
   410→22 | Metabolic Training
   411→This is accomplished through an increased focus on acceleration. Along with general
   412→strength, increased levels of explosive strength and power can be developed when
   413→performing functional movements using medicine balls. Cardiorespiratory fitness
   414→is another important benefit of utilizing medicine balls in a workout. Performing
   415→total-body medicine ball exercises such as slams for 30 to 45 seconds provides a
   416→strong cardiorespiratory stimulus, along with helping to develop power and strength
   417→(Faigenbaum et al. 2018).
   418→Emphasizing medicine ball exercises that link upper and lower extremities can help
   419→to develop core strength and enhance postural alignment (Haff, Berninger, and Caul-

   421→medicine ball training enhances functional performance through movements that
   422→combine multiple patterns and planes of motion. For individuals experiencing lower-
   423→back pain or other orthopedic issues, medicine balls can aid rehabilitation by promot-
   424→ing proper alignment, which, in turn, reduces stress to muscles and connective tissue.
   425→Medicine balls are a safe, simple, and effective fitness tool for rehabilitative as
   426→well as fitness- and performance-based exercises, allowing users to focus on strength,
   427→endurance, and recovery with minimal risk for injury. In addition to their train-
   428→ing benefits, medicine ball exercises, which can often be done with a partner or in
   429→a group, provide an opportunity for socialization that is sometimes lacking from

   431→Heavy Rope, Suspension, Weighted Sleds,
   432→and Sandbag Training
   433→When developing metabolic-training programs, heavy ropes, suspension trainers,
   434→weighted sleds, and sandbags can be valuable additions to traditional training tools.
   435→These alternative pieces of equipment have grown rapidly in popularity and become
   436→staples in metabolic-training programs, alongside traditional training tools (Cissik and
   437→Dawes 2015; Haff, Berninger, and Caulfield 2016). When selecting these implements
   438→for training, consider the advantages and disadvantages of each piece of equipment,
   439→along with your level of fitness. As with all exercise modalities, proper technique
   440→and appropriate assistance are necessary aspects of safe and effective training. Let’s
   441→look at each of these pieces of equipment and consider how they can be used to vary
   442→your training.
   443→Heavy ropes, also known as battle ropes, are thick ropes of varying lengths and

   541→Periodization for
   542→Metabolic Training
   543→Periodization is the variation of training over a period of time in order to achieve
   544→an optimal outcome. Or, in more detailed terms, “Periodization is a theoretical
   545→and practical construct that allows for the systematic, sequential, and integrative
   546→programing of training interventions into mutually dependent periods of time
   547→in order to induce specific physiological adaptations that underpin performance
   548→outcomes” (Haff 2016). Another way to think of it is that periodization is the con-
   549→cept, and programming (sets, reps, exercises, weight used, days per week, etc.)
   550→is the application.
   551→Components of Periodization
   552→There are three concepts central to understanding periodization: general adap-
   553→tation syndrome, stimulus–fatigue–recovery–adaptation (SFRA) theory, and the
   554→fitness–fatigue paradigm. In the following, we explain what they are and how
   555→they fit into metabolic training.
   556→General Adaptation Syndrome
   557→This theory is the most fundamental to physical training. When you perform a
   558→training session that takes your body beyond its current state, you’re applying
   559→a stress to your systems. The body initially responds to the stress in an alarm
   560→stage where fatigue is experienced possibly in the form of soreness or stiffness
   561→in, generally, the first 24 to 48 hours following your training session. After this
   562→alarm stage the body transitions into the resistance stage and the body further
   563→adapts to the stress. In the final stage, the body experiences what is known as
   564→supercompensation. Upon reaching the supercompensation stage, the body is at a
   565→higher level of adaptation which means you are becoming stronger or fitter than
   566→you were before the training.
   567→Stimulus–Fatigue–Recovery–Adaptation Theory
   568→The SFRA theory suggests that training stimuli produce a general response (Haff
   569→2016). The greater the magnitude of the stimulus or workload, the greater the
   570→25
   571→--- PAGE 35 ---
   572→26 | Metabolic Training
   573→fatigue response. In other words, there is a direct positive correlation between
   574→training workload and fatigue. As you adapt to the training workload, however,
   575→your fatigue will lessen. When a new training stimulus is introduced, the adap-
   576→tation cycle continues. The SFRA theory emphasizes timing or scheduling of
   577→interventions and is essential to optimizing training effects. Keep in mind that
   578→if you stop training after a single intervention, you will return to your untrained
   579→state. Consistency is key to maximizing your results.
   580→Complete recovery is often not reached after training interventions. However,
   581→partial recovery does often occur, and training programs should have the fre-
   582→quency, intensity, and volume of training adjusted in such a manner to optimize
   583→results. The general trend is to gradually decrease volume of training while
   584→increasing intensity. This concept is central to periodization.
   585→Fitness–Fatigue Paradigm
   586→The fitness–fatigue paradigm posits a relationship between fitness and fatigue
   587→known as the “state of readiness” (Haff 2016; see figure 4.1). This model was first
   588→proposed by the sport scientist Vladimir Zatsiorsky (Zatsiorsky & Kraemer 2006).
   589→There is a direct positive relationship between volume load of training and fatigue,
   590→where volume load refers to amount of weight times the number of repetitions
   591→assigned to an exercise. As an example, 10 reps at 100 pounds (45.4 kilograms)
   592→performed for three sets results in a volume load of 3,000 pounds (1,360.7 kilo-
   593→grams) for that exercise. The volume load of an exercise for a specific period of
   594→time (a day, a week, a month, etc.) can be quickly calculated by multiplying reps
   595→by weight by sets. The importance of accumulated fatigue as a result of volume
   596→load cannot be overstated.

   618→Training Principles of a Periodization Model
   619→There are several models of periodization that can be applied to metabolic train-
   620→ing programs For the purpose of this book we will focus on block, linear, and
   621→undulating periodization. These models adjust training volume and intensity over
   622→a period of time in an effort to avoid mental and physical stagnation. It is impor-
   623→tant to remember that proper fatigue management during the training session
   624→and between training sessions is a central element of metabolic training. For the
   625→purposes of metabolic training, we will provide frequency, intensity, and volume
   626→guidelines to ensure fatigue management in very general terms.
   627→Block Periodization
   628→The general approach to block periodization involves the sequencing of three
   629→different training blocks (Issurin 2008). The three different training blocks are
   630→accumulation, transmutation, and realization. During each block of training, there
   631→is a targeted physical characteristic to be improved. Those characteristics include
   632→strength, power, endurance, agility, or speed to name a few.
   633→During the accumulation phase, training volume is emphasized. As stated,
   634→there is a specific focus on one aspect of fitness (e.g., power, speed, or endurance).
   635→During transmutation, workload is decreased, allowing the body to adapt fully to
   636→the stresses introduced during accumulation. Workouts become shorter and more
   637→goal specific. During realization, workload is decreased even further, allowing
   638→fitness adaptations to peak. The training intensities associated with these phases
   639→are 50 to 75 percent for accumulation, 75 to 90 percent for transmutation, and
   640→90 percent and higher for realization. The salient point to remember is intensity
   641→starts low and volume starts high, progressing to high intensity to low volume.
   642→Each block lasts about two to four weeks. Progressions from more repetitions at
   643→lower weight to fewer repetitions at higher weight over two- to four-week blocks
   644→are the most applicable to metabolic training (see table 4.1).

   657→Another concept of block periodization is training a physical characteristic

   669→Linear and Undulating Periodization
   670→Linear periodization describes programs that progress in a straight, or nearly
   671→straight, line. Exercise programming according to this model is characterized by
   672→gradual increases in intensity and gradual decreases in volume.
   673→Undulating periodization describes programs that rise and fall on a scheduled
   674→timeframe—most often weekly. At its most fundamental, undulating periodiza-
   675→tion involves scheduling high-repetition, low-volume days in close proximity to
   676→low-repetition, high-volume days. (Of course, there are many different applied
   677→models, but we will keep the fundamental variables of undulating periodization
   678→simple.) For instance, when training twice a week, Monday’s exercises are per-
   679→formed with three sets of 10 repetitions, and Thursday’s exercises are performed
   680→for three sets of six repetitions. A corresponding increase in weight is used when
   681→there is a decrease in repetitions.
   682→Periodization’s Value to Metabolic Training
   683→A central purpose of periodization is to avoid stagnation and maximize improve-
   684→ment. Periodization is achieved through the manipulation of variables such as
   685→frequency, intensity, and duration, among other loading parameters. There have
   686→been a multitude of scientific studies validating the use of periodized training
   687→models to improve performance and fitness (Bompa, 2009; Bondarchuk, 1988;
   688→Bondarchuk, 1994; Foster, 1998; Stone, 1982; Stone, 2007).
   689→Besides being a more effective method of training, periodization offers benefits
   690→to the participant by way of
   691→• Psychological mitigation. Varying training through sets, reps, and exercises
   692→can offer a psychological or mental break.
   693→• Goal setting. Periodization offers the participant the opportunity to set both
   694→short-term and long-term goals. These goals can be set in accordance with
   695→individual desires. Performance goals could be to improve endurance, add
   696→muscle mass, or increase strength/power or O max. Health and fitness goals

   698→could be to improve body composition, weight loss, blood profile, blood pres-

   701→sure, or muscle strength and endurance, to name a few. Another goal could
   702→be to consistently perform a number of workouts or consecutive workouts.
   703→Regardless of what the goal is, it can be measured using the acronym SMART.
   704→• Specific: Focus on one or two specific goals.
   705→• Measurable: Use quantifiable metrics.

   709→Organizational Methods
   710→There are several methods that can be used to implement periodization strate-
   711→gies. Any one of these strategies can be effective; which strategy is most effec-
   712→tive depends on the individual, with factors such as training history, response to
   713→training, age, and others playing a determining role. In an effort to simplify and
   714→possibly demystify the application of periodization, the following training model
   715→is offered as a guide.
   716→“Phases” of training simply refer to time periods. These time periods can be
   717→short (lasting only one day) or quite long (lasting weeks or even months). There
   718→are many reasons why you may want to stay in a particular training period. It is
   719→essential for exercisers to change the phase of training, however, and the frequency,
   720→intensity, type, and duration of training on a consistent basis (phases should gen-
   721→erally not last longer than two to four weeks). Let’s further discuss some of the
   722→most common phases of training and how they are characterized.
   723→Hypertrophy Phase
   724→The hypertrophy phase is performed with the intention of increasing muscle mass.
   725→Traditionally, the hypertrophy phase is characterized by multiple sets, relatively
   726→light weight, and high repetitions. For instance, if you wanted to increase the
   727→muscle mass of your thighs, you would perform, squats, lunges, leg extensions,
   728→and leg curls, completing three to four sets per exercise and anywhere from 8 to
   729→12 repetitions per set. It should be noted that a one-size-fits-all approach is rarely
   730→advisable when considering individual training strategies—a certain level of flex-
   731→ibility is required to achieve optimal results.
   732→Strength Phase
   733→The goal of the strength phase is to increase the amount of force that a muscle or
   734→action can produce. Linear periodization models often move immediately from a
   735→hypertrophy phase to a strength phase. The strength phase utilizes lower repeti-
   736→tions and heavier weights than the hypertrophy phase. Strength work, therefore, is
   737→characterized by a higher level of intensity paired with lower volume. For example,
   738→a strength workout might incorporate four sets of five to eight repetitions of squats.
