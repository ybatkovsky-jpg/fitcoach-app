  2912→Competitive Period
  2913→During the competitive period (in-season), we maintain or even continue to
  2914→increase strength and power slightly as we decrease volume. Decreasing volume
  2915→(usually sets and reps), as well as eliminating any eccentric tempos greater than

  2933→recovery) period. This may include taking complete time off to allow the athlete

  2947→LINEAR AND UNDULATING PERIODIZATION
  2948→This section covers the two most popular methods of periodization: linear and
  2949→nonlinear (also known as undulating). We will first take a brief look at each and
  2950→discuss when one method may be the better option of the two.
  2951→
  2952→Linear Periodization
  2953→In 1964, Leon Matveyev popularized linear periodization. It typically involves
  2954→staying in the same loading zone over an entire mesocycle before transitioning
  2955→into a different loading zone or training phase. Doing so allows the athlete to
  2956→stay in a particular training adaptation for a longer period of time.
  2957→Linear periodization is the most common method used with novice athletes
  2958→and athletes who can afford a longer (greater than eight weeks) off-season. This
  2959→type of programming is appealing because it prepares the athlete’s body to
  2960→accept more variations in future training. This plays a considerable role in-
  2961→
  2962→season because the longer we train a strength adaptation, the longer it takes
  2963→before that trait begins to decline once game play starts. For example, an athlete
  2964→who stays in a maximal strength phase for four to eight weeks through linear
  2965→periodization will retain that strength for a longer period of time than an athlete
  2966→who trained strength only one time a week for a month in a nonlinear, or
  2967→undulating, model. (Chapter 11 covers this in greater detail with in-season
  2968→training and training residuals.)
  2969→A linear method can be applied in two ways:
  2970→Sets and reps remain the same, while intensity and volume increase. The
  2971→early-to-mid–off-season is a better time for this method to be used for
  2972→building strength because it produces an increase in overall volume as the
  2973→phase progresses. (See table 7.3. Remember that VBT ranges are estimates.)
  2974→Intensity increases while training volume decreases. This decrease in
  2975→volume occurs when sets and reps are adjusted within the training phases.
  2976→The late off-season is a better time to use this method when training
  2977→progresses toward competition. (See table 7.4. Remember that VBT ranges
  2978→are estimates.)
  2979→I use a linear method of periodization for most of my athlete’s yearly plan.
  2980→However, I do move to a nonlinear (undulating) method during the in-season and
  2981→sometimes in the transition periods as well if practice or time becomes an issue
  2982→and limits time in the weight room.

  3087→Phase I: Tissue Prep
  3088→Phase I tissue prep is the foundation for the other phases of training, and
  3089→knowing this is especially crucial before entering into a strength phase
  3090→where volume and load are higher. I have found that athletes who skip
  3091→phase I and jump right into phase II hypertrophy do not reap the same
  3092→benefits in later phases as those athletes who spend the necessary four
  3093→to six weeks in this phase first. The name of this phase reflects the fact
  3094→that the main objective is not to achieve an immediate overload, but,
  3095→rather, to elicit a progressive adaptation of the athlete’s anatomy and
  3096→tensile strength. While this phase does not focus on hypertrophy
  3097→specifically, increases in cross-sectional area may develop due to the
  3098→isometric time under tension nature of the training. In addition, an
  3099→extended amount of time is spent in the eccentric and isometric phases
  3100→of the exercise (see figure 8.1).
