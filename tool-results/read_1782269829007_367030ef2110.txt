    50→mitochondria, the so-called workhorses of the muscle. This adaptation
    51→promotes the use of fatty acids, allowing a greater production of energy
    52→ATP. This is particularly important for the endurance athlete, because it
    53→slows the depletion of body glycogen stores, which are limited. The net
    54→result is a longer time to fatigue. The preference for fatty acid use over
    55→glycogen by the endurance-trained body also results in slower lactic acid
    56→accumulation.
    57→At this point, we start to see the extent of the integration of various body
    58→systems. An increase in respiratory economy and endurance allows for
    59→greater carbon dioxide removal and oxygen transport to the blood. An
    60→--- PAGE 95 ---
    61→increase in capillary density then facilitates the transport of these gases to
    62→the mitochondria in the muscle. The gas diffusion rate at the mitochondria
    63→affects not only energy production but also the substrate source for this
    64→energy production. As carbohydrate (glycogen) is used more sparingly
    65→because of the greater use of fats, time to fatigue is prolonged and lactate
    66→accumulation is slowed. The net result is a faster or longer performance.
    67→Thus, we need to ensure that these three key systems—cardiovascular,
    68→respiratory, and muscular—are developed in unison, because failure of one
    69→system to adapt will compromise the adaptation of any other.
    70→Several other adaptations take place as you develop endurance:
    71→Less carbohydrate is burned and therefore less lactic acid is produced.
    72→The greater number of blood vessels (those little capillaries) allows for
    73→greater removal of metabolic waste products.
    74→Muscle strength improves.
    75→Joints, bones, tendons, and ligaments get stronger.
    76→Efficiency improves.
    77→These and other subtle changes improve a muscle’s ability to keep going.
    78→Think of it as going long, going slow, and going with someone who talks
    79→too much. That’s endurance.
    80→We can see from this list of myriad adaptations that a varied training
    81→approach is needed to address all the required adaptations. Not only should
    82→training be varied, but it should also be sequential because some adaptations
    83→are required before others. For example, long, slow work should precede
    84→high-intensity work. The following sections explain the progression from
    85→developing endurance at 60 to 75 percent MHR (or your walking–jogging
    86→mix), to enhancing endurance at 75 to 80 percent, to maintaining endurance
    87→at 65 to 70 percent and 60 to 65 percent.
    88→Endurance Development
    89→To develop and improve endurance in the zone of 60 to 75 percent MHR, it
    90→is more productive to spend as much time as close to 75 percent as possible.
    91→Exercising at the 60 percent end of the zone can be painfully slow. In the
    92→general endurance zone of 60 to 75 percent, the muscle uses a mixture of fat
    93→--- PAGE 96 ---
    94→and carbohydrate for fuel. These fuels are supplied at varying ratios
    95→depending on the intensity of the activity. For low-intensity endurance
    96→activities with lots of oxygen present, the majority of the fuel comes from
    97→fat, although carbohydrate still provides limited amounts of fuel. However,
    98→if the event lasts long enough, the limited supply of carbohydrate stored in
    99→the muscle can be depleted. In marathon runners this is known as hitting the
   100→wall and happens when the muscle has only fat remaining for its fuel
   101→supply. To burn fats cleanly, the body needs lots of oxygen, which forces
   102→the runner to slow down. Improving the body’s ability to burn fat more
   103→efficiently so carbohydrate supplies will last until the end of the event is one
   104→of the major goals of endurance training (i.e., becoming a better butter
   105→burner). A second one, which we will elaborate on shortly, is the
   106→strengthening of the musculoskeletal system.
   107→To demonstrate the importance of oxygen in endurance activities,
   108→consider that you draw 95 percent of your fuel from fat and 5 percent from
   109→carbohydrate at basal metabolism (when you are in deep sleep and your
   110→body is awash with oxygen). However, once you exercise hard enough to
   111→elevate your heart rate into the endurance training zone, that ratio quickly
   112→changes. For unfit folks, carbohydrate becomes more important as the
   113→availability of oxygen decreases. In the 70 to 75 percent maximum heart
   114→rate zone, as much as half of the energy comes from carbohydrate. It takes
   115→the liver 24 to 48 hours to convert glycogen, the chemical form of
   116→carbohydrate stored in the muscles, from various forms of carbohydrate. If
   117→you exercise after only 24 hours of recovery (i.e., daily), you will create a
   118→short-term glycogen deficit that will then cause muscle enzymes to improve
   119→their ability to break down and metabolize fats. This can happen to such a
   120→degree that endurance athletes such as long-distance runners and cross-
   121→country skiers get as much as 80 percent of their fuel from fat when in the
   122→low range of 60 to 70 percent MHR. Have you ever wondered why the great
   123→endurance athletes are so thin? One reason is that they burn a lot of fat.
   124→Keep this revelation in mind: Once you have felt what drop-dead, all-out
   125→maximum heart rate exhaustion feels like at 100 percent, you will never feel
   126→any worse. Every 100 percent effort in a race after that will feel the same—
   127→just as painful and agonizingly breathless, but those efforts will not feel any
   128→harder. If your training is successful and makes you faster, the pain, torture,
   129→and agony of an all-out effort just does not last as long. A 100 percent MHR
   130→--- PAGE 97 ---
   131→effort is the limit of human capacity that keeps us from working ourselves
   132→to death. The good-old-college try of 110 percent effort is a sportswriter’s
   133→myth.
   134→The good news is that your other training zones also will feel the same.
   135→Your perception of the effort of your slow pace at 70 percent MHR is still
   136→the same: It’s easy. Look at it this way: If your pace for a new 5K personal
   137→record gets faster at 100 percent MHR, why wouldn’t your training pace at
   138→70 percent also get faster while still feeling just as easy as it did when your
   139→PR was slower? If you are in better shape at harder, faster paces, your
   140→easier, slower paces will also get faster. Right? Therefore, do not make the
   141→false assumption that, in order to improve, you have to work harder at
   142→higher percentages. You can’t recover fully from a previous hard workout or
   143→race if you increase the effort on an easy day from 70 to 80 percent MHR.
   144→Harder workouts such as that are the poster children of the dreaded
   145→overtraining syndrome that inevitably leads to trashed and prematurely aged
   146→legs.
   147→So what do you do if you can’t train harder but want to get better?
   148→Simple—you run more. Go back and add minutes to your training
   149→programs. If you have already learned that you can’t get faster by training
   150→harder more often, then use the variable of duration to add more easy
   151→minutes to your training pattern while keeping the intensity levels at the
   152→same respective percentages. Run longer warm-ups before and cool-downs
   153→after hard workouts. Run a little longer on your recovery days and lengthen
   154→the time of your long run. When you run more low-intensity miles, you will
   155→get stronger as you progressively add the extra resistance of more strides
   156→against gravity. It’s the same principle of progressive resistance training as
   157→doing more reps of light weights in order to get stronger. You can compare
   158→the ground-hugging stride of easy jogging to lifting light weights. There is
   159→not too much gravity to overcome when you don’t bounce high into the air
   160→with each stride. Then sprinting, with its greater gravitational resistance,
   161→can be thought of as similar to lifting heavier weights fewer times in order
   162→to get stronger. Because you have probably already maxed out how often
   163→you can survive high-intensity work, the safer option is simply more LSD—
   164→long, slow distance, not the drug. In short, the stronger you get, the faster
   165→you will run. While reaching this stage of energy efficiency, you also will
   166→be performing enough aerobic exercise in the 60 to 75 percent zone to cause
   167→--- PAGE 98 ---
   168→the adaptations you need in the cardiovascular, respiratory, and muscular
   169→systems.
   170→Equally important as the ability of your muscular engine to use fuel is
   171→the strength of the transmission—your connective tissues. Ligaments,
   172→tendons, bones, cartilage, and joints take much longer than muscles to get
   173→stronger because their blood supply is not nearly as rich. Therefore, you
   174→need to beware of the dreaded Cadillac engine with a Chevy transmission
   175→syndrome. Because your powerful muscular engine can easily tear up your
   176→delicate connective transmission, you’ll be susceptible to classic overuse
   177→injuries such as Achilles tendonitis, plantar fasciitis, iliotibial band
   178→syndrome, and even stress fractures. Avoiding these perils will be covered
   179→in later discussions concerning the length of time you need to spend in
   180→phase I.
   181→Training Techniques for Developing
   182→Endurance
   183→You will develop and improve your endurance by spending more time
   184→exercising in the zone of 60 to 75 percent MHR. You can do so by using
   185→either one or both of two basic types of workouts. The most common is
   186→LSD using steady, continuous exercise in the 60 to 75 percent MHR range.
   187→Research, however, has shown that varying the paces of your workout is
   188→also an effective technique. In the first edition of this book, we labeled that
   189→type of training “heart rate fartlek.” The idea was to enjoy changes of speed
   190→that caused your heart rate to quickly reach the upper 75 percent limit.
   191→Then, by slowing down, your heart rate would recover to the lower limit of
   192→60 percent MHR. In the spirit of this book and with an apology to the
   193→Swedes who developed and popularized fartlek (which they defined as
   194→“speed play”) training, we prefer to use the term “heartlek,” which we
   195→coined while working with Wahoo Fitness a few years ago.
   196→Our experience is that beginners should not use heartlek training because
   197→the change in speeds can increase the likelihood of injury. If you can
   198→perform a continuous 30-minute bout of LSD, even at a lower intensity,
   199→that’s your best start.
   200→--- PAGE 99 ---
   201→To execute a heartlek workout, picture your heart rate fluctuating up and
   202→down like a roller coaster. You can make that happen by jogging up to 75
   203→percent MHR and then walking down to 60 percent MHR. Or, if you are in
   204→better shape, you can run up to 75 percent MHR and then jog your heart
   205→rate back down. You can be as creative as you wish. Use both target heart
   206→rates within this zone to elevate and lower your heart rate and vary your
   207→pace. The goal is simply to be able to increase the amount of work using
   208→either of these low-intensity modes of exercise to improve running duration
   209→from at least 30 minutes up to 60 minutes and beyond if your goal activity
   210→is something like a marathon.
   211→We have included this piece in the aerobic chapter even though we will
   212→develop it more in the anaerobic chapter. By this point you have achieved a
   213→solid endurance base that is strong enough to allow you to run for 30 to 60
   214→minutes and maintain your pace across a range of terrain. For example, you
   215→can push yourself on a hill and see an increase in exercise heart rate, and
   216→you are capable of holding that pace and then dropping your heart rate back
   217→down on the flats. Using the heartlek approach, you should be able to inject
   218→three or four higher-intensity spurts for 30 to 120 seconds over your 30-
   219→minute run without ever falling below your base of 60 percent MHR.
   220→Continuous or varied pace workouts can be used singularly or in
   221→combination with the base phase of conditioning to develop aerobic
   222→endurance. This is what we refer to as getting in shape. Using these
   223→approaches will allow you to stimulate your muscles long enough and over
   224→long enough periods of time to induce adaptations.
   225→If you are just getting in shape or if your level of athletic activity has
   226→been interrupted, you will need to redevelop your endurance base. Phase I
   227→of conditioning usually takes the average, sedentary adult up to 16 weeks. If
   228→you have already been through the conditioning process, you should be able
   229→to redevelop an endurance base within six to eight weeks. A shorter period
   230→of four weeks should be sufficient to redevelop endurance if you are coming
   231→back after a postseason period of active rest. In any case, an adaptation of
   232→classic heartlek training is ideal for training in the 60 to 75 percent MHR
   233→zone.
   234→--- PAGE 100 ---
   235→Heartlek workouts, in which you cycle your heart rate up and down within your
   236→training zone, will help you quickly build a base of endurance and prepare for more
   237→challenging events, such as races.
   238→If you are building a new base, take careful note of the following
   239→discussion. Our heart rate heartlek workouts are the essence of heart rate
   240→monitor training. If you have been fairly active and have had athletic
   241→experiences earlier in life, you might consider LSD the most attractive
   242→activity for getting back into shape. But please read the following to see
   243→how the advantages of monitored training can be applied. Heart rate
   244→heartlek training is perfect for whichever activity—jogging, rowing, cycling
   245→—you choose. Heart rate heartlek workouts are a great way to introduce
   246→small elements of faster movement into your workouts for manageable
   247→periods of time and still keep the risk of injury at a minimum.
   248→Our adaptation of heartlek training to the heart rate monitor is the perfect
   249→example of smart, individualized training. Here’s an example of a 20-
   250→minute session:
   251→1. Calculate your target heart rate zone with the upper limit at 75
   252→percent and a lower limit at 60 percent MHR.
   253→2. Pick an exercise mode—walking, jogging, running, cycling, rowing,
   254→or any of the machines such as the elliptical or stair-climbing types in
   255→the aerobic section of your fitness center.
   256→3. Start exercising slowly and easily as your heart rate eases up into
   257→your 60 to 75 percent zone. As you warm into the workout, you’ll
   258→--- PAGE 101 ---
   259→probably find yourself gradually picking up the pace and effort. Your
   260→heart rate will rise and eventually hit the 75 percent upper limit.
   261→4. At that point, to keep the workout aerobic, quickly slow until your
   262→heart rate drops back to as close to 60 percent as you can get it.
   263→5. Next, gradually play with the speed of your workout until you come
   264→back up to 75 percent effort. By this we mean that you should
   265→experiment with different speeds until you get a nice feel for how fast
   266→you need to move to elicit the heart rate response you need.
   267→Following this routine for the recommended number of weeks will
   268→greatly improve endurance and your overall fitness. Raising your heart rate
   269→will require faster and longer pickups. The result is that you will cover more
   270→distance during the 20-minute workout. You also will enjoy a nice, safe
   271→improvement in your speed without risk of injury because, at the upper limit
   272→of 75 percent MHR, your pickups will still be aerobic and, therefore, free of
   273→lactic acid buildup in the muscles.
   274→After your first couple of workouts, you’ll have a mental measure of the
   275→effort and be able to increase and decrease your heart rate without too much
   276→difficulty. How long you take to work up to 75 percent and then coast down
   277→to 60 percent will be your choice. This option gives you a choice of a
   278→variety of movements. You can increase the effort to 75 percent quickly by
   279→going faster, recover to 60 percent slowly by gradually easing off the pace,
   280→or vice versa.
   281→Heartlek workouts are a creative version of interval training without the
   282→rigorous repetitions of the same distance. It is a training system that can be
   283→used to develop all four fitness components of endurance, stamina,
   284→economy, and speed.
   285→The fartlek system originated in the 1930s and was made famous in the
   286→1940s by Gunder Hagg and Arne Andersson, who took turns lowering the
   287→world record for the mile as they tried to break the barrier of the magic 4-
   288→minute mile. Hagg eventually got his time down to 4:01.4, where the record
   289→stood for nine years until Roger Bannister ran 3:59.4 in 1954.
   290→LSD training, on the other hand, is all about consistency of pace and
   291→effort. Rather than raising and lowering your heart rate like a roller coaster,
   292→you simply maintain the same jog, or slow run, pace over a set time. As you
   293→work your way into shape, you start at the lower end of the intensity range
   294→--- PAGE 102 ---
   295→(60 to 75 percent MHR) to maintain the same pace for the allotted time and
   296→distance. Your heart rate will gradually rise to 75 percent as the workout
   297→continues. As your fitness improves, you’ll find it easier to train at a higher
   298→intensity with the same sense of effort, resulting in either more distance
   299→covered in the same amount of time or the same distance covered in less
   300→time.
   301→A note of caution: Depending on the shape you’re in, the opposite may
   302→happen. You may start out working too hard and reach your upper heart rate
   303→target in less than a New York minute. If so, then you’ll have to spend your
   304→whole workout tapping the brakes to stay in this zone. It’s best to keep in
   305→mind the advice of Walt Stack, a great amateur philosopher, about the
   306→running boom in the early 1970s: “Start slow, and then taper off.”
   307→LSD training is the natural follow-up to low-level heartlek training. As
   308→you increase fitness through heartlek training, you will be able to hold your
   309→pickups close to and at 75 percent for longer and longer periods. Eventually,
   310→you will be able to complete the entire training session holding pace at 75
   311→percent MHR with no need to slow down to keep your heart rate from
   312→creeping over 75 percent. When you get to this point, you are ready to add
   313→LSD training to the mix. The LSD training will be done at a lower intensity
   314→to allow you to go longer, but the concept is the same: Maintain an aerobic
   315→pace for the desired duration, gradually increasing that duration for greater
   316→endurance. Then once you have completed 12 to 16 weeks of solid LSD
   317→training, you can introduce the heartlek approach, which is a more advanced
   318→and higher-intensity version of training.
   319→Another note of caution: To prevent overuse injuries, sports medicine
   320→specialists advise that the volume of work should increase only 10 to 15
   321→percent per week. Of course, you should exercise caution, but if you are
   322→starting at 10 miles (16 km) per week, adding just 1 to 1.5 miles (1.6 to 2.4
   323→km) over the week might be a little too conservative.
   324→Duration of Workouts for Endurance
   325→Development
   326→Ultimately, you will execute the workouts outlined in this chapter for longer
   327→periods of time. However, for an exercise session to provide enough
   328→--- PAGE 103 ---
   329→stimulus to elicit a conditioning effect, it must last a minimum of 20
   330→minutes. The number of times during a heartlek workout that you raise and
   331→lower your heart rate within your 60 to 75 percent effort zone depends on
   332→your level of general fitness. As you get stronger and fitter, it will gradually
   333→take longer to elevate your heart rate to your upper target and take less time
   334→to recover.
   335→This 20-minute period may seem too short, and with the intensity so low,
   336→you may think that it is too easy. You may wonder whether you’ll ever get
   337→in shape at this rate. But hold on. This is a delicate period of adaptation.
   338→Your muscular system will get stronger rather quickly thanks to its good
   339→blood supply and may give you the impression that you’re ready for bigger
   340→and better workouts. At the start of this period of getting into shape, you
   341→will probably experience muscle stiffness and soreness. However, you’ll
   342→find that you quickly get over those little aches and pains, and your engine
   343→will start adding horsepower. To avoid setbacks, we suggest that you follow
   344→a hard–easy pattern of workouts until you finish the recommended number
   345→of weeks appropriate for your circumstance.
   346→Sample Base-Building Training
   347→Pattern
   348→At its most elemental level, here’s how a typical training plan begins and
   349→follows the classic pattern of hard–easy days. We use the word hard rather
   350→loosely here to refer to the days that you do the heartlek workouts. The
   351→remaining days are easy, continuous activity at 60 to 65 percent MHR, or
   352→total rest (because nothing is easier than a real day off).
   353→Please keep in mind as you study these samples that they are designed to
   354→demonstrate the principles involved and are not training programs. The
   355→sample training programs presented in the last chapters of the book contain
   356→the progressions in volume, intensity, and frequency that you would
   357→realistically expect to follow.
   358→For weeks 1 and 2, follow this pattern:
   359→Hard days (Monday and Thursday): 20-minute heartlek at 60 to 75
   360→percent MHR. The 20 minutes do not include slow, easy warm-up
   361→--- PAGE 104 ---
   362→activity or a similar period for cooling down at less than 60 percent
   363→MHR.
   364→Easy days (Tuesday and Saturday): 20-minute recovery effort at a
   365→steady pace of 65 to 70 percent MHR. Follow the same warm-up and
   366→cool-down patterns as you used on the hard days.
   367→Off days (Wednesday, Friday, and Sunday): Take days off. These are
   368→complete rest days with no activity.
   369→If you experience aches or pains or feel you need more time to adjust to
   370→this routine, extend these workouts for another week or two. If you feel
   371→good and are ready and eager to advance to harder work, move on to the
   372→next pattern.
   373→For weeks 3 and 4, follow this pattern:
   374→Hard days (Tuesday and Thursday): 20-minute heartlek at 60 to 75
   375→percent MHR.
   376→Easy days (Monday and Friday): 25- to 30-minute recovery effort at
   377→65 to 70 percent MHR.
   378→Long workout (Saturday): Begin to extend your continuous easy effort
   379→at 65 to 70 percent by making this workout 5 minutes longer than the
   380→time of your Monday and Friday workout each week until you reach 45
   381→minutes.
   382→Off days (Wednesday and Sunday): Take these days off.
   383→Repeat this pattern until you can do the heartlek workouts for 30 minutes
   384→by adding 3 to 5 minutes to them each week. Gauge your readiness to
   385→increase the length of the workouts by how your legs feel. Heartlek
   386→workouts are a rather artistic system of training and allow you the freedom
   387→to determine your own fate. One good rule of thumb to apply is the “three
   388→out of four” rule—every fourth week should be a shorter or lower-intensity
   389→recovery week.
   390→Be very conservative about how fast you add more volume in these
   391→zones to give those connective tissues a chance to strengthen so you don’t
   392→blow your transmission. One sign that you are ready to move on would be
   393→noting that your fitness level has improved to the point that the distance
   394→covered during the workouts has plateaued.
   395→--- PAGE 105 ---
   396→Once you are ready, move to the following plan for the next two to four
   397→weeks, adding the other endurance-building workout—long, slow distance,
   398→or LSD—to the pattern. Use nothing harder than 75 percent MHR on the
   399→LSD.
   400→For the next two to four weeks, follow this pattern:
   401→Hard days (Tuesday and Saturday): 30 minutes of heartlek at 65 to 75
   402→percent MHR.
   403→Hard days (Thursday and Sunday): 45 to 60 minutes of LSD at 60 to
   404→75 percent MHR.
   405→Easy days (Monday and Wednesday): 30 to 45 minutes of recovery
   406→effort at 65 to 70 percent MHR.
   407→Off day (Friday): Take the day off to recover fully.
   408→Start adding 5 minutes per workout to the time of your easy recovery
   409→days from the previous pattern. Work up to 45 minutes on Thursday and 60
   410→minutes on Sunday.
   411→During these weeks, the increased frequency of six sessions per week
   412→will result in your experiencing some days of accumulating fatigue in your
   413→working muscles. We’ve also increased the intensity of the heartlek by
   414→raising the lower limit of the recovery target heart rate from 60 to 65
   415→percent MHR. This will make the workout harder by cutting down the
   416→recovery time. All this is due to our deliberate attempt to create a short-term
   417→chronic glycogen deficit in your muscles to stimulate the cells to use more
   418→fat as fuel. The easy days will allow you to recover, but you will also have
   419→to make a determined effort to slow down and take it easy on the recovery
   420→workouts. To be sure that you recover as much as possible, keep your upper
   421→target heart rate at just 70 percent. You’ll notice that these workouts are
   422→definitely much harder, but still in the zone that has a low risk of injury.
   423→If going so easy on your recovery days seems like punishment, keep this
   424→pearl of wisdom about physiology in mind: The easier and more aerobic the
   425→workout, the more oxygen is present in the circulating blood. It is then
   426→available for the muscle to use so it can burn a higher percentage of its fuel
   427→from fat, thus sparing the carbohydrate you eat for conversion to glycogen.
   428→Transition to Enhance Endurance
   429→--- PAGE 106 ---
   430→A consequence of lower-intensity, easier training to develop endurance is
   431→the less-than-complete strengthening of muscles, ligaments, and tendons.
   432→Another, by the very definition of aerobic work (no huffing and puffing
   433→allowed), is that your respiratory system’s muscles are just beginning to get
   434→into good shape. Now it’s time to move those parts of your body through a
   435→transition zone that will focus on the upper edge of your aerobic capacity.
   436→The extra effort and speed will put a little more stress on these systems and
   437→will prepare you for the strains of high-intensity anaerobic conditioning that
   438→will come when you move up the training triangle into the stamina,
   439→economy, and speed zones.
   440→This transition work will be in the steady-state zone of 75 to 80 percent
   441→MHR. These two percentages are, respectively, right at the top of the
   442→aerobic zone and right at the bottom of the anaerobic zone. Workouts in this
   443→zone can put a modest stress on your respiratory system while you push
   444→your biomechanical system through bigger and faster ranges of motion.
   445→To see what we mean, picture this example of 28-year-old Hannah
   446→making this transition from jogging and easy, low-intensity LSD running to
   447→steady-state running:
   448→On easy days, she’s not just jogging with those little, bitty, mincing
   449→steps anymore or alternating easy strides for short heartlek pickups.
   450→She’s running lightly and comfortably with a smoother, longer, but not
   451→full stride for extended periods of time.
   452→She’s not breathing hard, but may have to talk in Hemingway
   453→sentences, which are much shorter than the Faulkner-style run-on
   454→sentences she used when jogging.
   455→She feels like she could run forever at this pace, which is close to what
   456→she would do if in shape to run for 10 to 13 miles (16 to 21 km).
   457→Picture yourself doing your activity of choice at an effort comparable to
   458→this example, and then apply the following sample workout pattern to your
   459→sport.

   478→Building Your Baseline, Trimming
   479→Your Waistline
   480→If you’re like us, you might come off the winter holiday season with a few extra pounds
   481→or kilograms and a bunch of missed workouts. However, in your haste to remedy the
   482→weight issue, you should literally slow down and consider the following points. First,
   483→although you might be concerned about body weight, doing the correct type of base
   484→training in the winter will more appropriately address this issue. Second, this time of
   485→year should be about base building, or developing an aerobic foundation. Third, to do
