   102‚Üí  2861‚ÜíTo be able to train more efficiently with VBT, we must first expand our
   103‚Üí  2862‚Üíunderstanding of training periods and what we are trying to accomplish when
   104‚Üí  2863‚Üíbreaking them down into phases.
   105‚Üí  2864‚Üí
   106‚Üí  2865‚ÜíPreparatory Period
   107‚Üí  2866‚ÜíThe goal of the preparatory period (early or mid‚Äìoff-season) is to develop a
   108‚Üí  2867‚Üíbaseline of tensile strength, lean muscle mass, and absolute strength so that
   109‚Üí  2868‚Üíathletes can train at higher intensities and speeds in later phases when
   110‚Üí  2869‚Üítransitioning to sport. The preparatory period‚Äôs training phases generally include
   111‚Üí  2870‚Üíthe following:
   112‚Üí  2871‚ÜíTissue prep (0.75 to 1.0 meters per second)
   113‚Üí  2872‚ÜíHypertrophy I (0.75 to 1.0 meters per second)
   114‚Üí  2873‚ÜíHypertrophy II (0.40 to 0.60 meters per second)
   115‚Üí  2874‚Üí
   116‚Üí  2875‚ÜíAbsolute strength (<0.50 meters per second)
   117‚Üí  2876‚ÜíCore lifts such as squats, deadlifts, and bench presses and rows are the
   118‚Üí  2877‚Üíprimary focus in the preparatory period. We use compound lifts (lifts that use
   119‚Üí  2878‚Üímulti-joint movements) early on to address overall strength of the prime movers
   120‚Üí  2879‚Üíwhile simultaneously working the accessory muscles (calves, triceps, etc.). We
   121‚Üí  2880‚Üíuse heavier loads and long times under tension to create these adaptations in this
   122‚Üí  2881‚Üíperiod, so not much sport-specific work is done at this time.
   123‚Üí  2882‚Üí
   124‚Üí  2883‚ÜíCore lifts such as squats, deadlifts, and bench presses and
   125‚Üí  2884‚Üírows are the primary focus in the preparatory period.
   126‚Üí  2885‚Üí
   127‚Üí  2886‚ÜíDeveloping a good, solid base of strength in the preparatory period helps prepare the athlete for higher
   128‚Üí  2887‚Üíintensities and speeds later in the off-season.

   132‚Üí  2891‚ÜíTransition Period I (Conversion to Sport)
   133‚Üí  2892‚ÜíThe transition I period (late off-season or preseason) takes the strength gained in
   134‚Üí  2893‚Üíthe preparatory period and works on translating it into both the strength side of
   135‚Üí  2894‚Üípower (strength‚Äìpower) and speed side of power (speed-strength). Which
   136‚Üí  2895‚Üístrength to use for training these types of power takes a higher priority and
   137‚Üí  2896‚Üídepends on both the athlete and the athlete‚Äôs respective sport. (This is covered in
   138‚Üí  2897‚Üímore detail in chapter 10.) The following training phases are generally used in
   139‚Üí  2898‚Üíthe transition I period:
   140‚Üí  2899‚ÜíAbsolute strength (<.50 meters per second)
   141‚Üí  2900‚ÜíStrength-speed (0.75 to 1.0 meters per second)
   142‚Üí  2901‚ÜíSpeed-strength (1.0 to 1.3 meters per second)
   143‚Üí  2902‚ÜíThe adaptations previously attained earlier in the off-season training transfer
   144‚Üí  2903‚Üíover to the performance of the athlete‚Äôs specific sport, and exercises, at this
   145‚Üí  2904‚Üípoint, will begin to mimic movements closer to that of the athlete‚Äôs sport (see
   146‚Üí  2905‚Üífigure 7.1). For example, if we were training a basketball player or wide receiver
   147‚Üí  2906‚Üíin football, exercises such as walking lunges and lateral lunges could be used for
   148‚Üí  2907‚Üítraining strength-power, and loaded jumps could be used for training speedpower.

   153‚Üí  2912‚ÜíCompetitive Period
   154‚Üí  2913‚ÜíDuring the competitive period (in-season), we maintain or even continue to
   155‚Üí  2914‚Üíincrease strength and power slightly as we decrease volume. Decreasing volume
   156‚Üí  2915‚Üí(usually sets and reps), as well as eliminating any eccentric tempos greater than
   157‚Üí  2916‚Üí2-0-0, reduces our chances of causing any residual soreness that may translate to
   158‚Üí  2917‚Üínext day. It is also important to note the use of a conjugate style of training due
   159‚Üí  2918‚Üíto the specific training residuals that occur from playing competitive sport
   160‚Üí  2919‚Üíduring this time. This is done in order to address several adaptations with little
   161‚Üí  2920‚Üítime available to train in the gym (more on this in chapter 11). The following
   162‚Üí  2921‚Üítraining phases are generally used in the competitive period:
   163‚Üí  2922‚ÜíAccelerative strength (0.50 to 0.75 meters per second)
   164‚Üí  2923‚ÜíStrength-speed (0.75 to 1.0 meters per second)
   165‚Üí  2924‚ÜíSpeed-strength (1.0 to 1.3 meters per second)
   166‚Üí  2925‚ÜíExercises and energy system work mimic movements specific to a sport and
   167‚Üí  2926‚Üímay include: core lifts such as squat variations and bench variations at 0.50 to
   168‚Üí  2927‚Üí0.75 meters per second, or 60 to 80 percent 1RM, (accelerative strength);
   169‚Üí  2928‚Üíweighted jumps and Olympic lifts at 0.75 to 1.3 meters per second, or 20 to 60
   170‚Üí  2929‚Üípercent 1RM, (strength-speed or speed-strength); and sprint work and change-ofdirection (COD) drills (body weight to 20 percent) (see figure 7.2).
   171‚Üí  2930‚Üí
   172‚Üí  2931‚ÜíTransition Period II
   173‚Üí  2932‚ÜíRecovery is of the utmost importance in the transition II (post-season or
   174‚Üí  2933‚Üírecovery) period. This may include taking complete time off to allow the athlete
   175‚Üí  2934‚Üíto regroup both physically and mentally before beginning a new annual training
   176‚Üí  2935‚Üíplan or rehabilitating an injury. Another option is to perform a low volume of
   177‚Üí  2936‚Üístrength training to maintain strength levels while incorporating soft tissue and
   178‚Üí  2937‚Üímobility work to expedite recovery. This option involves lighter strength training
   179‚Üí  2938‚Üíat low sets and intensities (volume), during which VBT can still be used
   180‚Üí  2939‚Üísparingly. Unless more time is warranted for an injury, it is recommended to
   181‚Üí  2940‚Üíspend no more than one to four weeks in this period; otherwise an athlete will
   182‚Üí  2941‚Üíhave to devote much more time in the following preparatory period when
   183‚Üí  2942‚Üíbeginning the next macrocycle.

   188‚Üí  2947‚ÜíLINEAR AND UNDULATING PERIODIZATION
   189‚Üí  2948‚ÜíThis section covers the two most popular methods of periodization: linear and
   190‚Üí  2949‚Üínonlinear (also known as undulating). We will first take a brief look at each and
   191‚Üí  2950‚Üídiscuss when one method may be the better option of the two.
   192‚Üí  2951‚Üí
   193‚Üí  2952‚ÜíLinear Periodization
   194‚Üí  2953‚ÜíIn 1964, Leon Matveyev popularized linear periodization. It typically involves
   195‚Üí  2954‚Üístaying in the same loading zone over an entire mesocycle before transitioning
   196‚Üí  2955‚Üíinto a different loading zone or training phase. Doing so allows the athlete to
   197‚Üí  2956‚Üístay in a particular training adaptation for a longer period of time.
   198‚Üí  2957‚ÜíLinear periodization is the most common method used with novice athletes
   199‚Üí  2958‚Üíand athletes who can afford a longer (greater than eight weeks) off-season. This
   200‚Üí  2959‚Üítype of programming is appealing because it prepares the athlete‚Äôs body to
   201‚Üí  2960‚Üíaccept more variations in future training. This plays a considerable role in-
   202‚Üí  2961‚Üí
   203‚Üí  2962‚Üíseason because the longer we train a strength adaptation, the longer it takes
   204‚Üí  2963‚Üíbefore that trait begins to decline once game play starts. For example, an athlete
   205‚Üí  2964‚Üíwho stays in a maximal strength phase for four to eight weeks through linear
   206‚Üí  2965‚Üíperiodization will retain that strength for a longer period of time than an athlete
   207‚Üí  2966‚Üíwho trained strength only one time a week for a month in a nonlinear, or
   208‚Üí  2967‚Üíundulating, model. (Chapter 11 covers this in greater detail with in-season
   209‚Üí  2968‚Üítraining and training residuals.)
   210‚Üí  2969‚ÜíA linear method can be applied in two ways:
   211‚Üí  2970‚ÜíSets and reps remain the same, while intensity and volume increase. The
   212‚Üí  2971‚Üíearly-to-mid‚Äìoff-season is a better time for this method to be used for
   213‚Üí  2972‚Üíbuilding strength because it produces an increase in overall volume as the
   214‚Üí  2973‚Üíphase progresses. (See table 7.3. Remember that VBT ranges are estimates.)
   215‚Üí  2974‚ÜíIntensity increases while training volume decreases. This decrease in
   216‚Üí  2975‚Üívolume occurs when sets and reps are adjusted within the training phases.
   217‚Üí  2976‚ÜíThe late off-season is a better time to use this method when training
   218‚Üí  2977‚Üíprogresses toward competition. (See table 7.4. Remember that VBT ranges
   219‚Üí  2978‚Üíare estimates.)

   315‚Üí  3074‚ÜíIt is important to keep in mind that many athletes expect to see huge leaps in

   317‚Üí  3076‚Üígenerally do not experience the highest peaks in performance until the
   318‚Üí  3077‚Üícompetitive period, or at best, when the competitive period draws near. Strength,
   319‚Üí  3078‚Üímuch like athletic ability and technique, is a skill that can be developed much

   321‚Üí  3080‚Üíoff-season and progress toward a peak at the beginning of the competitive period

   325‚Üí  3084‚Üíphase II hypertrophy, and their primary goal is to prepare the athlete in both
   326‚Üí  3085‚Üístrength and conditioning for heavier lifting later in the off-season.
   327‚Üí  3086‚Üí
   328‚Üí  3087‚ÜíPhase I: Tissue Prep
   329‚Üí  3088‚ÜíPhase I tissue prep is the foundation for the other phases of training, and
   330‚Üí  3089‚Üíknowing this is especially crucial before entering into a strength phase
   331‚Üí  3090‚Üíwhere volume and load are higher. I have found that athletes who skip
   332‚Üí  3091‚Üíphase I and jump right into phase II hypertrophy do not reap the same
   333‚Üí  3092‚Üíbenefits in later phases as those athletes who spend the necessary four
   334‚Üí  3093‚Üíto six weeks in this phase first. The name of this phase reflects the fact
   335‚Üí  3094‚Üíthat the main objective is not to achieve an immediate overload, but,
   336‚Üí  3095‚Üírather, to elicit a progressive adaptation of the athlete‚Äôs anatomy and
   337‚Üí  3096‚Üítensile strength. While this phase does not focus on hypertrophy
   338‚Üí  3097‚Üíspecifically, increases in cross-sectional area may develop due to the
   339‚Üí  3098‚Üíisometric time under tension nature of the training. In addition, an
   340‚Üí  3099‚Üíextended amount of time is spent in the eccentric and isometric phases
   341‚Üí  3100‚Üíof the exercise (see figure 8.1).
   342‚Üí  3101‚Üí
   343‚Üí  3102‚ÜíGOALS
   344‚Üí  3103‚ÜíThe main goals during the tissue-prep phase are the following:
   345‚Üí  3104‚ÜíImproving tensile strength. This phase prepares the tendons,
   346‚Üí  3105‚Üíligaments, and joints for longer, more strenuous sessions in later
   347‚Üí  3106‚Üíphases. This is partially because hydrogen ions, which are released
   348‚Üí  3107‚Üíby lactic acid, have been proven to stimulate the release of growth
   349‚Üí  3108‚Üíhormone and, therefore, collagen synthesis (5). Using isometric
   350‚Üí  3109‚Üíholds in low positions increases time under tension and solidifies
   351‚Üí  3110‚Üímovement patterns where they are at the biggest mechanical
   352‚Üí  3111‚Üídisadvantage. Both tissue-prep and hypertrophy phases help
   353‚Üí  3112‚Üíincrease applications of force in later phases when heavier loads are
   354‚Üí  3113‚Üíbeing used.
   355‚Üí  3114‚ÜíRegrooving good movement patterns. This involves multiple muscle
   356‚Üí  3115‚Üígroups in order to groove or re-groove more efficient motor patterns
   357‚Üí  3116‚Üí(neuromuscular coordination) specific to the athlete‚Äôs sport. For this
   358‚Üí  3117‚Üíreason, longer eccentric tempos and isometric holds are
   359‚Üí  3118‚Üíimplemented using low-resistance loads. However, concentric
   360‚Üí  3119‚Üíphases should remain explosive to stimulate fast-twitch fibers.
