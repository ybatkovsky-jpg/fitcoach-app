    35→CREATING STRENGTH TRAINING
    36→ADAPTATIONS
    37→Developing strength involves more than just lifting heavy loads. The
    38→reality is that force comes in many shapes and sizes and while we know
    39→by definition strength is the ability of a muscle or muscle group to
    40→generate force, having a well-rounded approach to achieving strength
    41→gains is a smarter alternative than just lifting heavy. In this chapter,
    42→we’ll discuss two methods of strength development that will be critical to
    43→your success in the gym. We’ll discuss why you should consider utilizing
    44→these methods along with their benefits, their reasoning, and how they
    45→play a part in keeping your programming balanced (in order to avoid
    46→things like overtraining). For more information about other training
    47→methods, see chapter 3.
    48→Dynamic Effort Training
    49→It’s a common misconception that you need to lift heavy in every workout.
    50→Many think that the effectiveness of training is judged by how hard it
    51→is. But the human body isn’t a gumball machine—it doesn’t spit out
    52→candy every time you put a quarter in. That’s because there is a point of
    53→diminishing returns. If you’re not allowing for proper recovery between
    54→higher-threshold, or maximal lifting, sessions (sessions that are more
    55→demanding on the central nervous system), you’ll eventually overtrain
    56→and start losing your progress or get injured. The reality is that hard
    57→training sessions need to be interspersed with easier training sessions.
    58→It’s also important to incorporate strength methods that differ with
    59→regard to bar velocity.
    60→What does this mean to the average individual who wants to get
    61→stronger? It means that there is low-hanging fruit to be picked with
    62→methods such as dynamic effort (DE) training, which aims to improve
    63→strength through nonmaximal measures that activate high-threshold
    64→motor units to a high degree. The goal of this method is to use nonmaximal
    65→loads with the highest attainable velocity (bar speed) to improve the rate
    66→of force development (RFD) and increase the number of recruited and
    67→trained motor units (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021).
    68→These are the key features of DE training:
    69→• Uses high-threshold motor units and facilitates RFD
    70→• Places high demands on the nervous system
    71→• Works best with accommodating resistance (AR) to ensure proper
    72→loading throughout full range of motion (ROM). AR is the use of
    73→bands or chains to provide maximal tension through full ROM.
    74→--- PAGE 223 ---
    75→Strength Programs 207
    76→• Must be separated from maximal effort training by at least 72 hours
    77→• Works the velocity portion of the force-velocity curve
    78→In short, DE training is used in this book both for strength development
    79→and athletic performance. This method maximizes the velocity component
    80→of the force-velocity curve while creating balanced programming to
    81→reduce the risk of overtraining.
    82→Submaximal Effort Training
    83→The repeated effort method, covered in chapter 3, is an integral aspect
    84→of every program in this book. While there are some similarities between
    85→it and the submaximal effort method, the major difference is that
    86→submaximal work is delivered exclusively through bilateral movements
    87→(i.e., squats, presses, pulls). The submaximal effort method will be
    88→used as a multiple-repetition maximum scenario (two- to six-rep max)
    89→because true maximal effort training consists of a singular one-rep
    90→maximal load set.
    91→The goal with the submaximal effort method is twofold: (1) to develop
    92→proper motor patterns with bilateral movements approaching heavier
    93→loads and (2) to improve strength qualities via recruitment of high-
    94→threshold motor units and thus improve neuromuscular efficiency.
    95→Furthermore, this method is a prerequisite for maximal effort training,
    96→meaning that one should have first developed efficient movement
    97→before considering attempting maximal effort lifts. Moreover, the
    98→submaximal effort method can be used strategically for autoregulation.
    99→Stress affects all individuals differently, and asking someone to adhere
   100→to rigid percentages (five sets of three reps at 75 percent of one-rep
   101→max) does not account for daily fluctuations. Using submaximal effort
   102→training allows the individual to build up in weight based on how they
   103→are feeling on any given day. This method is also easier to navigate
   104→because building to a heavy triple for the day is less confusing than
   105→adhering to strict percentage-based guidelines. Submaximal effort
   106→training works both aspects of the force-velocity curve while creating
   107→balanced programming to reduce the risk of overtraining.

   415→In conclusion, strength development requires a multifaceted approach
   416→in which all strengths are developed strategically in a concurrent training
   417→plan. In this case, the objective is to work both ends of the force-velocity
   418→curve: both the force component (submaximal effort training) and the
   419→velocity component (DE training) within the same cycle of training.
   420→Although there are varying opinions on whether it is prudent to train
   421→multiple aspects of strength within the same microcycles, from my
   422→professional experience it is not only possible but optimal for a large
   423→percentage of the population. To be clear, I am taking into consideration
   424→that the majority of people reading this book are likely very experienced
   425→in training bilateral movements and likely have at least five years of
   426→serious training under their belt. These approaches are certainly not for
   427→the beginner, who should aim first to achieve a high level of competency
   428→with foundational movement patterns and focus on one aspect of strength

   435→The athletic training programs you’ll find in this chapter are different
   436→in many ways from the other training plans in the book, although there
   437→are parallels to the hypertrophy and strength programs presented in the
   438→previous two chapters. A training program with an athletic performance
   439→focus needs to resemble the demands of your chosen sport with regard
   440→to the following:
   441→• Energy systems development (ESD)
   442→• Volume, selection, and intensity of plyometric exercises
   443→• Your choice of strength methods
   444→Does that mean that athletic training programs can’t be used for
   445→general fitness? No, it does not; many former athletes enjoy keeping their
   446→competitive edge and like to train as if they were still competing as an
   447→athlete. If that’s the case for you, keep in mind that your training methods
   448→should always align with your goals. For example, maximal effort (ME)
   449→training is something I use sparingly for myself and my clients who are
   450→former athletes because we still like to hit the occasional personal record
   451→on lifts. While ME training is a viable method for the right individual,
   452→going past the point of mechanical breakdown will increase the risk in
   453→the risk versus reward ratio, so it’s not something that I use with most
   454→clients. Use the training methods that are appropriate for your goals and
   455→your sport.
