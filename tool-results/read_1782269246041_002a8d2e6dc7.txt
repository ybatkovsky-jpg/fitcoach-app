     5→A basic understanding of how the musculature of the lower body works
     6→and functions is paramount to effectively programming lower limb
     7→exercises in a diverse and balanced way. A deeper understanding of the
     8→basic anatomy will allow you to better analyze limitations and imbalances,
     9→will help you avoid overuse injuries, and can help you design programs
    10→that will expedite recovery after higher-threshold training sessions.
    11→This chapter will cover the basics of primary and secondary movers—
    12→the musculature that is responsible for driving human movement. Though
    13→this text will not delve as deeply as an anatomy text, I want to provide the
    14→“basics” so you are able to identify limitations while also understanding
    15→better how to strategically target different muscle groups. For instance,
    16→it’s not uncommon for hip shifts to present when maximal loads are used
    17→with a movement such as a squat variation. Hip shifts are quite common
    18→with bilateral movements such as the squat and occur when the hips shift
    19→laterally during the concentric phase of the squat pattern. Although it’s
    20→nearly impossible to eliminate all muscular imbalances, you can assume
    21→that with bilateral training these imbalances will only be exacerbated.
    22→Instead, we look to unilateral exercises to provide the basis for improving
    23→current limitations. As a result, the level of hip shift that may present with
    24→maximal loads in the squat can be decreased, but more importantly, the
    25→level of strength in agonist and antagonist muscle groups can be improved,
    26→which directly correlates to improving muscle size.

   120→strength or symmetry (or both) in the quadriceps. This will allow you
   121→to make progressive improvements to your quadriceps in a 12- to 16-

   123→specific to quadriceps is important not only for aesthetics but for everyday

   129→HAMSTRINGS
   130→The three muscles of the posterior (flexor) compartment of the thigh,
   131→known as the hamstrings, are the semitendinosus, semimembranosus,
   132→and biceps femoris (see figure 1.2). The hamstrings get their name
   133→because their elongated tendons are stringlike. The three hamstring
   134→muscles play an important role in the actions of hip extension and knee
   135→flexion. The biceps femoris is a unique muscle of the hamstring group
   136→because it has two heads (long and short). The long and short head of the
   137→biceps femoris have different sites of origin but do have the same insertion
   138→point. The long head of the biceps femoris is more superficial compared
   139→to the short head; if you looked at the muscles from a posterior view, the
   140→short head would be mostly blocked from view. The short head of the
   141→biceps femoris originates at the linea aspera of the femur (lower down
   142→the leg than other hamstring muscles). For this reason, it is sometimes
   143→not included as part of the hamstring group since originating at the
   144→ischial tuberosity is considered a defining characteristic of the hamstring
   145→group. Furthermore, due to its site of origin, the short head of the biceps
   146→femoris only crosses the knee joint and therefore plays a role only in knee
   147→flexion and not in hip extension. It is important to note that the short
   148→head of the biceps femoris can be completely absent in some individuals.

   283→blood back to the heart and therefore aid in the heart’s ability to effectively
   284→pump blood throughout the remainder of the body.

   286→twitch muscle fiber, they respond well to high-frequency training and
   287→higher-volume sets of 15 or more repetitions. The calves also use the
   288→stretch-shortening cycle. The stretch-shortening cycle is what allows
   289→for an increase in force and power production if a rapid eccentric
   290→countermovement is immediately followed by an explosive concentric
   291→movement. Think of a vertical jump in which there is a rapid eccentric
   292→countermovement; elastic energy is stored within the tendon and can be
   293→immediately released if a quick concentric muscle action follows, thus
   294→increasing force and power for a higher jump. In other words, the calves
   295→have the potential to use the energy created through the eccentric phase
   296→of the exercise variation (e.g., a calf raise) to their advantage. Because of
   297→this, to achieve full benefits when training the calves, it is best to pause
   298→between repetitions to allow the calves to bear the brunt of the work.
   299→Common calf exercises include seated calf raises, single-leg dumbbell calf
   300→raises, and standing calf raises with a barbell in the back rack position
   301→(placed on the mid traps as is done for a high-bar back squat).

   362→It’s no secret that we are living in a society plagued by chronic conditions
   363→such as lower back disorders, generalized front-sided shoulder pain, and
   364→obesity. For many, lower back pain occurs through no fault of their own.
   365→The daily postures of most—that is, sitting in flexion, internal rotation,
   366→and adduction—present many issues for people that only exacerbate
   367→their current issues. Lower back disorders are more connected to these
   368→daily postures than most would suspect. In fact, one study found that
   369→gluteus medius weakness and gluteal muscle tenderness are common
   370→symptoms in people with chronic nonspecific lower back pain (Cooper
   371→et al. 2016). Although this finding is not particularly surprising to most
   372→fitness professionals (I’ve seen it for the past decade), it gives these
   373→professionals the unique opportunity to profoundly affect their clients’
   374→health and wellness through strength training, particularly training that
   375→focuses on the proper use of quad- versus hip-dominant movements as
   376→well as hip adduction and abduction.
   377→UNDERSTANDING THE BILATERAL VERSUS
   378→UNILATERAL TRAINING DEBATE
   379→In strength and conditioning circles, it is common knowledge that one
   380→needs to have a balance between bilateral movements (e.g., multijoint
   381→movements like squats and deadlifts) and unilateral movements (e.g.,
   382→single-joint movements like lunges). There is no debate there, but a
   383→11
   384→--- PAGE 28 ---
   385→12 Lower Body Training
   386→commonly asked question is how much of each should make up one’s
   387→training program. First, let’s try to understand the consequences of
   388→taking on only one of these forms as stand-alone training, or engaging
   389→in a program that neglects unilateral movements and focuses more on
   390→their bilateral counterparts. This is a common theme in many training
   391→programs today such as CrossFit or Olympic weightlifting. If the
   392→programming of bilateral movements such as squats and deadlifts makes
   393→up the bulk of your training, there certainly could be positive adaptations
   394→made, but the question is, for how long would these positive adaptations
   395→occur? For most people in this situation, which is common in functional
   396→fitness communities, there will be setbacks due to phenomena such as
   397→bilateral asymmetries and bilateral deficits.
   398→First off, a bilateral asymmetry is a discrepancy in strength between
   399→right and left limbs. Of course, no two limbs are created perfectly equal,
   400→so this makes sense, but too high of doses of bilateral movements will
   401→exacerbate these discrepancies instead of remedying them. A bilateral
   402→deficit occurs when there are asymmetries in force production between
   403→unilateral and bilateral movements (Haff 2016). An easy example of this
   404→would be performing a one-rep max of a split squat variation, taking the
   405→sum of both legs, and comparing that to a bilateral max, such as for a
   406→back squat. While this may not be a reliable test for everyone and involves
   407→high levels of motor control, the point is that trained athletes want to see
   408→some semblance of balance between the right and left limbs. However,
   409→please note that while many well-established coaches have used this
   410→test as a marker of lower body strength symmetry, the reliability of this
   411→test may not be consistent with real-world data. It is possible to have
   412→an athlete test poorly for unilateral strength yet exhibit high levels of
   413→bilateral strength.
   414→At Westside Barbell (WSBB), considered the “strongest gym in the
   415→world,” they follow the 80/20 rule—80 percent of their training volume
   416→is made up of “special exercises”—assistance exercises performed with
   417→dumbbells and unilateral movements—while the other 20 percent comes
   418→from the classic bilateral lifts and their variations (Simmons 2015). You
   419→may be thinking, “How is this the strongest gym in the world when it comes
   420→to the squat, bench press, and deadlift considering they only perform
   421→these variations 20 percent of the time?” The answer is resoundingly
   422→simple—“We use special exercises to build lean-muscle mass, improve
   423→imbalances and lagging muscle-groups, and improve upon our weakest
   424→links—we are only as strong as our weakest link” (Simmons 2015). Of
   425→course, the athletes who train at WSBB are always technicians when it
   426→comes to squatting, pressing, and pulling, so spending the bulk of their
   427→time building where they are weak makes sense, but assistance work is
   428→--- PAGE 29 ---

   430→still vitally important for the average person while building component
   431→motor patterns with the squat and hinge patterns.
   432→Furthermore, given the physiology of unilateral movements, it makes
   433→sense that we are able to better address limitations and imbalances
   434→with them since we are able to train our lower body in isolation with
   435→less contribution of opposing limbs (most times). Lastly, an important
   436→consideration is the central nervous system (CNS) fatigue that is created
   437→by performing multijoint movements. For instance, if I asked a client to
   438→squat and deadlift for their entire 60-minute training session, not only
   439→would this create tissue damage, but the CNS fatigue could take up to
   440→96 hours to fully recover from. If you do not consider these aspects of
   441→training and only train with movements that result in high levels of CNS
   442→fatigue, your risk of overtraining will be higher. Conversely, if the dose
   443→of multijoint movements is commensurate with the dose of single-joint
   444→assistance exercises within the same session, the demand on the CNS
   445→will be reduced, thus allowing for better recovery between sessions of
   446→both the CNS and the peripheral nervous system.
   447→It’s commonplace to see higher-level athletes pushing their squat and
   448→pulling volume and neglecting the less “sexy” components of training
   449→such as single-joint exercises. This yields a greater chance of creating
   450→compensatory motor patterns that could result in an overuse injury and—
   451→because bilateral movements apply more stress to the CNS—higher levels
   452→of fatigue. The fatigue may manifest with lesser-known symptoms such as
   453→loss of motivation (French 2016). In other words, higher doses of bilateral
   454→movements present many challenges to the CNS and the musculoskeletal
   455→system. Practitioners who prescribe high doses of bilateral movements
   456→frequently are increasing the risk of overtraining among their clients, so
   457→a balance between bilateral and unilateral work must be achieved, with

   491→training the musculature of the posterior regions of the body. This

   507→instead of stabilizers.

   943→The term general physical preparedness (GPP) gets thrown around
   944→quite a bit—many coaches claim that the goal of their program is to

   948→will likely get you hurt. We are talking about building your foundation.

   950→you cannot pull a sled or carry two heavy kettlebells without having to
   951→rest excessively between sets, then higher-skill work like ME squats or
   952→deadlifts isn’t a smart move. The Russians had a GPP plan for all of their
   953→athletes called the “rule of three” that they employed for three years in
   954→the early stages of training before specialization took place. Yup, you
   955→read that right. Why is this relevant to your training programs? If the
   956→Russians were smart enough to build their athletes’ foundations first,
   957→then maybe this is something you should consider. More importantly, all
   958→of the training goals expressed by most people (look better, feel better,
   959→get stronger, have fun) can be addressed with GPP measures.

  1037→Repeated Effort Method
  1038→The repeated effort method is a premier method of improving muscular
  1039→imbalance and muscular hypertrophy and providing rehabilitative or
  1040→prehabilitative work to ensure you’re constantly improving symmetry.
  1041→This work is done through single-joint exercises and isolation work to
  1042→target musculature limitations. It’s important to prioritize unilateral
  1043→assistance exercises because you can dedicate time to improving
  1044→limitations, and classic lifts such as the squat, press, and deadlift will
  1045→improve through establishing symmetry and strength in lagging muscle
  1046→groups. Because unilateral exercises are less demanding on the nervous
  1047→system, you are able to add volume and frequency with the overall
  1048→objective of improving deficiencies. The volume of assistance exercises
  1049→can be pushed to high levels and align with your training age and needs
  1050→quite well with less risk. Moreover, people often fail to take inventory of
  1051→where they struggle the most and experiment with exercises that will
  1052→potentially help improve their limiting factors. This is what assistance
  1053→exercises are for—to strategically improve limitations in a muscle or
  1054→muscle group that may be holding one back, or worse, increasing their risk
  1055→of injury in the future. A simple example of this is the glutes, which extend
  1056→the hips and abduct and externally rotate the legs. They are therefore
  1057→responsible for the finishing, hip extension–based portions of the squat
  1058→and deadlift. Performing exercises that duplicate these actions is key.
  1059→The Romanian deadlift (RDL) and the glute hip thrust are two excellent
  1060→choices to build glute strength around hip extension movements.
  1061→Of course, improving performance is important to many individuals,
  1062→but many people tend to have the primary goal of improving body
  1063→composition. Luckily, assistance exercise work can be instrumental in
  1064→helping add lean muscle mass because the 8- to 12-rep range will facilitate
  1065→muscular hypertrophy (also known as sarcoplasmic hypertrophy), which
  1066→is an increase in the volume of sarcoplasmic fluid in the muscle cell
  1067→with no actual increase in muscular strength—envision the “muscle

  1070→pump” you get after doing three sets of biceps curls (French 2016). In
  1071→this case, time under tension will be significantly higher than for your
  1072→max effort, and DE work as the objective is quite different in terms of
  1073→strength development. (Hypertrophy work prioritizes time under tension,
  1074→whereas DE work prioritizes bar velocity to improve RFD.) The ultimate
  1075→goal here is to improve and isolate lagging muscle groups, so single-joint
  1076→exercises like split squats, single-leg RDLs, and glute hip thrust variations

  1129→Maximal Effort Method
  1130→The ME method is considered the superior method of improving both
  1131→intramuscular and intermuscular coordination; the muscles and CNS
  1132→adapt only to the load placed upon them, and this method brings forth
  1133→the greatest strength gains (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021). With
  1134→that said, what is meant by “maximal” can span a number of scenarios,
  1135→making the ME method a polarizing topic to say the least. Is training
  1136→maximally done with multiple repetitions such as a two-, three-, or even
  1137→five-rep max? Or are we talking about simply loading the bar with as much
  1138→weight as one can move for one repetition? In this book, we are referring
  1139→to maximal tension with the heaviest load for one repetition. The ME
  1140→method here is lifting a maximal load for one repetition performed with
  1141→bilateral movements such as squat, deadlift, and press variations. These
  1142→variations are included in the training programs provided in part III.
  1143→--- PAGE 46 ---
  1144→30 Lower Body Training
  1145→The adaptations occur in both intermuscular and intramuscular
  1146→coordination—in essence improving both the ability of muscles to fire
  1147→in synchronicity with one another and motor patterns. The ME method
  1148→brings forth the greatest gains in maximal strength with the most amount
  1149→of motor units activated (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021) and has the
  1150→highest-intensity strength measure (maximum load/resistance for one
  1151→repetition) but lowest-volume bilateral training measure (intensity and
  1152→volume should not intersect with this method) (Simmons 2015).
  1153→As previously mentioned, there are connections between maximal
  1154→strength and other biomotor abilities like strength endurance, but we
  1155→must first consider if the ME method can fit into your training plan,
  1156→allowing you to arrive at optimal results and a favorable risk-versus-
  1157→reward ratio. Exercise variations should be rotated weekly to reduce
  1158→risk of injury and to identify variations that are anthropometrically most
  1159→suited to the individual. Also, individuals using this method should be
  1160→fully comfortable building to one-rep maximum with any foundational
  1161→movement pattern such as a box squat or a floor press. Another safety
  1162→note is that this method requires the use of a spotter every time.
  1163→While this method does come with a stigma of being dangerous and is
  1164→not recommended for beginner or even intermediate individuals, it’s only
  1165→dangerous if the individual has not learned how to properly perform the
  1166→big lifts. Not having learned proper motor patterns can be a detriment
  1167→with any method, but it’s certainly not prudent to imagine working with
  1168→maximal loads without the proper movement patterns enforced first.
  1169→However, there are certainly other methods that will be a better match
  1170→for novices.
  1171→Submaximal Effort Method
  1172→The submaximal effort method differs from the ME method in the
  1173→number of repetitions executed, not necessarily the level of effort. While
  1174→the ME method results in the highest levels of motor-unit recruitment,
  1175→the submaximal effort method still results in high levels of motor-unit
  1176→recruitment that correspond with the size principle—recruitment order is
  1177→determined by the load placed upon the body; as the relative load increases
  1178→across multiple repetitions, large motor units will be recruited (Zatsiorsky,
  1179→Kraemer, and Fry 2021). With that said, the adaptations with this method
  1180→will be different from those with the ME method; however, a key takeaway
  1181→is that this method is optimal for individuals who have less experience.
  1182→This is because the emphasis is on building motor patterns with more
  1183→exposure to lighter loads. Individuals will not typically be asked to go
  1184→to one-rep maximum if they are still learning how to move with a given
  1185→pattern. Performing a multiple-rep max with a bilateral movement such
  1186→--- PAGE 47 ---
  1187→Go Beyond the Basics 31
  1188→as a squat variation technically constitutes submaximal work—remember
  1189→that true ME work is trained with a maximum singular effort.
  1190→The adaptations for this method are intermuscular and intramuscular
  1191→coordination—in essence improving both the ability of muscles to fire
  1192→in synchronicity with one another and motor patterns. Also, time under
  1193→tension is higher and results in more hypertrophic adaptations. The
  1194→submaximal effort method presents moderate-intensity, moderate-volume
  1195→measure—it’s recommended that you do not attempt to ramp up to the
  1196→same degree one would expect with a method like the repeated effort
  1197→method. Exercise variations should be rotated every two to three weeks
  1198→to allow time to ingrain good motor patterns.
  1199→In short, the logic behind the submaximal effort method is twofold:
  1200→(1) improve motor patterns with bigger bilateral movements—this is
  1201→critical for newer individuals before attempting to progress to a method
  1202→such as the ME method; and (2) incur higher bouts of volume with bigger
  1203→lifts. Remember, you will still incur the largest volume with the repeated
  1204→effort method and smaller unilateral exercises, but that’s not to say that
  1205→including heavier multiple-rep maxes with bilateral movements does not
  1206→have a place in your training plan. You must be mindful of proper volume
  1207→prescriptions and how this method synergizes with other methods, which
  1208→will play out optimally in the programs in part III.
  1209→Dynamic Effort Method
  1210→The DE method is used not to increase maximal strength but only to
  1211→improve the RFD and explosive strength (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry
  1212→2021). Working harder is often associated with making more gains in the
  1213→gym, but I’d urge you to consider working smarter. The different methods
  1214→introduced here will allow you to create a more well-rounded training
  1215→plan that covers all bases in terms of special strength development. The
  1216→DE method works with lighter loads and emphasizes bar velocity; only
  1217→working with submaximal or maximal loads does not offer the benefits
  1218→of dedicating time to work on bar velocity or power such as with the DE
  1219→method. Oftentimes people associate power training with the Olympic lifts,
  1220→and while there is certainly a power component to the Olympic lifts, for
  1221→many the Olympic lifts are not practical as they require years of experience
  1222→and coaching from a qualified coach. Instead, you can arrive at the same
  1223→improvements in RFD, in this case speed strength, by using the DE method
  1224→without having to learn the snatch or clean and jerk. Again, the focus is
  1225→on bar velocity against intermediate resistance, which will provide better
  1226→results than simply using ME or submaximal effort training alone.
