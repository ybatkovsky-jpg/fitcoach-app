    55→HORMONAL RESPONSES OF STRENGTH

    57→The benefits of effective lower limb training extend to optimal hormone
    58→production because the lower body represents the largest muscle groups
    59→in the body that signal the growth hormone insulin-like growth factor 1
    60→(IGF-1) and serum testosterone. It’s been widely established that long-
    61→term heavy resistance training can bring about significant increases of
    62→muscle size, strength, and power. These increases can boost hormonal
    63→interactions, and the type of exercise is a major player in how big the
    64→hormonal response is. Because heavy compound movements like squats
    65→and deadlifts require high levels of motor-unit recruitment, they activate
    66→androgen receptors for anabolic hormones such as testosterone. The
    67→inflammatory response to skeletal muscle damage causes remodeling to
    68→take place and consequently new growth to occur. Moreover, long-term
    69→training effects from heavy lower body resistance exercises can result
    70→in adaptations in the endocrine system, thus creating an environment
    71→in the human body that is suited for transport of anabolic hormones.

   101→take place upon initial exposure to strength training.

   228→can also occur if you undergo doses of heavy resistance training that

   259→General Physical Preparedness Work
   260→The term general physical preparedness (GPP) gets thrown around
   261→quite a bit—many coaches claim that the goal of their program is to
   262→develop GPP, yet many of these same coaches don’t use measures that
   263→actually develop it. Throwing heavy deadlifts into a workout if you are
   264→already lacking in the strength department won’t develop GPP and
   265→will likely get you hurt. We are talking about building your foundation.
   266→This is something that should take precedence in any programming—if
   267→you cannot pull a sled or carry two heavy kettlebells without having to
   268→rest excessively between sets, then higher-skill work like ME squats or
   269→deadlifts isn’t a smart move. The Russians had a GPP plan for all of their
   270→athletes called the “rule of three” that they employed for three years in
   271→the early stages of training before specialization took place. Yup, you
   272→read that right. Why is this relevant to your training programs? If the
   273→Russians were smart enough to build their athletes’ foundations first,
   274→then maybe this is something you should consider. More importantly, all
   275→of the training goals expressed by most people (look better, feel better,
   276→get stronger, have fun) can be addressed with GPP measures.
   277→Improving maximal strength indirectly connects to qualities of
   278→strength endurance, although the two strengths could not be further
   279→from each other. Using measures like sled drags, we can effectively
   280→improve aerobic capacity and oxidative qualities of the musculature of
   281→the hips, hamstrings, and calves, as well as bridge the gap between ME
   282→and DE training sessions. Think of the sled as an intermediary to improve
   283→strength qualities with only a small cost on the nervous and muscular
   284→systems due to the fact there is zero axial loading. Moreover, the sled
   285→also will improve the oxidative quality of fast-twitch muscle fibers.
   286→Fast-twitch type II muscle fibers have an aerobic as well as
   287→anaerobic ability, which strengthens the connection between maximal
   288→and submaximal strength development and strength endurance.

   293→really is that powerful. The sled may be one of the most valuable training
   294→tools we have available, and at less than 120 dollars it is cost-effective.
   295→The sled is a mainstay in effective program design and can be used for

   354→Repeated Effort Method
   355→The repeated effort method is a premier method of improving muscular
   356→imbalance and muscular hypertrophy and providing rehabilitative or
   357→prehabilitative work to ensure you’re constantly improving symmetry.
   358→This work is done through single-joint exercises and isolation work to
   359→target musculature limitations. It’s important to prioritize unilateral
   360→assistance exercises because you can dedicate time to improving
   361→limitations, and classic lifts such as the squat, press, and deadlift will
   362→improve through establishing symmetry and strength in lagging muscle
   363→groups. Because unilateral exercises are less demanding on the nervous
   364→system, you are able to add volume and frequency with the overall
   365→objective of improving deficiencies. The volume of assistance exercises
   366→can be pushed to high levels and align with your training age and needs
   367→quite well with less risk. Moreover, people often fail to take inventory of
   368→where they struggle the most and experiment with exercises that will
   369→potentially help improve their limiting factors. This is what assistance
   370→exercises are for—to strategically improve limitations in a muscle or
   371→muscle group that may be holding one back, or worse, increasing their risk
   372→of injury in the future. A simple example of this is the glutes, which extend
   373→the hips and abduct and externally rotate the legs. They are therefore
   374→responsible for the finishing, hip extension–based portions of the squat
   375→and deadlift. Performing exercises that duplicate these actions is key.
   376→The Romanian deadlift (RDL) and the glute hip thrust are two excellent
   377→choices to build glute strength around hip extension movements.
   378→Of course, improving performance is important to many individuals,
   379→but many people tend to have the primary goal of improving body
   380→composition. Luckily, assistance exercise work can be instrumental in
   381→helping add lean muscle mass because the 8- to 12-rep range will facilitate
   382→muscular hypertrophy (also known as sarcoplasmic hypertrophy), which
   383→is an increase in the volume of sarcoplasmic fluid in the muscle cell
   384→with no actual increase in muscular strength—envision the “muscle
   385→--- PAGE 44 ---

   387→pump” you get after doing three sets of biceps curls (French 2016). In
   388→this case, time under tension will be significantly higher than for your
   389→max effort, and DE work as the objective is quite different in terms of
   390→strength development. (Hypertrophy work prioritizes time under tension,
   391→whereas DE work prioritizes bar velocity to improve RFD.) The ultimate
   392→goal here is to improve and isolate lagging muscle groups, so single-joint

   416→bilateral work, there will almost always be a breakdown in the chain
   417→as fatigue sets in. This can exacerbate your current asymmetries
   418→and set you up for injury instead of making actual improvements.

   446→Maximal Effort Method
   447→The ME method is considered the superior method of improving both
   448→intramuscular and intermuscular coordination; the muscles and CNS
   449→adapt only to the load placed upon them, and this method brings forth
   450→the greatest strength gains (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021). With
   451→that said, what is meant by “maximal” can span a number of scenarios,
   452→making the ME method a polarizing topic to say the least. Is training
   453→maximally done with multiple repetitions such as a two-, three-, or even
   454→five-rep max? Or are we talking about simply loading the bar with as much
   455→weight as one can move for one repetition? In this book, we are referring
   456→to maximal tension with the heaviest load for one repetition. The ME
   457→method here is lifting a maximal load for one repetition performed with
   458→bilateral movements such as squat, deadlift, and press variations. These
   459→variations are included in the training programs provided in part III.
   460→--- PAGE 46 ---
   461→30 Lower Body Training
   462→The adaptations occur in both intermuscular and intramuscular
   463→coordination—in essence improving both the ability of muscles to fire
   464→in synchronicity with one another and motor patterns. The ME method
   465→brings forth the greatest gains in maximal strength with the most amount
   466→of motor units activated (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry 2021) and has the
   467→highest-intensity strength measure (maximum load/resistance for one
   468→repetition) but lowest-volume bilateral training measure (intensity and
   469→volume should not intersect with this method) (Simmons 2015).
   470→As previously mentioned, there are connections between maximal
   471→strength and other biomotor abilities like strength endurance, but we
   472→must first consider if the ME method can fit into your training plan,
   473→allowing you to arrive at optimal results and a favorable risk-versus-
   474→reward ratio. Exercise variations should be rotated weekly to reduce
   475→risk of injury and to identify variations that are anthropometrically most
   476→suited to the individual. Also, individuals using this method should be
   477→fully comfortable building to one-rep maximum with any foundational
   478→movement pattern such as a box squat or a floor press. Another safety
   479→note is that this method requires the use of a spotter every time.
   480→While this method does come with a stigma of being dangerous and is
   481→not recommended for beginner or even intermediate individuals, it’s only
   482→dangerous if the individual has not learned how to properly perform the
   483→big lifts. Not having learned proper motor patterns can be a detriment
   484→with any method, but it’s certainly not prudent to imagine working with
   485→maximal loads without the proper movement patterns enforced first.

   488→Submaximal Effort Method
   489→The submaximal effort method differs from the ME method in the
   490→number of repetitions executed, not necessarily the level of effort. While
   491→the ME method results in the highest levels of motor-unit recruitment,
   492→the submaximal effort method still results in high levels of motor-unit
   493→recruitment that correspond with the size principle—recruitment order is
   494→determined by the load placed upon the body; as the relative load increases
   495→across multiple repetitions, large motor units will be recruited (Zatsiorsky,
   496→Kraemer, and Fry 2021). With that said, the adaptations with this method
   497→will be different from those with the ME method; however, a key takeaway
   498→is that this method is optimal for individuals who have less experience.
   499→This is because the emphasis is on building motor patterns with more
   500→exposure to lighter loads. Individuals will not typically be asked to go
   501→to one-rep maximum if they are still learning how to move with a given
   502→pattern. Performing a multiple-rep max with a bilateral movement such
   503→--- PAGE 47 ---
   504→Go Beyond the Basics 31
   505→as a squat variation technically constitutes submaximal work—remember
   506→that true ME work is trained with a maximum singular effort.
   507→The adaptations for this method are intermuscular and intramuscular
   508→coordination—in essence improving both the ability of muscles to fire
   509→in synchronicity with one another and motor patterns. Also, time under
   510→tension is higher and results in more hypertrophic adaptations. The
   511→submaximal effort method presents moderate-intensity, moderate-volume
   512→measure—it’s recommended that you do not attempt to ramp up to the
   513→same degree one would expect with a method like the repeated effort
   514→method. Exercise variations should be rotated every two to three weeks
   515→to allow time to ingrain good motor patterns.
   516→In short, the logic behind the submaximal effort method is twofold:
   517→(1) improve motor patterns with bigger bilateral movements—this is
   518→critical for newer individuals before attempting to progress to a method
   519→such as the ME method; and (2) incur higher bouts of volume with bigger
   520→lifts. Remember, you will still incur the largest volume with the repeated
   521→effort method and smaller unilateral exercises, but that’s not to say that
   522→including heavier multiple-rep maxes with bilateral movements does not
   523→have a place in your training plan. You must be mindful of proper volume
   524→prescriptions and how this method synergizes with other methods, which
   525→will play out optimally in the programs in part III.
   526→Dynamic Effort Method
   527→The DE method is used not to increase maximal strength but only to
   528→improve the RFD and explosive strength (Zatsiorsky, Kraemer, and Fry
   529→2021). Working harder is often associated with making more gains in the
   530→gym, but I’d urge you to consider working smarter. The different methods
   531→introduced here will allow you to create a more well-rounded training
   532→plan that covers all bases in terms of special strength development. The
   533→DE method works with lighter loads and emphasizes bar velocity; only
   534→working with submaximal or maximal loads does not offer the benefits
   535→of dedicating time to work on bar velocity or power such as with the DE
   536→method. Oftentimes people associate power training with the Olympic lifts,
   537→and while there is certainly a power component to the Olympic lifts, for
   538→many the Olympic lifts are not practical as they require years of experience
   539→and coaching from a qualified coach. Instead, you can arrive at the same
   540→improvements in RFD, in this case speed strength, by using the DE method
   541→without having to learn the snatch or clean and jerk. Again, the focus is
   542→on bar velocity against intermediate resistance, which will provide better
   543→results than simply using ME or submaximal effort training alone.
   544→The DE method is integral for improving RFD and explosive strength.

   760→Accommodating Resistance
   761→We alluded to the fact that using AR is optimal with the DE method, so
   762→let’s explore what AR is and why it can be beneficial to your training
   763→plan. AR refers to the use of chains or bands to develop maximal tension
   764→throughout the full ROM, rather than at your weakest point. While there
   765→are a number of benefits to using AR, one of the most noteworthy is
   766→accommodating the strength curve, in which tension is highest where
   767→we are strongest and lowest where we are weakest (Simmons 2015). Here
   768→are a few key points when using AR:

   771→Bands Versus Chains
   772→The major difference between bands and chains is the phenomenon
   773→known as “overspeed eccentric.” Put simply, in the case of a squat,
   774→bands actually pull you down in the lowering portion of your lift,
   775→increasing the amount of kinetic energy that is produced. Because
   776→of this, you are able to enhance reversal strength and your ability
   777→to absorb force, crucial to any sport. With chains, the overspeed
   778→eccentric is not present because the resistance does not stay consis-
   779→tent when an athlete is lowering the weight. Overall, using bands is
   780→particularly advantageous to strength athletes who want to become
   781→more explosive. These benefits can translate into cracking personal
   782→records in the big three.
   783→• AR allows for greater accountability with bar speed compared to
   784→just straight weight—as tension increases through ROM, you are
   785→forced to accelerate through each repetition and not get complacent.
   786→• AR coincides with the strength curve, meaning that your band
   787→tension will be highest where you are strongest (top of the movement)
   788→and lowest where you are weakest (bottom of the movement).
   789→• The RFD provides more resistance without compromising bar speed
   790→with regard to the strength curve of a given movement (i.e., band
   791→tension is highest where you are strongest and vice versa).
   792→• As weight increases, bar speed decreases. Maximal strength force is
   793→high, and velocity is low. AR gives you the ability to develop speed
   794→strength, whereas with simply adding straight weight, bar velocity
   795→will inevitably decline.
   796→• AR teaches you how to absorb more force, which in turn allows you
   797→to become more powerful.
   798→• Without bands or chains, bar deceleration is inevitable, and when
   799→bar speed is too slow, RFD simply cannot be developed. AR forces
   800→you to accelerate through full ROM, and becoming more explosive
   801→translates to becoming stronger!
   802→• AR can also be used for ME work. The advantage here is that you
   803→will be able to use less straight weight, with overload occurring at
   804→the top of a given movement. As a result, external loading through
   805→ROM is lower, which equates to less breakdown and less delayed
   806→onset muscle soreness. For a movement like an ME rack deadlift,
   807→--- PAGE 54 ---
   808→38 Lower Body Training
   809→where loading for some can reach supramaximal levels, using AR
   810→will reduce the amount of wear and tear compared to what would
   811→occur with just straight weight.
   812→Reverse Bands
   813→Reverse bands are the opposite of AR; bands are fixed to the top of the
   814→squat rack, and the load is lightest at the top and heaviest at the bottom
   815→as a given movement goes through eccentric ROM. (Think of the lowering
   816→phase of an RDL.) The benefits of using this method are improvements in
   817→neuromuscular efficiency and, more importantly, the ability to fine-tune
   818→and repattern foundational movements. In addition, being able to work
   819→with supramaximal loads at the top of each movement creates different
   820→scenarios from just working with straight weight—the degree to which
   821→you’ll be able to exceed your one-rep max will depend on the thickness
   822→of the band you choose. Because you’re able to work with supramaximal
   823→loads, this method tends to be quite demanding on the nervous system,
   824→particularly when used in true ME scenarios with big movements like
   825→the deadlift. However, in submaximal or repeated effort scenarios, you’ll
   826→likely find you’re able to maintain better position even with loads that
   827→exceed your current one-rep max. For the purposes of this book, though,
   828→we recommend the use of reverse bands not in an ME session but as an
   829→assistance exercise with a pattern like the RDL to reinforce (or rebuild)
   830→an effective hip hinge pattern (more on this in chapter 6). With that
   831→said, the reverse band method can also work quite well with repeated
   832→effort work and allow you to reinforce better movement patterns. Even
   833→if you approach loads where you would normally have some mechanical
   834→breakdown if you were using straight weight, the band will deload a
   835→percentage of the weight in the bottom position, thus allowing you to
   836→reinforce better motor patterns with less breakdown while still receiving
   837→the desired training effects.
   838→Reverse bands are optimal when used for the following:
   839→• ME work: one-repetition maximum (1RM)
   840→• Submaximal effort work: 2-6RM
   841→• Repetition effort work: 6- to 10-rep sets
   842→• Technique work: Straight sets with moderately heavy loads of 80
   843→to 85 percent of 1RM
   844→These are the benefits of reverse bands:
   845→• Improved neuromuscular efficiency. This is the ability of the
   846→nervous system to recruit the correct muscles to produce force.
   847→--- PAGE 55 ---
   848→Go Beyond the Basics 39
   849→• Improved stability. This is the ability of your body to stabilize and
   850→brace while achieving supramaximal loading at the end ROM.
   851→• Improved motor patterns. Reverse bands deload a percentage of
   852→the weight at the bottom of a movement and allow you to in essence
   853→do the opposite of AR. Where with AR tension is highest where we
   854→are strongest (accommodating the strength curve), with reverse
   855→bands tension is highest where you are weakest.
   856→• Opportunity for technique work. Reverse bands allow you to work
   857→with heavier loads with less chance of mechanical breakdown.
   858→• Hypertrophy. If gaining lean mass is your goal, reverse bands work
   859→great with big movements so you can create situations with high
   860→amounts of metabolic stress but less mechanical breakdown.
   861→In conclusion, the best part of using reverse bands is that they can
   862→serve a variety of purposes—maximal strength development, technique

   864→critical aspect of this method is that it allows you to maximize trainability
