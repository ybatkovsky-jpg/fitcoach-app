    98‚Üí   143‚Üí   167‚Üí   167‚Üíto Metabolic Training
    99‚Üí   144‚Üí   168‚Üí   168‚ÜíPhysiological adaptation refers to how the body responds to, accommodates, or
   100‚Üí   145‚Üí   169‚Üí   169‚Üíadjusts to exercise. To give an over-simplified example, if you lift heavy weights,
   101‚Üí   146‚Üí   170‚Üí   170‚Üíyour muscles get bigger. But there are actually a number of adaptations taking
   102‚Üí   147‚Üí   171‚Üí   171‚Üíplace in response to such a stimulus. These adaptations include a more coordi-
   103‚Üí   148‚Üí   172‚Üí   172‚Üínated neural signal to the activated muscles, uptake of nutrients, and the release
   104‚Üí   149‚Üí   173‚Üí   173‚Üíof muscle-building hormones.
   105‚Üí   150‚Üí   174‚Üí   174‚ÜíLearning about the process of physiological adaptation will help you design
   106‚Üí   151‚Üí   175‚Üí   175‚Üímore effective workouts, understand how your body will respond to training,
   107‚Üí   152‚Üí   176‚Üí   176‚Üíand know what to expect with consistent and focused training. Two of the most
   108‚Üí   153‚Üí   177‚Üí   177‚Üícommon fitness goals are burning fat and enhancing muscular development. This
   109‚Üí   154‚Üí   178‚Üí   178‚Üíchapter takes a deeper dive into some of the physiological adaptations to training
   110‚Üí   155‚Üí   179‚Üí   179‚Üíthat can turn these goals into realities.
   111‚Üí   156‚Üí   180‚Üí   180‚ÜíAdapting to Demands
   112‚Üí   157‚Üí   181‚Üí   181‚ÜíAs discussed in chapter 1, in reference to the specificity principle, adaptations are
   113‚Üí   158‚Üí   182‚Üí   182‚Üíspecific to imposed demands. Adaptions can be morphological (related to muscle
   114‚Üí   159‚Üí   183‚Üí   183‚Üísize), biomechanical (related to strength and power), anatomical (related to muscle,
   115‚Üí   160‚Üí   184‚Üí   184‚Üíbone, or fat volumes), neurological (related to motor control), or metabolic (related
   116‚Üí   161‚Üí   185‚Üí   185‚Üíto aerobic or anaerobic energy pathways) in nature. Workout parameters such
   117‚Üí   162‚Üí   186‚Üí   186‚Üías intensity level and rest duration can be set to elicit specific adaptations. For
   118‚Üí   163‚Üí   187‚Üí   187‚Üíinstance, short, high-intensity exercise intervals (up to six seconds in duration,
   119‚Üí   164‚Üí   188‚Üí   188‚Üífocusing on quick movement with maximal intensity) paired with longer rest
   120‚Üí   165‚Üí   189‚Üí   189‚Üíintervals (1:1 to 1:4 work:rest ratio) can target the short-duration phosphagen meta-
   121‚Üí   166‚Üí   190‚Üí   190‚Üíbolic pathway. Conversely, lower-intensity activity paired with shorter-duration
   122‚Üí   167‚Üí   191‚Üí   191‚Üírest intervals can target the aerobic metabolic pathway. Also, keep in mind that
   123‚Üí   168‚Üí   192‚Üí   192‚Üíexercises targeting multiple joints and muscle groups at one time are more meta-
   124‚Üí   169‚Üí   193‚Üí   193‚Üíbolically challenging than exercises targeting individual joints and muscle groups.
   125‚Üí   170‚Üí   194‚Üí   194‚ÜíIt should be pointed out that metabolic training utilizes both aerobic and anaero-
   126‚Üí   171‚Üí   195‚Üí   195‚Üíbic metabolic pathways, while also placing demand on the neuromuscular system
   127‚Üí   172‚Üí   196‚Üí   196‚Üí11
   128‚Üí   173‚Üí   197‚Üí   197‚Üí--- PAGE 21 ---
   129‚Üí   174‚Üí   198‚Üí   198‚Üí12 | Metabolic Training
   130‚Üí   175‚Üí   199‚Üí   199‚Üíby way of strength and power exercises. In other situations, metabolic training
   131‚Üí   176‚Üí   200‚Üí   200‚Üícan elicit more of an aerobic metabolic (endurance) response‚ÄĒfor example, when
   132‚Üí   177‚Üí   201‚Üí   201‚Üílighter weights are used. This de-emphasizes the neuromuscular system. These
   133‚Üí   178‚Üí   202‚Üí   202‚Üítwo examples represent the various ways in which metabolic training can be pro-
   134‚Üí   179‚Üí   203‚Üí   203‚Üígrammed. Neither approach is superior to the other; the optimal program is best
   135‚Üí   180‚Üí   204‚Üí   204‚Üídetermined by the goals of the program and the individual. With this concept in
   136‚Üí   181‚Üí   205‚Üí   205‚Üímind, both aerobic and anaerobic adaptations can be observed.
   137‚Üí   182‚Üí   206‚Üí   206‚ÜíSome metabolic-training programs implement strength-power and aerobic
   138‚Üí   183‚Üí   207‚Üí   207‚Üí(endurance) methodologies together. This approach is called concurrent training.
   139‚Üí   184‚Üí   208‚Üí   208‚ÜíResearch on the effects of concurrent training has yielded the following conclu-
   140‚Üí   185‚Üí   209‚Üí   209‚Üísions:
   141‚Üí   186‚Üí   210‚Üí   210‚Üí‚ÄĘ Concurrent training does not appear to compromise gains in aerobic capacity.
   142‚Üí   187‚Üí   211‚Üí   211‚Üí‚ÄĘ The incorporation of resistance training may have a beneficial effect on
   143‚Üí   188‚Üí   212‚Üí   212‚Üíshort-term endurance.
   144‚Üí   189‚Üí   213‚Üí   213‚Üí‚ÄĘ The incorporation of aerobic training may compromise maximal strength in
   145‚Üí   190‚Üí   214‚Üí   214‚Üíexperienced, resistance-trained individuals.
   146‚Üí   191‚Üí   215‚Üí   215‚ÜíConsider the intensity of training: Short bursts of high-intensity training target
   147‚Üí   192‚Üí   216‚Üí   216‚Üíthe anaerobic pathway. Longer-duration efforts, lasting more than three minutes,
   148‚Üí   193‚Üí   217‚Üí   217‚Üístart to emphasize the aerobic pathway. Metabolic activity is elevated by anaerobic
   149‚Üí   194‚Üí   218‚Üí   218‚Üíefforts of shorter duration if these efforts are performed at high intensity, which
   150‚Üí   195‚Üí   219‚Üí   219‚Üíincludes heavy resistance training exercise (Herda & Cramer 2016). This idea
   151‚Üí   196‚Üí   220‚Üí   220‚Üíconstitutes a fundamental element and benefit of metabolic training.

   159‚Üí   204‚Üí   228‚Üí   228‚Üícircuit weight training. In other words, EPOC is intensity dependent in response
